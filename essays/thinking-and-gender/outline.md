## Scope and ground rules

- **Scope:** Western (US and Europe), with acknowledgment that the masculine/feminine framing is culturally contingent.
- **Gender framing:** Primarily the male/female binary, as specified. Trans and non-binary scholarship is integrated where it sharpens a point (e.g., research on trans men's post-transition workplace credibility bears directly on §3).
- **Intersectionality:** Race, class, and background are woven in where they materially shape the pattern, without displacing the main dichotomy.
- **Stoicism framing (§5):** Both classical Stoicism (as the intellectual root of the reason-as-virtue tradition) and contemporary cultural stoicism (as its popular descendant) — with the specific throughline that **logic has been elevated to the only respected mode of thought**.
- **Sources:** Contemporary peer-reviewed scholarship (last 20 years primary, older foundational work cited where essential). Neutral posture: present findings and competing interpretations, reserve the author's opinion until the research is complete.

## 1. Creative thinking across disciplines — art, math, science, engineering

**Thesis to test:** Our modern separation of "creative" and "analytical" thinking is a recent, culturally constructed binary. We hold up Leonardo da Vinci as a polymath precisely because our taxonomy no longer accommodates him. The split misrepresents what engineers, scientists, and mathematicians actually do, and underestimates what artists and designers actually do.

### 1.1 The false binary between creative and analytical cognition
- How did the separation of "creative" from "technical/analytical" work emerge historically, and when?
- What does contemporary cognitive science say about the shared cognitive substrates of mathematical, scientific, and artistic creativity (e.g., divergent thinking, analogical reasoning, working memory)?
- How do scientists and engineers themselves describe the role of imagination, aesthetics, and intuition in their work (e.g., Einstein on "combinatory play," Poincaré on mathematical beauty)?
- What is the empirical evidence that creativity in STEM and creativity in the arts draw on overlapping cognitive and neural resources?
- Is there a gendered component to how "creativity" versus "rigor" gets coded and attributed? (E.g., men in science are "rigorous," women in science are "creative" or "intuitive.")

## 2. Masculinization and feminization of professions

**Thesis to test:** Professions do not have stable gender identities. They migrate — and prestige and pay tend to migrate with them. The direction of migration is not random.

### 2.1 Marketing, PR, HR, psychology, sociology: masculine → feminine
- When did each of these fields tip from majority-male to majority-female, and what drove the shift?
- How did compensation, cultural prestige, and academic standing change as the fields feminized?
- Is "devaluation theory" (Levanon, England, Allison 2009) supported: does work pay less once it becomes coded female, controlling for skill?
- How has marketing specifically been reframed — from a "hard" discipline (economics, statistics) to a "soft" one (empathy, storytelling, brand) — and how does that map to gender?
- What's the intersectional picture? Which women entered these fields first, and how does race/class factor in?

### 2.2 Engineering and computing: feminine → masculine
- Who did the earliest programming work (ENIAC women, Bletchley Park, "computers" as a job title held mostly by women)? What was the cultural framing at the time?
- When and how did software and hardware engineering re-code as masculine? Key works: Mar Hicks (*Programmed Inequality*), Nathan Ensmenger (*The Computer Boys Take Over*), Janet Abbate (*Recoding Gender*).
- What role did professionalization, credentialing, personality testing, and the GI Bill play in pushing women out?
- How does the "brilliance bias" (Leslie, Cimpian, Meyer, Freeland 2015) operate in fields where innate ability is presumed necessary — and how does that correlate with gender representation?
- What does contemporary data show about the gender composition of engineering sub-fields (e.g., why is biomedical engineering more gender-balanced than mechanical or computer engineering)?

## 3. Perception of women in professional environments

**Thesis to test:** Women's professional outcomes — credibility, advancement, pay — are shaped not just by their individual choices but by measurable, well-documented patterns of bias in how they are perceived and evaluated.

### 3a. Credibility and being taken seriously
- What does the empirical literature show about how women's contributions are attributed, interrupted, credited, and evaluated in professional settings?
- Key research: Heidi vs. Howard (Flynn & Anderson), Goldin & Rouse blind auditions, Tinsley & Ely's research on gender dynamics, Kieran Snyder on meeting interruptions.
- How does "prove-it-again bias" (Joan C. Williams) operate, and what's the evidence for it?
- What does research on trans men's post-transition workplace experience reveal about gendered credibility (Schilt, 2006; 2010)?
- How do stereotype threat and identity-conscious professional contexts shape women's performance and self-assessment?

### 3b. Hiring into leadership roles
- What is the state of evidence on the "glass ceiling," "broken rung," and "glass cliff" phenomena?
- Ryan & Haslam's glass cliff research; McKinsey/LeanIn's *Women in the Workplace* annual reports; Eagly & Karau's role congruity theory.
- How do likeability penalties and the "double bind" (Catalyst 2007) operate in promotion decisions?
- What's the evidence on gender composition of boards, C-suites, and VC funding? What interventions (e.g., quotas, transparency) have moved the needle?
- How does intersectionality shape the leadership pipeline (Rosette, Koval, Ma, Livingston on Black women leaders; "concrete ceiling")?

### 3c. Pay equity
- What's the current state of the gender wage gap, controlled vs. uncontrolled? (BLS, Pew, OECD data.)
- Claudia Goldin's Nobel-winning work on the "greedy job" hypothesis — how does temporal flexibility explain a large share of the gap?
- The motherhood penalty / fatherhood bonus (Correll, Benard, Paik 2007; Budig & England).
- Negotiation penalty: the evidence that women who negotiate are penalized (Bowles, Babcock, Lai 2007).
- Occupational segregation and the devaluation cycle (Blau & Kahn).

## 4. Philosophical underpinnings from 19th–20th century thinkers

**Thesis to test:** Contemporary gendered intuitions about reason, labor, and the public/private split did not fall from the sky. They were codified by specific philosophers in specific historical moments, and that codification still structures our institutions.

### §4 research questions (pending Annika's source list)
- How did Enlightenment and post-Enlightenment philosophers (Kant, Rousseau, Hegel) define "the rational subject," and how did that definition implicitly or explicitly exclude women?
- What did Wollstonecraft, Mill, and later feminist philosophers argue in response?
- How did 19th-century ideologies of "separate spheres" get philosophical justification, and how did industrial capitalism reinforce them?
- How did Weber's theory of rationalization gender the process of modernization?
- How did 20th-century feminist philosophers (Beauvoir, Arendt, Irigaray, Butler, Fraser, Nancy Hartsock) critique the gendering of reason, labor, and the public sphere?
- Where do contemporary scholars like Nancy Fraser, Silvia Federici, and Amia Srinivasan locate the philosophical stakes today?

## 5. Stoicism and the cult of logic

**Thesis to test:** The cultural elevation of logic, rationality, and emotional suppression — whether as classical Stoic virtue or modern "alpha male" self-help — has functioned to code a narrow slice of human cognition as serious and morally superior, and to code everything else as frivolous. The valorization of logic is a gender script.

### 5.1 Masculine interests and pursuits as serious and morally superior
- What did classical Stoics (Zeno, Seneca, Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius) actually argue about reason, passion, and virtue, and how gendered was that framing at the time?
- How did the Enlightenment's valorization of reason map onto gendered ideas of public virtue (Pateman, *The Sexual Contract*)?
- How does the contemporary "stoicism revival" (Holiday, Peterson, manosphere) re-stage classical Stoicism as a masculine self-help script?
- Why are STEM, finance, and law treated as serious even when they involve significant creative or interpretive work, and which structural factors (pay, prestige, licensing) hold that coding in place?
- How is "logic" used rhetorically in contemporary online discourse ("facts over feelings") as a gendered trump card? (Research on "logic bros," debate-me culture.)

### 5.2 Feminine interests and pursuits as frivolous, unserious, juvenile
- How do taste hierarchies (Bourdieu, *Distinction*) map onto gender? What's coded "highbrow" vs. "lowbrow," and how does gender inflect that?
- Academic defenses of feminine-coded genres: romance novels (Radway, Regis), pop music (rockism vs. poptimism), fashion (Entwistle), "chick lit," soap operas, teen girl fandoms.
- The "pinking" and infantilization of feminine-coded consumer culture.
- Feminist philosophy and cultural studies on why feminine pleasures are systematically dismissed (e.g., Lauren Berlant, Rita Felski, Janice Radway).
- How does the dismissal of feminine interests function politically (e.g., dismissal of "girl" issues in public policy, "frivolous" feminized professions being defunded)?

## Cross-cutting threads to watch for

- The **devaluation cycle**: fields tip female, pay and prestige decline, stereotypes harden, women get blamed.
- The **double bind**: women penalized for being too feminine (unserious) and too masculine (unlikeable).
- The **brilliance bias**: fields that believe innate genius is required are more male-dominated, regardless of actual demands.
- The **public/private split**: philosophical roots of the idea that "real" work happens in the masculine-coded public sphere.
- **Intersectionality**: Black, Latina, Asian American, and Indigenous women face compounded versions of these patterns, and white middle-class feminism has often been blind to this.
- **Class**: Many of these dynamics look different across class lines. Working-class women have always been in the paid workforce; professional-class feminism is a narrower frame.
