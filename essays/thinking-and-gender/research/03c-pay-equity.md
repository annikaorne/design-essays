# §3c — Pay equity and the gender wage gap

## Key findings

- In 2024, US women working full-time earned about 83% of men's median weekly earnings ($1,043 vs. $1,261), per BLS (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics 2025). Pew's broader measure, which includes full- and part-time hourly workers, put the 2024 ratio at roughly 85 cents on the dollar — a figure that has moved only modestly since 2003 (Kochhar 2023; Pew Research Center 2025).
- In the EU, the unadjusted gender pay gap was 12.0% in 2022 and 11.1% in 2024 (hourly earnings basis), down from 16.2% in 2011 (Eurostat 2024). Country variation is wide: from near-parity in Luxembourg to 18.8% in Estonia.
- "Controlled" or adjusted gap estimates — which condition on occupation, industry, experience, hours, and education — typically range from ~5% to ~8% in the US (Blau and Kahn 2017). This is the number critics (O'Neill and O'Neill 2012; Perry and Biggs 2014) emphasize. Proponents of the uncontrolled framing note that many of those controls (occupation, hours, tenure) are themselves gendered outcomes, not neutral "choices."
- Claudia Goldin's 2023 Nobel-recognized body of work argues the remaining gap, especially in high-earning professions, is principally generated by "greedy jobs" — roles that pay disproportionately per hour for long, inflexible, on-call availability. Because heterosexual couples typically allocate the greedy job to the man and the flexible job to the woman, couple-level "equity" is traded away (Goldin 2014, 2021; Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences 2023).
- Motherhood, not womanhood per se, drives most of the modern gap. Budig and England (2001) found a ~5% wage penalty per child net of experience; Correll, Benard and Paik (2007) showed audit and lab evidence that employers discount mothers' competence and starting salary while fathers enjoy a small bonus; Kleven, Landais and Søgaard (2019) showed the child penalty now accounts for ~80% of Denmark's gender earnings gap.
- Negotiation-based stories are real but narrower than popularly claimed. Bowles, Babcock and Lai (2007) showed women face backlash for initiating negotiations. Roussille (2024) found in engineering-hire data that an "ask gap" of 2.9% mechanically generates essentially all of the final-offer gap on that platform — and that a simple UI prefill eliminates it.
- Occupational segregation remains a major channel (Blau and Kahn 2017). Levanon, England and Allison (2009) provide causal-direction evidence that occupations lose wages after they become female-dominated, not the reverse — i.e., devaluation rather than sorting.
- Wage gaps compound with race and ethnicity. In 2023, Latinas earned ~58 cents and Black women ~67 cents per dollar earned by white men; AANHPI women earned ~93 cents; Indigenous women earn less still (IWPR 2024a, 2024b).
- Pay transparency laws show modest but consistent narrowing effects. Colorado's 2021 Equal Pay for Equal Work Act raised posted and realized pay, with the gains concentrated among women (Arnold, Quach and Taska 2024; Cullen 2023).

## Synthesis

The honest description of the contemporary evidence is that the "raw" and "adjusted" gaps both mean something, but they answer different questions.

The **uncontrolled gap** — ~83 cents (BLS 2024), ~85 cents (Pew 2024), ~11% (Eurostat 2024) — is the right number for a total-earnings question: across all work the economy currently organizes, how much less do women take home? It reflects occupational sorting, hours, tenure interruptions, promotion patterns, and overt discrimination all bundled together. Blau and Kahn (2017) show in their comprehensive *JEL* review that by 2010, human-capital variables (education, experience) had collapsed as explanatory factors — women now match or exceed men in schooling and work continuously for more years. What remains is occupation, industry, and "unexplained" residual.

The **controlled gap** — roughly 5–8 cents in the US — is the right number for a narrower question: among workers doing ostensibly the same job with the same observable credentials, how much less are women paid? Critics such as June O'Neill and Dave O'Neill (2012) and AEI scholars Mark Perry and Andrew Biggs (2014) have argued that once controls are layered in, the residual gap is near-zero, and therefore the policy inference of systemic discrimination is misplaced.

Blau and Kahn (2017) do not dispute the arithmetic; they dispute the framing. Controlling for occupation, they argue, absorbs the outcome of interest. Women are not randomly sorted across occupations, and several of the largest "controls" (hours worked, firm tenure, industry) are themselves affected by employer behavior, care responsibilities, and the structure of work. Levanon, England and Allison's (2009) panel evidence — that feminizing occupations subsequently *lose* pay, with only weak evidence for the reverse queuing story — directly challenges the assumption that occupational controls are exogenous to gender.

Goldin's work, which earned the 2023 Sveriges Riksbank Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel (Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences 2023), offers the synthesis the field has converged on. In "A Grand Gender Convergence" (Goldin 2014, *AER*) and *Career and Family* (Goldin 2021), she documents that within narrow occupations and educational categories — lawyers, MBAs, pharmacists, physicians — men and women start near parity, then diverge sharply in their 30s, precisely when children typically arrive. The divergence is largest in occupations with "nonlinear" returns to hours (law firm partners, finance), where someone working 60 hours earns more than twice someone working 30. It is nearly absent in occupations with "linear" returns (pharmacy, after technological and organizational changes made pharmacists substitutes for one another). Her argument: the modern gap is less about women being paid less for equal work than about work itself being structured to reward greedy availability, which heterosexual couples rationally allocate asymmetrically given persistent norms around caregiving.

This reframes the debate. Goldin's account is compatible with the "controlled gap is small" observation *and* with the claim that the uncontrolled gap is a real feature of how the economy treats women, because the structure of jobs — not discrimination inside a given job title — is doing most of the work. It also explains why Kleven, Landais and Søgaard (2019) find that in Denmark (a country with generous family policy and a small aggregate gap) the arrival of children still produces a ~20% long-run earnings hit that falls almost entirely on mothers, and that child penalties now account for roughly 80% of remaining gender inequality in earnings there, up from ~40% in 1980.

The motherhood-penalty literature sharpens this further. Budig and England (2001, *ASR*) first documented the ~5% per-child penalty in US longitudinal data, net of experience. Correll, Benard and Paik (2007, *AJS*) used a paired audit and laboratory design to show mothers are rated less competent, less committed, and offered ~$11,000 less in starting salary than identically credentialed non-mothers; fathers, meanwhile, are rated *more* committed and occasionally receive a bonus. Budig, Misra and Boeckmann (2012, 2016) extend this cross-nationally, finding that moderate-length leaves, public childcare, lower second-earner tax rates, and paternity leave all predict smaller penalties — but the interaction with cultural attitudes matters, and poorly targeted long leaves can entrench rather than relieve penalties.

Negotiation-based accounts explain some of what remains. Bowles, Babcock and Lai (2007) experimentally demonstrated a social penalty: female candidates who initiated salary negotiations were rated less likable and less hireable, while male candidates who did the same were not — meaning the "lean in and ask" prescription can backfire. Roussille (2024, *QJE*) uses data from Hired.com and finds an "ask gap" of ~2.9% accounts for essentially the entire final-offer gap on the platform; when the platform changed its interface to prefill a suggested ask based on median bids for comparable candidates, the gap closed to zero. The implication is dual-edged: women's initial asks do matter, but the fix is structural (better information, pay transparency) rather than behavioral exhortation.

On intersectionality, IWPR data (2024a, 2024b) and Pew (Kochhar 2023) consistently show that aggregate "women earn X cents" statistics hide large disparities. Latinas and Indigenous women face the largest gaps relative to white men; Black women face both substantial gender gaps and are concentrated in lower-wage occupations; AANHPI women's aggregate gap is smallest but masks wide heterogeneity by national-origin subgroup.

On policy: pay transparency laws in Colorado (2021), New York City (2022), California (2023), and across the EU (Pay Transparency Directive, 2023) are still too new for definitive effect sizes, but Colorado-focused evidence is encouraging. Arnold, Quach and Taska (2024, NBER WP 34480) find that Colorado employers raised posted pay by ~3.6% after the law took effect, with realized pay rising similarly and with concentrated gains for women — narrowing the state's gap without reducing male pay. Cullen's (2023) meta-review of private-sector transparency experiments similarly finds modest narrowing on the order of 1–2 log points.

## Gaps and debates

1. **How much is "discrimination" vs. "structure"?** The controlled-gap critics are not wrong that the residual within-occupation-within-experience gap is small in the US. The Goldin/Blau-Kahn response is that this understates the phenomenon because the structure of jobs is itself gendered. This is more a framing dispute than a numerical one and should be presented as such.

2. **Are greedy jobs the mechanism, or a correlate?** Goldin's hours-nonlinearity story is the contemporary consensus but not unchallenged. Critics ask whether nonlinear pay reflects genuine productivity (client continuity, deep specialization) or a coordination norm that could be unwound. Goldin (2021) herself argues remote/flexible work post-COVID could be a structural shock that weakens the nonlinearity, though early evidence is mixed.

3. **How much of the child penalty is preference vs. constraint?** Kleven et al.'s intergenerational evidence — that daughters' child penalties track their maternal grandparents' labor histories — suggests internalized norms transmitted across generations, not pure discrimination. This complicates simple "fix employers" prescriptions.

4. **Does negotiation advice help or harm?** Bowles et al. (2007) and follow-up work show women face real backlash. Roussille's (2024) finding that transparency mechanically closes the ask gap is perhaps the most actionable result in the literature.

5. **Transparency's long-run effects.** Short-run evidence is encouraging, but some theoretical work (Cullen and Pakzad-Hurson 2023, *Econometrica*) warns transparency can also *reduce* average wages by weakening worker bargaining power. The gender-specific effect appears net positive so far.

6. **The top tail.** Blau and Kahn (2017) show the gap is noticeably *larger* at the top of the distribution than at the middle. Bertrand, Goldin and Katz (2010) on MBAs, and Goldin (2014) more broadly, argue this is where greedy-job effects are sharpest. Piketty, Saez and Zucman's (2018) income-inequality work documents the compounding interaction between rising top-tail inequality and gender, though the causal mechanisms are still being worked out.
