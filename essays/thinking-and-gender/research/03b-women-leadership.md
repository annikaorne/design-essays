# §3b — Women in leadership

> Section of *Thinking and Gender* by Annika Orne. Research phase, neutral posture.
> Thesis under test: Barriers to women's leadership are not primarily pipeline problems. They are structural and interpersonal, and they are well-documented.

---

## Key findings

### 1. From "glass ceiling" to "labyrinth"

The "glass ceiling" metaphor dates to a 1986 *Wall Street Journal* series and became the dominant frame for describing a single, invisible barrier at the top of organizations. By the mid-2000s, leadership scholars argued the image had outlived its usefulness. Eagly and Carli (2007) proposed "labyrinth" as a better metaphor: women face not one terminal barrier but a series of interlocking, context-dependent obstacles at many career stages, including hiring, first promotion, networking, mentoring, work-family negotiation, and evaluation. Their *Harvard Business Review* article and accompanying book *Through the Labyrinth* argued the labyrinth frame has better diagnostic power because it directs interventions at the full path rather than at the penultimate rung (Eagly & Carli, 2007a; 2007b).

### 2. The broken rung

McKinsey and LeanIn's *Women in the Workplace* report, an annual study begun in 2015 that now covers roughly 280 US and Canadian companies and 10 million employees, consistently identifies the first promotion from individual contributor to manager, not the C-suite, as the largest pipeline leak. The 2024 tenth-anniversary report found that for every 100 men promoted to manager, only 81 women were promoted; in 2018 the number was 79 (McKinsey & LeanIn, 2024). The pattern is sharper by race: in 2024, 54 Black women and 65 Latinas were promoted for every 100 men. Because managerial attainment compounds, the broken rung explains most of the gender gap at senior levels without any additional "ceiling" being invoked. McKinsey's 2025 commentary identifies the broken rung as the single most consequential lever in the pipeline (McKinsey, 2025).

### 3. The glass cliff

Ryan and Haslam (2005) analyzed FTSE 100 appointments and found women were disproportionately appointed to board positions during periods of sustained poor company performance. They coined "glass cliff" for this pattern: women reach leadership more often in precarious conditions where failure is likely. The finding has been replicated across domains (political candidacies for unwinnable seats, law firm partnerships, university leadership) and through experimental manipulations showing that participants associate female candidates with crisis-management roles (the "think crisis, think female" stereotype).

The effect is real but contested. Morgenroth et al. (2020), a large meta-analysis, found a small overall effect size that varied by domain: evidence was stronger in political and experimental studies and weaker in CEO/management appointments. Bechtoldt, Bannier, and Rock (2019) found no glass cliff in a decade of German and UK board appointments. More recent archival work on female CEO selection (Gupta et al., 2024 and related) continues to report mixed results depending on how "precarious" is operationalized. The phenomenon is best described as context-dependent rather than universal: supported in several domains, weaker or absent in others, and sensitive to analytic choices.

### 4. Role congruity and the double bind

Eagly and Karau's (2002) role congruity theory gives the mechanism. Cultural stereotypes code the leader role as agentic (assertive, dominant, decisive) and the female role as communal (warm, nurturing, deferential). The perceived incongruity produces two forms of prejudice: women are rated less favorably than men as potential leaders, and the same agentic behavior is evaluated more harshly when performed by a woman. Rudman, Moss-Racusin, Phelan, and Nauts (2012) elaborated this into a "status incongruity hypothesis": agentic women trigger backlash specifically because they violate the gender hierarchy, and backlash intensifies when perceivers are primed with system threat.

Heilman and Okimoto (2007) identified the psychological content of the penalty: success in a male-typed task triggers an "implied communality deficit." Successful women are assumed to lack warmth, which drives lower likability, hostility, and reluctance to work for them. The penalty was attenuated when communal traits (motherhood, helping behavior) were made explicit, confirming that the issue is perceived communality, not perceived competence or agency per se.

Catalyst's (2007) *The Double-Bind Dilemma for Women in Leadership: Damned if You Do, Doomed if You Don't* synthesized these dynamics into three predicaments: women are seen as too soft or too tough but rarely right; held to a higher competence standard for lower rewards; and perceived as competent or likable but not both. Bowles, Babcock, and Lai (2007) provided complementary field evidence from negotiation: female candidates who initiated salary negotiations were penalized by evaluators (especially male ones), while male candidates were not, supplying a concrete mechanism for why negotiation advice alone cannot close pay gaps.

### 5. Intersectionality and the concrete ceiling

Intersectionality, coined by Crenshaw (1989), names the way race and gender discrimination combine rather than operate in parallel. Applied to leadership, Rosette, Koval, Ma, and Livingston (2016) showed that the "agentic deficiency" and "agentic penalty" predicted by role congruity theory do not distribute evenly across racial groups. Black women are stereotyped as dominant but not competent, which produces different backlash patterns than white women face; Asian American women are stereotyped as competent but not dominant, producing different barriers again. Earlier work by Livingston, Rosette, and Washington (2012) found Black women faced smaller agentic penalties than white women for dominant behavior but encountered sharper competence judgments. Catalyst and practitioner literature describe the compound barrier facing women of color as the "concrete ceiling": opaque where the glass ceiling is transparent, because women of color often cannot see the paths that exist for white women, let alone for men.

### 6. Boards and quotas

Norway's 2003 law requiring 40% female representation on public company boards (fully in force 2008) produced the most-studied natural experiment. Matsa and Miller (2013) found affected firms made fewer workforce reductions and showed higher short-term labor costs and lower short-term profits than Nordic controls, consistent with a different managerial style rather than an incompetence story. Later work (Ahern & Dittmar, 2012, using Norwegian market-value data) found negative valuation effects concentrated in firms furthest from compliance. Reviews (IZA World of Labor; Gibert, 2025) conclude that quotas reliably shift board composition and spill over modestly into pipeline effects, but they do not by themselves transform executive or managerial ranks.

In the US, California's SB 826 (2018) required one to three women on boards of public companies headquartered in the state. In the three years before it was ruled unconstitutional by a Los Angeles court in 2022 (on equal-protection grounds under the state constitution), female board seats rose from roughly 15% to 32% at covered firms. The EU "Women on Boards" Directive, adopted in 2022 after a decade-long impasse, requires listed companies with 250+ employees to reach 40% women on non-executive boards or 33% across all directors by mid-2026 (European Parliament, 2022). Evidence so far replicates the Norwegian pattern: binding quotas work to shift board composition; softer measures do not (Consilium, 2024).

### 7. Venture capital

VC funding shows the starkest numeric gap in the leadership-adjacent literature. PitchBook's *All In* series has tracked the share of US VC capital going to all-female founding teams since the mid-2010s. In 2024, all-female teams received roughly 1% of US VC dollars (around 2.3% globally), a five-year low; mixed-gender teams received another 20-ish percent (PitchBook, 2024). Because founder success depends on capital access, and because only about 17% of decision-makers at US VC firms with $50M+ AUM are women (PitchBook, 2024), the VC pipeline recreates gendered filtering at the earliest moment of business formation.

### 8. Interventions

Evidence on what moves the needle converges on a few points. (i) Transparency and structured evaluation: Goldin and Rouse's (2000) study of orchestra blind auditions remains the cleanest causal evidence that removing identifying information raises female hire rates, with the screen explaining 30–55% of the rise in female new hires. Structured interviews and written evaluation rubrics produce smaller but replicable effects in managerial hiring. (ii) Sponsorship over mentorship: Ibarra, Carter, and Silva (2010) showed women are over-mentored and under-sponsored; mentors advise, sponsors advocate for promotions and high-visibility assignments, and sponsorship correlates with advancement while mentorship alone does not. (iii) Binding targets: quotas and quota-like disclosure rules change board composition where voluntary targets do not (Matsa & Miller, 2013; EU Directive evidence). (iv) Joan Williams's (2014) *What Works for Women at Work* synthesizes four bias patterns, Prove-It-Again, the Tightrope, the Maternal Wall, and the Tug-of-War, and offers organization-level interventions (bias-interrupt training, calibrated performance review, pattern tracking) whose effects are documented in mid-sized organizational pilots.

### 9. *Lean In* and its critics

Sandberg's (2013) *Lean In* argued women underestimate themselves, avoid negotiation and visibility, and withdraw prematurely from career investment before having children, and urged individual women to change those patterns. Critics including bell hooks (2013, "Dig Deep: Beyond Lean In") argued the book offered a corporate, trickle-down feminism compatible with existing power structures, assuming privileged white women's advancement would benefit other women, and placing responsibility on the individual rather than on institutions. Dawn Foster's (2016) *Lean Out* extended the critique to argue that the framing serves 1% feminism while leaving the 99% worse off, and that structural remedies (unionization, childcare, paid leave, antidiscrimination enforcement) do more than self-improvement advice. Michelle Duster, Koa Beck (*White Feminism*, 2021), and others have continued the structural-feminist line. The empirical literature summarized above tends to support the critics on one key point: even when women do all the individual-level things *Lean In* recommends, the evaluative penalties documented by Heilman, Rudman, Bowles, Eagly, Catalyst, and Rosette do not disappear.

## Synthesis

The thesis that barriers to women's leadership are primarily pipeline problems (women self-select out, do not negotiate, opt out for family reasons) is not well supported by the current literature. The evidence points instead to a stack of structural and interpersonal mechanisms:

- **First-promotion attrition** (McKinsey/LeanIn broken rung) is numerically the largest gap and occurs *before* women opt out, at a career stage where family demands are typically not yet operative.
- **Role congruity and backlash effects** (Eagly & Karau; Heilman & Okimoto; Rudman et al.; Bowles et al.) supply the interpersonal mechanism: the same behaviors that produce advancement for men produce social and evaluative penalties for women.
- **The double bind** (Catalyst) is not a perception problem on women's part; it is a documented constraint in how evaluators rate them.
- **Intersectional stacking** (Crenshaw; Rosette et al.; Livingston et al.) means white, middle-class-centered accounts understate the barriers for Black, Latina, Asian American, and other women of color.
- **Structural interventions** (quotas, blind evaluation, sponsorship, transparency) produce the largest measured gains; individual-level interventions (confidence coaching, negotiation training) produce smaller and more fragile ones, partly because the environment they must operate in still penalizes the behavior being taught.

The labyrinth metaphor (Eagly & Carli, 2007) fits this literature better than the glass ceiling because the barriers are distributed across the career, not concentrated at the top. The glass cliff (Ryan & Haslam, 2005) adds a distinct pattern: in the cases where women do reach top positions, they are disproportionately placed in high-risk contexts, though the effect is domain-variable.

## Gaps and debates

- **Glass cliff replicability.** Morgenroth et al.'s (2020) meta-analysis and Bechtoldt et al.'s (2019) null result in German/UK board data show the effect is smaller and more context-dependent than the original framing implied. It is more robust in experimental and political-candidacy data than in CEO appointments. The essay should present it as a real but bounded pattern, not a universal law.
- **Quota effects on firm performance.** Matsa and Miller (2013) find reduced short-term profits; Ahern and Dittmar (2012) find negative valuation effects; other studies find no performance effect or positive long-run governance effects. The performance debate is genuinely unresolved. What is settled is that quotas change board composition where voluntary measures do not.
- **Individual vs. structural framing.** *Lean In* versus its critics maps onto a live debate about whether the optimal intervention target is behavior or institutions. The empirical literature increasingly weights structural interventions, but individual-level coaching and sponsorship still produce measurable gains, so the debate is about relative weight, not either/or.
- **Measurement of intersectional effects.** Intersectional research on Black women leaders (Rosette et al.; Livingston et al.) is strong; parallel work on Latina, Asian American, Indigenous, and disabled women leaders is thinner and often pooled in ways that obscure differences.
- **Transferability across contexts.** Most of the cited work is US and European. Nordic quota evidence may not generalize to countries with different governance models; US VC dynamics may not generalize to European venture ecosystems; and cultural variation in agency/communality stereotypes complicates cross-national comparisons.
