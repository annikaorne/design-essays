# §2.1 Feminization of marketing and social sciences

> Section research for *Thinking and Gender* (Essay 03). Neutral literature review; no opinions.
> Scope: Western (US + Europe), contemporary peer-reviewed scholarship with older foundational works cited where essential.
> Last updated: 2026-04-19

## Key findings

1. Across marketing, public relations, human resources, psychology, sociology, and anthropology, the same empirical pattern recurs: each field shifted from majority-male to majority-female between roughly the 1970s and the 2000s, and in each case the shift coincided with relative declines in pay and prestige (Reskin and Roos 1990; Grunig, Hon, and Toth 2001; Levanon, England, and Allison 2009; Mandel 2018).
2. The Levanon, England, and Allison (2009) longitudinal analysis of the 1950 to 2000 US Census finds substantial evidence for the "devaluation" view (pay falls after an occupation feminizes) and only scant evidence for the reverse-causation "queuing" view (men leave first because pay is already falling). Mandel (2018) replicates and qualifies the finding with more recent data, showing that the devaluation penalty is strongest in high-paid professional occupations and in recent decades.
3. Public relations has become the textbook case of the "velvet ghetto": women exceed 70 percent of practitioners but hold roughly 20 percent of top management roles, and the field's feminization has been accompanied by a persistent gender pay gap and status decline relative to adjacent communication professions (Grunig, Hon, and Toth 2001; Aldoory and Toth 2002; Aldoory and Toth 2021).
4. Psychology crossed the 50 percent threshold for doctorates awarded to women in the late 1980s and reached roughly 75 percent of new doctorates by the 2010s (APA Center for Workforce Studies 2017; Willyard 2011). Women remain underrepresented at the full-professor level and in the highest-paid subfields.
5. Sociology and anthropology show parallel curves: women have earned the majority of doctorates in anthropology since 1992 and the majority in sociology since the mid-1990s. Feminization has coincided with an expansion of qualitative, interpretive, and gender-focused subfields and a relative decline in disciplinary prestige in economics-dominated policy debates (Stacey and Thorne 1985; Cohen 2015; Cohen, Huffman, and Knauer 2009).
6. Marketing is a mixed case. Women hold about 60 percent of US marketing jobs (BLS-derived industry estimates) and roughly half of Chief Marketing Officer seats, but the discipline's academic self-description has migrated from quantitative-economic toward interpretive-emotional (branding, storytelling, customer empathy) across the same decades in which the workforce tipped female (England 2010; Duffy 2017; Grow and Deng 2014).
7. The most robust finding in the literature is directional, not absolute: once controls for skill, education, and task demands are in place, feminized work pays less than otherwise-equivalent masculinized work, and the same occupation pays less after it feminizes (Levanon, England, and Allison 2009; England, Budig, and Folbre 2002; Mandel 2018; Jiang 2025).
8. Causal debate remains live. Goldin's (2002) "pollution" theory, Reskin and Roos's (1990) queuing theory, and England's devaluation theory each explain part of the pattern. Recent work suggests they are not mutually exclusive and operate on different timescales (Jiang 2025; Mandel 2018).

## Synthesis

### The general pattern: occupational feminization and the devaluation cycle

The empirical literature converges on a three-step sequence. First, a formerly male-dominated occupation opens to women, usually because of some combination of labor shortage, technological or organizational change, declining relative pay, and anti-discrimination legislation. Second, the female share rises, often quickly. Third, the occupation's relative pay and prestige decline, and cultural descriptions of the work soften toward empathy, service, communication, or care (Reskin and Roos 1990; England 2010).

Levanon, England, and Allison (2009) provide the most-cited causal test. Using fixed-effects models on linked US Census data from 1950 through 2000, they compare the same occupation across decades. They find that a rise in the female share predicts a subsequent decline in median pay, controlling for education and skill demands, while the reverse relationship (pay decline predicts later feminization) is weak. The authors interpret this as support for the "devaluation" perspective: employers and labor-market institutions implicitly lower the valuation of work once it becomes associated with women.

Mandel (2018) revisits the Levanon, England, and Allison findings with data through 2010 and introduces further controls for selection into occupations. She largely replicates the devaluation finding but nuances it: the wage penalty for feminization is concentrated in high-paying professional occupations and has grown over time. Jiang (2025) extends this with linguistic data, showing that the cultural prestige of feminizing occupations, as measured by how they are described in American English corpora from 1900 to 2019, also declines in tandem with demographic change.

England, Budig, and Folbre (2002) and subsequent work on the "care penalty" (Folbre 2018) extend the devaluation argument to jobs involving nurturance: nursing, teaching, social work, childcare. They find a wage penalty that persists after human capital controls and argue it reflects the cultural association of such work with unpaid maternal labor.

### Public relations: the "velvet ghetto"

PR is the most studied case of occupational feminization. The phrase "velvet ghetto" originates in a 1986 IABC-commissioned report of the same name and was taken up by public relations scholars as a framework for persistent gender stratification inside a feminizing field (Cline et al. 1986, cited in Grunig, Hon, and Toth 2001). The Reskin and Roos (1990) case study of PR identified it as one of the paradigmatic feminizing occupations of the 1970s and 1980s.

Grunig, Hon, and Toth's (2001) *Women in Public Relations* synthesizes a longitudinal PRSA and IABC survey program. Women crossed the 50 percent threshold in PR practitioner ranks in the 1980s and reached roughly 70 percent by 2000. The authors document a wage gap ranging from about 20 to 30 percent at entry and widening with tenure, a glass ceiling in agency leadership, and a stated perception among practitioners that the field's overall organizational prestige declined during the same period. Aldoory and Toth (2002) deepen this with interview and focus-group data, showing that women practitioners are disproportionately channeled into "technician" roles (writing, media relations) while men move into "manager" roles (counsel to CEO, strategy).

Aldoory and Toth's (2021) *The Future of Feminism in Public Relations and Strategic Communication* updates the empirical picture: the pay gap has narrowed but not closed, minority women face compounded disadvantages, and PR remains a "double glass ceiling" field in which women dominate numerically but not structurally.

### Psychology: dramatic tipping

Psychology's gender composition has changed more sharply than that of any other large US discipline. APA Center for Workforce Studies data show that women began earning more psychology doctorates than men in the late 1980s, reached 73 percent of new doctorates by 2008, and roughly 75 percent by 2017 (Willyard 2011; APA 2017; Clay 2017). Among psychology graduate students overall, women reached about 75 percent by 2014 (APA 2018).

The explanatory literature identifies several co-acting factors: the post-1970s expansion of applied, clinical, counseling, and health subfields that attracted women at higher rates than experimental psychology; the decline of behaviorism and rise of relational, developmental, and community orientations that aligned with women's stated research interests; the 1972 passage of Title IX, which accelerated women's doctoral enrollment generally; and feedback loops whereby female faculty attracted female students (Willyard 2011; APA 2017).

As with PR, numerical majority has not produced proportional leadership. APA data (Clay 2017) show women concentrated in lower-paid practice settings (clinical, counseling, school psychology) and underrepresented in higher-paid academic experimental and industrial-organizational subfields, in department chair roles, and among full professors. Relative faculty pay in psychology has declined against economics, computer science, and the hard sciences over the same period (APA 2017).

### Sociology and anthropology: parallel curves

Women earned the majority of sociology doctorates in the US beginning in the mid-1990s, and the female share has remained near 60 percent since (Cohen 2015). Anthropology crossed the threshold earlier: women have earned the majority of anthropology doctorates since 1992 and nearly 70 percent of undergraduate anthropology degrees by the 2010s (Speier 2015, *Chronicle of Higher Education* data summary). In both fields, men continue to hold the majority of full professor positions (Cohen 2015).

Stacey and Thorne's (1985) landmark "The Missing Feminist Revolution in Sociology" argued that despite the numerical rise of women and the growth of feminist theory, core sociological frameworks remained gender-blind. Their follow-up reflections (Stacey and Thorne 1996; Adams 2015) acknowledge partial integration of feminist perspectives but argue that economics-style quantitative methods retain higher prestige than the qualitative, ethnographic, and feminist-theoretical methods that have grown alongside feminization.

The anthropology literature describes a similar pattern: a numerical majority of women at lower ranks, continuing male dominance at the top, and a proliferation of subfields focused on gender, sexuality, and embodiment that correlates with rising female representation (Brodkin, Morgen, and Hutchinson 2011).

### Marketing: from "hard" to "soft"

Marketing is the most theoretically interesting case for the thesis that cultural re-coding accompanies demographic change. BLS-linked industry data show women holding approximately 60 percent of US marketing specialist and analyst jobs in the 2020 to 2021 range, up from rough parity in the 1990s (BLS 2021, via industry workforce reports summarized in Grow and Deng 2014 and contemporary industry estimates). The female share of Chief Marketing Officer positions in the US reached roughly 50 percent by 2020, the highest female representation of any C-suite role.

Alongside this demographic shift, the academic and professional self-description of marketing has changed. Through the mid-twentieth century, marketing's dominant intellectual frame was economic and quantitative: the 4 Ps (Kotler and colleagues), econometric demand estimation, and operations-research-style optimization. From roughly the 1990s onward, the field absorbed substantial influence from consumer culture theory (Arnould and Thompson 2005), branding and storytelling (Holt 2004), and relational and experiential marketing frames (Schmitt 1999). Academic marketing journals have grown more interpretive, and practitioner discourse has converged on "empathy," "customer journey," and "brand narrative" as central competencies (Duffy 2017; Grow and Deng 2014).

Grow and Deng (2014) document gender dynamics within advertising creative departments specifically, finding that the perception of "creative" work as male-coded persists even as marketing broadly feminizes, with women concentrated in account management and research while men retain creative-director roles. Duffy's (2017) study of women in branded-content and social-media marketing describes the "aspirational labor" of digital marketing work as simultaneously feminized and devalued.

### HR: a long-feminized field that continued to feminize

Human Resources is distinctive because its roots in early-twentieth-century "welfare work" were already feminized (Pringle 1988; Scheibmayr and Reichel 2024). Roos and Manley (1996) document the share of US women in HR rising from 27.3 percent in 1970 to 53.3 percent by 1990, with similar patterns in the UK and Australia. Contemporary HR is roughly 70 percent female at the practitioner level. Ainsworth and Pekarek (2022) and Scheibmayr and Reichel (2024) argue that HR has struggled to achieve professional status relative to finance, law, or strategy, and that this status gap tracks both its female composition and its association with "soft" interpersonal work. Reichel and colleagues find that HR departments lose organizational status when female HR directors are appointed, controlling for departmental performance, consistent with the devaluation view.

### Intersectional picture

Whitening and classing of the feminization story matters. Early women entrants into PR, marketing, HR, psychology, sociology, and anthropology were disproportionately white, middle-class, and college-educated (Grunig, Hon, and Toth 2001; Reskin and Roos 1990). Black, Latina, Asian American, and Indigenous women entered these fields in meaningful numbers later and continue to face compounded wage and advancement gaps (Aldoory and Toth 2021; APA 2017; Rosette et al. 2016). In psychology specifically, women of color are overrepresented in clinical-practice roles and underrepresented in academic and experimental subfields (APA 2017). This uneven entry reshapes the "feminization" story: the fields did not feminize uniformly across race and class, and the devaluation penalty has not been distributed equally across women.

## Gaps and debates

### The causal chicken-and-egg

Three mechanisms compete, and recent work suggests they coexist rather than exclude one another.

- **Devaluation (Paula England; Levanon, England, and Allison 2009; Mandel 2018):** the cultural association of an occupation with women lowers its perceived value and, through institutional inertia in wage-setting, its pay. Strongest direct empirical support.
- **Queuing (Reskin and Roos 1990):** employers prefer men; men take the best jobs first; women enter occupations men are leaving because pay, conditions, or prospects are already declining. Supported for some occupations (pharmacists, book editors) but weak in aggregate panel tests.
- **Pollution (Goldin 2002):** because employers cannot observe individual women's productivity, the entry of women into an all-male occupation signals to outsiders that the occupation's skill threshold is lower, reducing its prestige. More theoretical than empirical, but corroborated by Jiang's (2025) linguistic prestige analysis.

Jiang (2025) combines census and corpus data from 1900 to 2019 and finds that cultural prestige (as measured by language) declines alongside demographic feminization, consistent with both devaluation and pollution mechanisms. The question of whether cultural coding precedes or follows demographic change remains contested.

### Marketing as a stress test

Marketing complicates the thesis in interesting ways. It has feminized less completely than PR or psychology, it remains well-paid at the top (CMO compensation has not obviously declined), and it has absorbed quantitative intensification (marketing analytics, data science) alongside its interpretive turn. Whether marketing's bifurcation, with quantitative "analytics" subfields coded male and interpretive "brand and content" subfields coded female, represents stabilization at a new equilibrium, or an earlier stage of a devaluation trajectory, is an open empirical question (Duffy 2017; Grow and Deng 2014).

### Measurement and endogeneity

The main methodological debate concerns whether feminization measures pick up something real or whether they are correlated with unmeasured skill shifts (routinization, technological change, declining unionization). Mandel (2018) and Jiang (2025) address this with time-varying skill and task controls and find the devaluation effect robust, but the debate continues.

### The "soft" versus "hard" re-coding

A persistent gap in the literature is rigorous empirical work on the claim that feminized fields are rhetorically re-coded from rigorous and analytical to soft and interpretive. Jiang's (2025) linguistic analysis is the most direct evidence to date and supports the claim at the aggregate level, but discipline-specific studies (e.g., tracking changes in marketing's journal-article abstracts, or in psychology's departmental mission statements) remain rare.

### Cross-cutting concern: intersectional data availability

Race- and class-disaggregated wage and composition data for feminizing professions remain patchy, especially before 2000. Most devaluation studies treat women as an aggregate, which underestimates the compounded effects on women of color (Rosette et al. 2016; Aldoory and Toth 2021).
