# §1 — Creative thinking across disciplines

> Literature review for §1.1 of *Thinking and Gender* (working title).
> Scope: Western (US and Europe), contemporary peer-reviewed scholarship with foundational older work where essential.
> Posture: Neutral synthesis. Findings and disagreements presented without authorial opinion.
> Last updated: 2026-04-19.

## Key findings

- The conceptual wall between "creative" and "analytical" cognition is historically recent. It congeals in two main moments: the Romantic reconstruction of the artist as an expressive, emotive genius (circa 1790 to 1830), and the mid-twentieth-century institutional split between "the two cultures" that C.P. Snow described in 1959. Renaissance and early-modern thinkers had no such category wall, which is why Leonardo is read today as anomalous rather than typical of his period.
- Contemporary cognitive neuroscience finds strikingly similar network dynamics across creative domains. Divergent thinking, poetry composition, musical improvisation, and scientific idea generation all show cooperation between the default mode network (associated with spontaneous thought) and the executive control network (associated with goal-directed evaluation). The same general-purpose machinery is recruited whether the output is a theorem or a sonnet.
- Scientists and mathematicians themselves describe their work using the vocabulary of beauty, play, intuition, and imagination. This is not merely rhetorical; philosophers of science (McAllister, Chandrasekhar) show aesthetic judgment is a load-bearing element of theory choice, and cognitive studies of scientists at work (Dunbar) show analogical and imagistic reasoning are the primary mechanisms of discovery.
- Whether creativity is best understood as a single domain-general capacity or a cluster of domain-specific abilities is actively contested. Baer and Kaufman argue for domain specificity; Simonton, Sawyer, and much of the network-neuroscience literature support substantial domain-general substrates. The evidence is genuinely mixed and depends on how creativity is operationalized.
- There is robust empirical evidence that creativity, like scientific competence, is attributed unequally by gender. Proudfoot, Kay, and Koval (2015) found the same creative output is rated more creative when attributed to a man. Leslie and colleagues (2015) found women are underrepresented in fields whose members believe "brilliance" is required. Carli and colleagues (2016) found scientist stereotypes overlap with male stereotypes and diverge from female stereotypes. Together these establish that the cultural coding of "rigor" and "creativity" is not gender-neutral.

## Synthesis

### 1. Historical emergence of the creative-analytical divide

The literature situates the modern separation of creative from analytical cognition as a relatively recent construction with two identifiable inflection points.

The first is the Romantic reinvention of the artist. M.H. Abrams' foundational *The Mirror and the Lamp* (1953) argues that before the late eighteenth century, Western aesthetic theory was dominantly mimetic: literature and art were understood as mirrors reflecting the world according to craft rules, and the artisan's job was skilled construction. Abrams traces the shift from "mechanical" to "organic" theories of creation, in which art became a lamp projecting the inner life of an expressive self. Wordsworth's 1800 preface to *Lyrical Ballads*, defining poetry as "the spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings," marks the moment this reorientation consolidates (Abrams 1953). Subsequent historians of the Romantic period extend this account: the period produces "the artist" as a distinct social type, characterized by emotive interiority, individual genius, and separation from craft labor and from natural science (Engell 1981).

Before this, the literature on the Renaissance polymath assumes a quite different taxonomy. Martin Kemp's work on Leonardo (Kemp 2006; Kemp 2011) frames Leonardo not as an anomaly who spanned two cultures but as a paradigmatic participant in a single one: the Renaissance workshop tradition in which anatomy, optics, hydraulics, painting, and engineering were treated as interlocking problems of representing and manipulating nature. Kemp's point is precisely that Leonardo looks astonishing only from within a later taxonomy that does not accommodate him. Pamela Smith's *The Body of the Artisan* (2004) makes the complementary argument that early-modern craftspeople understood their empirical skill as itself a mode of natural knowledge; the separation of "making" from "knowing" postdates them.

The second inflection point is post-war institutional. C.P. Snow's 1959 Rede Lecture, *The Two Cultures and the Scientific Revolution*, diagnosed what Snow saw as an entrenched cultural split between "literary intellectuals" and "scientists" in British and American elite education. Snow's historical claim has been revised (see Ortolano 2009, who treats the "two cultures" less as a description than as an intervention in Cold War cultural politics), but historians agree the post-war period saw the professionalization of scientific research, the consolidation of the modern research university, and the institutional separation of laboratories from studios (Kaiser 2005). The coding of art as expressive-emotional and of science and engineering as rigorous-analytical hardens as curricula, funding, and credentials bifurcate.

Historians of computing show the same dynamic inside STEM itself. Ensmenger (2010) documents how mid-century programming shifted from low-status feminized clerical work into a high-status masculine profession partly through aptitude tests and personality profiles that selected for traits culturally coded as masculine (antisocial, detail-obsessed, mathematical). The net effect is the post-war taxonomy in which "creative" and "analytical" read as opposing orientations rather than complementary faculties.

### 2. Shared cognitive substrates: what the science says

Contemporary cognitive science broadly finds that mathematical, scientific, and artistic creativity recruit overlapping mechanisms, though the literature is careful about how much weight to place on "domain-general" claims.

The cognitive work identifies several core processes that appear across domains. Divergent thinking, operationalized through tasks such as the Alternative Uses Test, correlates modestly but reliably with real-world creative achievement across domains (Runco and Acar 2012). Analogical reasoning, mapping structure from one domain onto another, appears in mathematical proof, scientific hypothesis generation, and artistic metaphor alike (Holyoak and Thagard 1995; Dunbar 1995). Working memory and executive function support the maintenance and manipulation of the elements being recombined (Benedek et al. 2014). Mental imagery and "body thinking" (kinesthetic simulation) appear in the self-reports of mathematicians, physicists, and choreographers (Root-Bernstein and Root-Bernstein 1999).

The neuroscience literature is the most striking site of convergence. Beaty, Benedek, Silvia, and Schacter (2016), reviewing roughly two decades of neuroimaging work, report that creative tasks as disparate as divergent thinking, poetry composition, jazz improvisation, and visual design all show cooperation between the default mode network, associated with spontaneous and self-generated thought, and the executive control network, associated with goal-directed cognitive control. These two networks typically show an antagonistic relationship during ordinary task performance; creative cognition is distinctive in that they engage together (Beaty et al. 2016). Jung and colleagues (2013; Jung et al. 2015) frame this in Darwinian terms: default-mode activation supplies "blind variation" (novel recombinations) and executive-control activation supplies "selective retention" (evaluation and pruning). Subsequent work has extended this to musical improvisation (Limb and Braun 2008) and scientific problem-solving (Kounios and Beeman 2014).

Keith Sawyer's *Explaining Creativity* (2012) synthesizes these findings into a "sociocultural" account: creativity depends on domain expertise, but the underlying cognitive architecture is broadly shared. Sawyer explicitly pushes back on the popular idea that creativity is the opposite of analysis; in his reading, both involve similar recombinatorial and evaluative processes operating on different materials.

### 3. Imagination, aesthetics, and intuition in scientific self-report

A substantial literature documents that scientists and mathematicians describe their own cognition in terms that the post-Romantic taxonomy would reserve for artists.

The classic text here is Jacques Hadamard's *The Psychology of Invention in the Mathematical Field* (1945), built around a survey of Einstein, Polya, Wiener, and other leading mathematicians and physicists. Hadamard documents mental imagery, incubation, unconscious processing, and "aesthetic selection" as core to mathematical discovery. Einstein's contribution to the Hadamard survey, often quoted, describes his thinking as "combinatory play" with visual and muscular elements, with words entering only secondarily. Henri Poincaré's 1908 lecture "Mathematical Creation" (reprinted in Poincaré 1914) makes the same point in the first person: the mathematician's selection among possibilities is guided by a sense of elegance and harmony, and the moment of insight follows a period of incubation.

The philosophy of science literature treats these reports as more than rhetoric. James McAllister's *Beauty and Revolution in Science* (1996) argues systematically that aesthetic criteria such as symmetry, simplicity, and unity do substantive work in theory choice, with what he calls the "aesthetic canon" shifting during scientific revolutions. Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar's *Truth and Beauty* (1987) offers a Nobel laureate's extended first-person account of the motivating role of beauty in physics, citing Heisenberg, Dirac, and Einstein. Dirac's often-quoted 1963 statement that physical theories should be judged first by their mathematical beauty is among the most cited data points in this literature.

Kevin Dunbar's in vivo studies of scientific laboratories (Dunbar 1995; Dunbar 2001) provide the most direct empirical support for intuition and analogy in working science. Observing molecular biology labs over many months, Dunbar found that analogical reasoning, often from distant domains, accounted for a substantial share of hypothesis generation and problem-solving, especially at key transitions. Richard Feynman's well-known emphasis on "play" as a mode of doing physics (Feynman 1985) is a popular-register articulation of the same pattern.

Arthur I. Miller's *Einstein, Picasso* (2001) is a historical synthesis arguing that early-twentieth-century physics and early-twentieth-century visual art were jointly responding to the same crisis of classical representations of space and time, with Poincaré's non-Euclidean geometry circulating through both. The argument is not that Einstein and Picasso did the same thing but that the standard partition between their "kinds of thinking" misdescribes the history.

### 4. Empirical evidence for overlapping cognitive and neural resources

The claim that creativity in STEM and the arts draws on overlapping resources is supported by several converging lines of evidence, with some caveats.

At the behavioral level, meta-analytic work on divergent-thinking tests shows modest positive correlations with real-world creative achievement across scientific and artistic domains (Runco and Acar 2012; Kim 2008). People with high creative achievement in one domain are more likely than chance to show above-average creative achievement in other domains, though the correlations are not large (Silvia et al. 2012).

At the neural level, Beaty and colleagues' (2018) large-sample study of brain connectivity found that a single functional connectivity pattern, integrating default, executive, and salience networks, predicted individual differences in divergent-thinking performance robustly across samples. This pattern tracked individual differences in real-world creative accomplishment across art and science domains. Follow-up work has extended these network findings to scientific creativity specifically (Beaty and Kenett 2023).

Dean Keith Simonton's historiometric studies provide a complementary, population-level body of evidence. Across large samples of eminent creators, Simonton (1999, 2009) finds similar distributions of productivity, similar age-of-peak-performance curves, and similar personality correlates (openness, tolerance for ambiguity, persistence) across scientists, artists, and writers, suggesting shared underlying mechanisms at the level of creative careers.

Root-Bernstein and Root-Bernstein's *Sparks of Genius* (1999) documents extensively that elite scientists and mathematicians are disproportionately engaged in serious artistic pursuits compared to the general population, a pattern also found by Robert Root-Bernstein's later quantitative analyses of Nobel laureates (Root-Bernstein et al. 2008), though these studies rely on self-report and have methodological limits.

The significant counter-literature is the domain-specificity tradition, led by John Baer and James Kaufman (Baer 2010; Kaufman and Baer 2005). Their argument, based on studies showing low cross-domain correlations in rated creative products, is that "creativity" as a single construct may be misleading, and that expertise and practice in a specific domain largely determine creative output there. Sawyer (2012) and Simonton (2009) read much of the same evidence as compatible with a "hybrid" position: substantial domain-general cognitive substrates, combined with large domain-specific expertise requirements for high-level creative work. The live debate, rather than overturning the overlap findings, complicates any strong claim that a single "creativity" faculty explains outputs across fields.

### 5. Gendered attribution of creativity and rigor

The literature provides clear empirical evidence that "creativity" and "rigor" are attributed in gender-patterned ways, though researchers differ on mechanisms.

The most directly relevant finding comes from Proudfoot, Kay, and Koval (2015), who, in two experiments, showed that participants rated identical creative outputs as more creative when attributed to a man than to a woman. In their architectural-design study, the male architect was rated as more creative than the female architect despite producing the same design; no such difference appeared when participants evaluated fashion designers, a domain coded feminine. The authors interpret this through a trait-overlap account: creative ability is culturally associated with agentic traits (independence, boldness, risk-taking) that are stereotypically masculine, and this association transfers to attribution of creative merit.

Leslie, Cimpian, Meyer, and Freeland (2015), in *Science*, surveyed US academics across thirty disciplines and found that the proportion of women with PhDs in a field was strongly predicted by the extent to which practitioners believed "raw, innate talent" or "brilliance" was required. Fields whose members endorsed brilliance-as-requirement most strongly (philosophy, physics, economics, mathematics, computer science) had the fewest women; fields emphasizing empathy or hard work had more. The mechanism they propose is that brilliance is stereotypically ascribed to men, so fields that treat it as a prerequisite implicitly screen out women. Follow-up studies have replicated the brilliance-bias association (Bian, Leslie, and Cimpian 2017, with children; Storage et al. 2020). The Leslie et al. findings attracted methodological critique (Ginther and Kahn 2015), and the authors have responded with additional analyses; the basic correlation between brilliance beliefs and underrepresentation has generally held across replications, though its causal status is contested.

Carli, Alawa, Lee, Zhao, and Kim (2016) compared stereotypes of women, men, and "successful scientists" and found that scientist stereotypes overlapped substantially with male stereotypes (agentic, analytical, cold) and diverged from female stereotypes (communal, warm). Their follow-up finding is important for §1's thesis: in scientific subfields with more women, stereotypes of scientists in that subfield become less agentic-coded and more communal-coded, consistent with the gender coding of traits being a function of who holds the role rather than of the work itself.

The broader bias literature reinforces the pattern. Moss-Racusin and colleagues (2012) found that science faculty rated identical application materials as more competent, more hireable, and worth a higher starting salary when the applicant's name was male; the gender of the evaluator did not moderate the effect. Cheryan, Ziegler, Montoya, and Jiang (2017), reviewing the literature on why computer science, engineering, and physics are more gender-skewed than biology, chemistry, and mathematics, converge on three drivers: masculine cultural defaults that signal low belonging for women, unequal early exposure, and self-efficacy gaps, all of which interact with the coding of these fields as requiring a particular kind of cognitive "type."

On the complementary question, whether women are disproportionately coded "intuitive" or "creative" rather than "rigorous" for the same work, the direct empirical evidence is sparser. The Proudfoot et al. (2015) finding is often cited as evidence that creativity itself is masculine-coded, which complicates any simple "men get rigor, women get intuition" claim. Luksyte and colleagues (2018) extend Proudfoot et al. by showing that women whose workplace behaviors are labeled creative receive less credit for them and that creativity carries a smaller reward for women's performance evaluations than for men's. There is also a literature on the "prove it again" bias showing women's competence is scrutinized more skeptically than men's (Williams and Dempsey 2014), which bears on the "rigor" side of the attribution question. Taken together, the evidence supports a general pattern in which both rigor and creativity, when they appear in fields culturally coded masculine, are credited more readily to men; but sharper claims about specific attributional asymmetries warrant careful scoping.

## Gaps and debates in the literature

- **Domain generality vs. specificity.** This is the most live debate. Baer and Kaufman's specificity tradition has generated real empirical pressure on domain-general claims, but network-neuroscience findings (Beaty et al. 2016, 2018) provide counter-evidence for shared substrates. Most contemporary researchers hold a hybrid view, but its precise form is unsettled.
- **Causal status of brilliance bias.** Leslie et al. (2015) report a strong correlation; critics (Ginther and Kahn 2015) argue field-level variables like math-intensiveness may confound it. Replications with children (Bian et al. 2017) help but do not fully resolve the causal question.
- **How much aesthetic criteria do in theory choice.** Philosophers of science disagree on whether aesthetic judgment is a reliable epistemic guide (McAllister 1996 offers a measured yes) or a post-hoc rationalization. This matters for how seriously to take scientists' first-person reports.
- **Historical scope of the "two cultures" narrative.** Revisionist historians (Ortolano 2009) treat Snow's formulation as itself a Cold War intervention rather than a neutral description, complicating the clean "post-war split" story.
- **Gendered creativity attribution outside STEM and architecture.** Proudfoot et al.'s finding is consistent across their studies but has been tested in a limited set of domains. Whether identical creative output is always attributed more generously to men, or whether the effect depends on whether the domain is masculine-coded, is still being mapped.
- **Intersectional gaps.** Most of the gender-bias studies cited here treat gender as binary and sample from predominantly white populations. The extent to which brilliance and creativity attributions interact with race is less studied, though Rosette and colleagues' work on Black women in leadership suggests compounding effects worth tracking.
