# §5.2 — Feminine pursuits as frivolous

## Key findings

1. **Taste hierarchies are not gender-neutral.** Bourdieu's *Distinction* (1984) established that judgments of taste are socially produced markers of class position. Feminist cultural studies has argued this framework is incomplete without gender: the traits coded as "legitimate" culture (distance, formal complexity, restraint) track closely onto traits coded masculine, while the "popular" register Bourdieu devalued (immersion, emotion, sentiment, the body) maps onto the feminine (Felski 1995; Skeggs 1997; Huyssen 1986).

2. **Feminine-coded genres have been systematically treated as aesthetically inferior.** A durable body of scholarship from roughly 1982 onward has shown that romance novels, soap operas, melodrama, gossip, chick lit, teen girl fandoms, and pop music consumed by young women are dismissed on grounds that scholars argue would not hold if the same features appeared in masculine-coded forms (Modleski 1982/2008; Radway 1984; Ang 1985; Williams 1991; Regis 2003; Harzewski 2011).

3. **The rockism/poptimism debate is the clearest contemporary case of gendered taste critique.** Sanneh's 2004 *NYT* intervention named "rockism" as an ideology that privileged (masculine, white, guitar-based, "authentic") rock while devaluing (feminine, Black, pop, manufactured) alternatives. Carl Wilson's *Let's Talk About Love* (2007; expanded 2014) extended this into a book-length study of Celine Dion fandom that explicitly draws on Bourdieu to argue taste judgments function as social sorting.

4. **The infantilization and "pinking" of girl culture is a marketing phenomenon with political reach.** Orenstein (2011) documents how a narrow pink-princess monoculture was deliberately engineered by Disney in the early 2000s. Scholarly work on girlhood studies (Projansky 2014; Cooper 2018) extends this to argue that "girl" operates simultaneously as diminutive (infantilizing adult women) and as a racialized category (white girlhood is protected; Black girlhood is adultified).

5. **Women's sports coverage provides a quantitative backbone to the "dismissal" thesis.** Cooky, Messner, and Musto (2015) found that across twenty-five years, women's sports received only 2–3% of televised sports news coverage, and that this figure had not improved, contradicting the "they just need to get better/more popular" narrative.

6. **The counter-view (craft-based hierarchy) has serious advocates but is internally contested.** Some critics maintain that complexity, formal innovation, and difficulty are defensible criteria independent of gender. Wilson (2014) is explicit that poptimism should not collapse into "everything is equally good"; the scholarly task is to separate genuine craft arguments from gender-coded assumptions smuggled in as craft arguments.

7. **Reclamation scholarship has accelerated since ~2000.** Contemporary work on romance (Regis 2003; Roach 2016), fashion (Entwistle 2015; Steele across her oeuvre), reality TV, rom-coms, and fandom has moved these objects from margin to center of cultural studies, though the broader culture-industry hierarchies remain largely intact.

## Synthesis

### Taste hierarchies theory and the feminist refinement

Bourdieu's *Distinction* (1984; French original 1979) argued that cultural taste functions as a mechanism of class reproduction: what counts as "good taste" is what the dominant class possesses, and access to that taste is distributed through education and habitus. The apparatus of legitimate culture (opera, fine art, literary fiction) appears neutral but encodes the dispositions of a particular class.

Feminist cultural studies has pushed this framework in two directions. First, scholars argue the distinction Bourdieu draws between "legitimate" and "popular" culture is already gendered. Huyssen (1986), writing roughly contemporaneously, coined the phrase "mass culture as woman" to describe the long modernist tradition of coding the popular, the emotional, the consumable, and the mass-produced as feminine, while coding high modernism as virile, austere, and masculine. Felski (1995) extends this in *The Gender of Modernity*, demonstrating that nineteenth- and twentieth-century accounts of what counted as "serious" modern culture were built on the systematic exclusion of feminine cultural production (sentimental fiction, fashion, consumer culture) from the category of modernity proper.

Second, scholars like Skeggs (1997) show that taste hierarchies map onto class *and* gender simultaneously. In her ethnographic study of working-class British women, "respectability" operated as a form of cultural capital whose value depended on gendered performance; the same tastes that afforded middle-class women distinction became evidence of vulgarity when performed by working-class women. The critique is not that Bourdieu was wrong but that his framework needs gender and race to be complete.

### Case studies by genre

**Romance novels.** Modleski's *Loving with a Vengeance* (1982/2008) and Radway's *Reading the Romance* (1984) are the foundational interventions. Radway, working at the University of North Carolina, conducted ethnographic research with readers at a Midwestern bookstore and argued that the act of reading romance was itself a form of claimed time and pleasure for women whose lives were structured around serving others. Regis (2003) extends this by tracing romance to Richardson and Austen and making a literary-critical case for its formal seriousness. Roach (2016) builds on this tradition with *Happily Ever After: The Romance Story in Popular Culture*, arguing that romance is the last cultural form almost wholly produced by and for women, and is systematically excluded from the literary canon for precisely that reason.

**Pop music and the rockism/poptimism debate.** Sanneh's "The Rap Against Rockism" (*New York Times*, 2004) is field-defining rather than scholarly. He argued that rock-critical orthodoxy privileged a narrow set of values (authenticity, "real" instruments, singer-songwriter sincerity) while dismissing the pop, R&B, and hip-hop forms that dominated the actual musical landscape, and that this privileging was racially and gender-coded. Carl Wilson's *Let's Talk About Love* (2007; expanded 2014 Bloomsbury edition) is the book-length scholarly-adjacent treatment: Wilson explicitly mobilizes Bourdieu to ask what it means that a critic like himself hates Celine Dion, whose listeners are disproportionately women, immigrants, and working-class. The expanded edition includes essays from Ann Powers, Sheila Heti, and others. Earlier academic work (Lewis 1990, *Gender Politics and MTV*) and Hills (*Fan Cultures*, 2002) documented how teen girl fandoms (Beatlemania as the paradigm case) have been coded as "hysteria" in ways male fandoms are not.

**Fashion.** Entwistle's *The Fashioned Body* (2000; 2nd ed. 2015) argues fashion is a serious site of embodied social practice that academic sociology and cultural studies long ignored precisely because it was coded feminine and commercial. Elizabeth Wilson's *Adorned in Dreams* (1985/2003) makes a related case, historicizing fashion as modernist practice. Steele's work at FIT has institutionalized fashion studies as an academic field.

**Soaps, melodrama, chick lit.** Ang's *Watching Dallas* (1985) interviewed Dutch *Dallas* viewers and argued that melodramatic pleasure is a structured, articulate response to emotional realism, not a failure of discernment. Linda Williams' work on melodrama (1991, 2001) argues melodrama is not a devalued genre but a fundamental *mode* of American popular narrative, including in works coded masculine. Harzewski (2011) situates chick lit in a genealogy running from the novel of manners through Bridget Jones, arguing the dismissal of chick lit as "unserious" recapitulates earlier dismissals of domestic fiction.

**Teen girl fandoms.** Hills (2002) in *Fan Cultures* argues fan studies has too often pathologized young female fans while celebrating (masculine-coded) cult fandom. Contemporary work extends this to One Direction, K-pop fandoms, the Beyhive, and Swifties, consistently finding that the analytic, organizational, and interpretive labor of these fandoms is substantial and routinely overlooked.

### Feminine pleasure as a political category

Berlant's *The Female Complaint* (2008) develops the concept of the "intimate public" to describe how feminine culture (Oprah, Hallmark, sentimental fiction) constructs a form of belonging that is neither trivial nor politically radical; it is a structured affective commons. McRobbie's *The Aftermath of Feminism* (2008) argues post-1990s culture has performed a "double entanglement" in which feminism is both taken into account and repudiated; the cultural forms addressed to young women (makeover TV, consumer empowerment, individualized success narratives) simultaneously acknowledge and defuse feminist critique. Douglas (1994; 2010) tracks the parallel American trajectory: *Where the Girls Are* documented how mass media shaped second-wave-era girlhood, while *The Rise of Enlightened Sexism* (Times Books, 2010) names the contemporary regime in which sexist tropes are reanimated under the cover of ironic self-awareness. Zeisler (2016) extends this into "marketplace feminism," the corporate-commercial appropriation of feminist language that leaves feminine-coded pleasure consumerized rather than politicized.

### "Girl" as cultural position

Girlhood studies has become a substantial subfield in the past fifteen years. Projansky's *Spectacular Girls* (NYU Press, 2014) argues contemporary media simultaneously adores and disdains girls, and that this ambivalence is structured by whiteness and heteronormativity. Orenstein's *Cinderella Ate My Daughter* (2011), though popular rather than academic, documents the deliberate engineering of a pink-princess monoculture by Disney in the early 2000s. Cooper (2018) in *Eloquent Rage* and in her academic work at Rutgers argues Black girlhood is systematically denied the category of "girl" altogether: Black girls are "adultified" in ways that deny them the protections (and the frivolous pleasures) white girlhood is granted. Harris (2023) in *Wannabe* extends this from memoir-criticism: growing up as a Black girl consuming 1990s white-dominated pop culture (Sex and the City, Spice Girls, Clueless) produced a specific kind of cultural doubling that the "Black Friend" trope foreclosed.

### Political consequences

The Cooky, Messner, and Musto (2015) longitudinal study in *Communication & Sport* is the single cleanest empirical anchor: across twenty-five years, televised sports news devoted 2–3% of airtime to women's sports, and the gap was *larger* in 2014 than in 1989. The argument that coverage tracks popularity collapses on examination, since coverage decisions precede and shape popularity. Analogous patterns appear in funding for "women's health" research (chronically underfunded relative to disease burden), in the wage structures of feminine-coded professions (nursing, teaching, social work, librarianship), and in the policy framing of "women's issues" as niche rather than general.

### Reclamation

A reclamation wave has moved across scholarly and critical writing since roughly 2000. Romance studies (Regis; Roach; the International Association for the Study of Popular Romance, founded 2009), fashion studies (Entwistle; Steele), fan studies (Hills; Jenkins), reality TV studies, and rom-com criticism have all matured as subfields. Critics like Ann Powers on pop music, Wesley Morris on Black popular culture, and Harris on 1990s girl culture have carried the argument into mainstream journalism. The frame has shifted from "is this worth studying?" to "what does serious study look like?"

The countervailing position, fairly stated, holds that some hierarchies do track craft, complexity, or formal innovation, and that dismantling all taste hierarchies risks a flatness in which distinction is impossible. Wilson (2014) takes this objection seriously and argues the task is not to abandon judgment but to make one's sociological position as a judge explicit. The stronger scholarly consensus is that craft-based hierarchies are defensible in principle but that, in practice, gender coding has so saturated our inherited hierarchies that "craft" arguments must be examined case by case.

## Gaps and debates

- **The craft vs. gender-coding question is not settled.** The honest scholarly debate is over how much of the hierarchy is salvageable on craft grounds once gender is bracketed, and what the evidentiary standard should be.
- **Intersectionality is uneven.** Work on white middle-class feminine culture (romance, chick lit, fashion) is mature; work on how taste hierarchies operate within feminine-coded culture (Black pop vs. white pop, telenovelas vs. prestige TV, K-pop vs. indie) is newer and less consolidated.
- **The digital shift is understudied.** Most canonical texts precede TikTok, stan culture, and the post-2015 pop culture landscape. How platform affordances reshape the dismissal-and-reclamation dynamic is an open question.
- **Male feminine-coded consumers remain largely invisible.** When men consume romance, K-pop, or fashion, they are often reclassified (as "ironic," as gay-coded, as cultural outliers) in ways that preserve the gendered hierarchy.
- **The "dismissal" thesis can flatten.** Not every critique of a feminine-coded form is gendered dismissal, and distinguishing gendered dismissal from ordinary critical disagreement remains methodologically difficult.
