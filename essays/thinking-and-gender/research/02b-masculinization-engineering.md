# §2.2 - Masculinization of engineering and computing

## Key findings

- In both the United States and Britain, the earliest electronic computing labor (1940s to 1950s) was substantially performed by women and was coded culturally as feminized, low-status clerical or scientific-support work. This pattern is documented across Light (1999), Abbate (2012), Ensmenger (2010), Hicks (2017), and Evans (2018).
- Between roughly the mid-1950s and the 1970s, programming was reconfigured as a masculine, high-status technical profession. Mechanisms include professionalization and credentialing, aptitude and personality tests that selected for "antisocial" traits, the founding of computer science as an academic discipline, GI Bill era employer preferences, and structural workforce policies (Ensmenger 2010; Hicks 2017; Abbate 2012).
- The shift was not merely cultural but institutional and economic. Hicks (2017) shows the British civil service reclassified its majority-female computing workforce into dead-end grades in the 1960s, then attempted to replace them with (scarce) male managers; Britain's early lead in computing collapsed as a direct consequence.
- Quantitatively, women's share of U.S. computer science bachelor's degrees rose from 13.6 percent in 1970 to a peak of 37.1 percent in 1984, then declined to about 18 percent by 2010-2011 (NCES, reported in AEI 2018; Zweben and Bizot tracking for CRA). Computing is the only major STEM field to have moved backward on this metric.
- Contemporary representation across engineering sub-fields is uneven. Biomedical engineering is the most gender-balanced of the large engineering sub-fields, while computer, electrical, and mechanical engineering remain heavily male. NSF/NCES data for 2020-2021 show women earning about 45 percent of biomedical engineering bachelor's degrees versus roughly 14 percent in computer engineering and about 15 percent in mechanical engineering (NCSES 2023).
- Leslie, Cimpian, Meyer, and Freeland (2015) find that fields whose practitioners believe success requires innate "brilliance" have lower female (and lower African American) PhD representation, net of other factors. This correlation spans STEM and the humanities and helps explain why philosophy and physics pattern together, and why molecular biology and social psychology pattern together.
- Classroom-level interventions demonstrably move the needle. Cheryan and colleagues show that stereotypical physical environments in CS depress women's sense of belonging and interest (Cheryan et al. 2009); Margolis and Fisher (2002) document Carnegie Mellon's structural curricular reforms that took female enrollment in the CS major from 7 percent in 1995 to 42 percent in 2000.

## Synthesis

### Who did the earliest computing work?

Before "computer" denoted a machine, it denoted a person, typically a woman, who performed repetitive mathematical calculations. Light's (1999) canonical article documents that the six primary programmers of ENIAC, the first general-purpose electronic digital computer, were women: Kathleen McNulty Mauchly Antonelli, Jean Jennings Bartik, Frances Elizabeth "Betty" Snyder Holberton, Marlyn Wescoff Meltzer, Frances Bilas Spence, and Ruth Lichterman Teitelbaum. Light shows that press coverage and official histories effectively erased them in the immediate postwar period, even though contemporary reporting had noted women's wartime entry into technical roles (Light 1999).

In Britain, women constituted roughly three-quarters of the Bletchley Park workforce at its January 1945 peak of nearly 10,000 personnel, with Wrens (Women's Royal Naval Service members) operating Colossus and Bombe machines on round-the-clock shifts (Smith 2015; Women in Bletchley Park, Wikipedia compilation of primary sources). Approximately 273 women worked directly on Colossus, the world's first programmable electronic digital computer (Copeland 2006).

In the United States, Grace Hopper programmed the Harvard Mark I from 1944, wrote the first compiler (A-0) in 1952, and later led the committee that produced COBOL in 1959 (Beyer 2009; Britannica entry on Hopper). Margaret Hamilton led the MIT Instrumentation Laboratory software team that wrote Apollo's on-board flight software, and is credited with coining the term "software engineering" in the mid-to-late 1960s precisely to claim disciplinary legitimacy for what was then dismissed work (Hamilton, in Rayl 2008; Smithsonian 2017). Ada Lovelace's 1843 notes on Babbage's proposed Analytical Engine provide an earlier symbolic precedent but are not structurally continuous with the 20th-century pattern.

The cultural framing in the 1940s and early 1950s treated programming as tedious clerical work analogous to typing or telephone switchboard operation, contrasted with the "real" engineering of hardware (Light 1999; Ensmenger 2010; Abbate 2012). Evans (2018) extends this genealogy into the internet era, profiling figures such as Elizabeth "Jake" Feinler, who ran the early ARPANET directory.

### How did software re-code as masculine?

Ensmenger's (2010) *The Computer Boys Take Over* provides the most detailed U.S. account. He traces programming's trajectory from "low-status, largely feminized labor" in the 1940s through reinvention as a glamorous "black art" practiced by "computer cowboys" in the 1950s, and then rationalization in the 1960s as the academic discipline of computer science and the software engineering profession. The professionalization project, including professional societies, certification, academic credentials, and codes of ethics, was framed by employers as a response to the "software crisis" but functioned simultaneously as a gatekeeping mechanism (Ensmenger 2010).

A critical mechanism was aptitude and personality testing. Cannon and Perry (1966), working for the System Development Corporation, interviewed roughly 1,400 programmers (about 1,200 men) to build a Vocational Interest Scale. They concluded programmers "don't like people" and were distinguished by disinterest in others alongside interest in puzzles. As Ensmenger (2010; 2015 blog companion) argues, using a male-skewed sample to define the "ideal programmer" personality produced a self-reinforcing loop: antisocial, mathematically inclined men were selected in, which ratified the stereotype, which shaped recruiting and curricula. The IBM Programmer Aptitude Test (PAT), widely adopted in the 1960s, further narrowed the pipeline toward mathematics-credentialed candidates, who were disproportionately male.

Hicks (2017) provides the British companion case in *Programmed Inequality*. Drawing on newly opened civil service archives, she shows that the UK government, the largest single computing employer in Britain through the 1960s, systematically reclassified its feminized machine operator workforce into low-paid, dead-end grades even as computing's strategic importance grew. When the government attempted to consolidate computing labor into a new (higher-paid, male-coded) management class in the late 1960s, it discovered it could not recruit enough men and had deliberately shut women out of the promotion track. Hicks's thesis is that this gendered technocratic restructuring directly contributed to the collapse of Britain's computing industry, including the failed ICL merger trajectory. The book's argument is that the U.S. is not immune to the same dynamics.

Abbate (2012) synthesizes both cases and adds texture via 52 oral histories with American and British women in computing. She documents the 1960s redefinition of programming as "software engineering," the rise of commercial firms run by women (Elsie Shutt's Computations Inc.; Stephanie Shirley's F International in the UK), and the ways women navigated, resisted, or exited a changing occupational identity. Abbate's contribution is to show that the masculinization was contested and uneven, not a clean top-down reclassification.

### The brilliance bias and field-level variation

Leslie, Cimpian, Meyer, and Freeland (2015), surveying roughly 1,800 U.S. academics across 30 disciplines, found that field-specific beliefs that success requires "a special aptitude that just can't be taught" correlate strongly and negatively with women's representation at the PhD level (R squared on the order of 0.6 in their primary analysis). Philosophy, physics, mathematics, and computer science rank high on the brilliance measure and low on women's representation; molecular biology, neuroscience, and psychology rank lower on brilliance and higher on women's representation. The correlation holds across STEM and the humanities, undermining explanations that rely purely on math intensity or work-life balance. Critics (Ginther and Kahn 2015, Comment in *Science*) have noted that selectivity and GRE scores explain part of the variance, but the brilliance signal persists in replication work (Meyer, Cimpian, and Leslie 2015; Bian, Leslie, and Cimpian 2017 on children as young as six endorsing the stereotype).

### Contemporary engineering composition

NSF's National Center for Science and Engineering Statistics (NCSES 2023, *Diversity and STEM*) reports that women earned roughly 24 percent of engineering bachelor's degrees in the U.S. in 2020-2021, up from 19 percent in 2011. The within-engineering variation is striking. Biomedical engineering reached approximately 45 percent women by 2020, while mechanical engineering sat near 15 percent, electrical engineering near 14 percent, and computer engineering near 14 percent (NCSES 2023; Roy 2022 for ASEE data). Several explanations coexist in the literature. Biomedical engineering is a newer field (most U.S. departments are post-2000) and is framed around human health, which may reduce stereotype threat and signal communal goals (Diekman et al. 2010 on communal goal congruity). Mechanical and electrical engineering carry older, hands-on, artifact-oriented cultures that Faulkner (2000, 2007) theorizes as constitutively gendered through a technical/social dualism.

Computer science's trajectory is distinctive. NCES data show women's share of U.S. CS bachelor's degrees peaked at 37.1 percent in 1984 and declined to a floor around 18 percent by 2010-2011 (Camp 1997 for early documentation of the decline; AEI 2018 for the longer series). Abbate (2012) and Ensmenger (2010) link the decline to the marketing of home computers as boys' toys in the early 1980s (Cassidy 2001), the tightening of undergraduate CS curricula to favor students with prior experience, and the cultural consolidation of the "geek" identity. Margolis and Fisher (2002) demonstrated at Carnegie Mellon that systematic curricular reform (removing the assumption of prior experience, diversifying faculty, changing recruitment language) raised women's share of entering CS majors from 7 percent in 1995 to 42 percent in 2000. Cheryan et al. (2009; Master, Cheryan, and Meltzoff 2016) show that even object-level cues in a classroom (Star Trek posters vs. nature images) shift women's reported interest and sense of "ambient belonging" in CS, with no effect on men.

### Pipeline effects

AAUW's *Why So Few?* (Hill, Corbett, and St. Rose 2010) synthesizes the K-12 and undergraduate literature: U.S. girls and boys take roughly equal math and science coursework through high school and perform comparably, yet women are substantially less likely to declare STEM majors as first-year college students, and the gap widens across engineering sub-fields by graduation. The report identifies stereotype threat, implicit bias in evaluation, and departmental climate as proximate causes, and documents intervention evidence (growth-mindset framing, early research exposure, female-faculty representation). Cheryan, Ziegler, Montoya, and Jiang (2017) extend this with a meta-analysis finding that masculine cultures, lack of early experience, and lower self-efficacy jointly account for much of the gender gap in CS, physics, and engineering, while mathematics and biology do not show the same pattern.

## Gaps and debates

- Historians broadly agree on the empirical claim that computing defeminized between the 1940s and 1970s. They disagree on emphasis. Ensmenger stresses the professionalization project and the aptitude-testing regime; Hicks stresses state policy and civil-service restructuring; Abbate stresses the heterogeneity of women's responses (some exited, some founded firms, some stayed and reframed the work). These are complementary rather than contradictory.
- The brilliance-bias literature is contested on magnitude and mechanism. The original Leslie et al. (2015) finding replicates, but Ginther and Kahn (2015) argue that field selectivity mediates part of the effect, and some economists dispute the causal direction. Proponents respond that children's endorsement of the stereotype by age six (Bian et al. 2017) is hard to square with a pure selectivity story.
- Causation in the 1980s CS decline is debated. Candidate causes include the personal computer marketed to boys, the tightening of CS curricula around students with prior exposure, broader cultural shifts, and the "clubhouse" dynamic Margolis and Fisher identify. No single-factor account is accepted.
- Biomedical engineering's relative gender balance is often attributed to communal goals framing, but this has not been cleanly tested against the alternative that BME is a newer field without entrenched cultural gatekeeping. The two explanations may be observationally equivalent.
- There is genuine disagreement about how much weight to give psychological/stereotype explanations versus structural/institutional explanations. Faulkner (2000) and Hicks (2017) argue structural explanations are primary; Cheryan et al. (2009, 2017) and Bian et al. (2017) argue psychological mechanisms are real and tractable through environment design. Most contemporary scholars accept both operate, but the relative weights remain unsettled.
- Finally, scholars disagree about implications for contemporary policy. Margolis and Fisher (2002) and AAUW (Hill et al. 2010) emphasize demonstrable intervention success at the curricular and classroom level. Hicks (2017) is more pessimistic, noting that structural fixes often fail when the underlying occupational prestige economy remains gendered.
