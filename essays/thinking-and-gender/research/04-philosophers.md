# §4 — Philosophical underpinnings

A parallel literature review on 19th–20th century thinkers who codified gendered conceptions of reason, labor, and the public/private split, and on the feminist philosophers who have interrogated that codification.

## Key findings

1. The modern "rational subject," as articulated across Rousseau, Kant, and Hegel, was not neutrally universal. It was constructed through explicit and repeated contrast with femininity, which was associated with particularity, feeling, the domestic, and nature (Okin 1979; Lloyd 1984; Pateman 1988).
2. The earliest sustained philosophical objections, Wollstonecraft (1792) and the Mills (1851, 1869), did not reject reason as such. They argued that women's apparent irrationality was the predictable effect of denied education and civil status, not a natural fact (Wollstonecraft [1792] 1992; Mill [1869] 1988; Taylor Mill [1851] 1998).
3. Nineteenth-century "separate spheres" ideology was reinforced by evolutionary biology and medicine, which claimed scientific warrant for women's confinement to the private sphere (Russett 1989; Ehrenreich and English [1978] 2005).
4. Twentieth-century feminist philosophy, beginning with Beauvoir (1949) and culminating in Lloyd, Pateman, Fraser, Butler, Federici, and Srinivasan, has argued that the apparent neutrality of reason, the social contract, the public sphere, and the wage relation conceal a sexed structure that continues to shape contemporary institutions.
5. The field contains serious internal debates, notably Fraser versus Butler on recognition and redistribution, materialist feminists versus Butler on performativity, and disputes over how to read Beauvoir's "immanence/transcendence" framework (Fraser 1995, 1998; Butler 1998; Moi 1999; Heinämaa 2003).

## Synthesis

### Enlightenment and post-Enlightenment codifications (late 18th to early 19th century)

Rousseau's *Emile* (1762), Book V, sets out a sharply differentiated education for Sophie. Women, Rousseau argues, are to be "passive and weak," formed to please and to be subject to men's judgment, and their reason is to be "practical" rather than speculative. Susan Moller Okin's *Women in Western Political Thought* (1979) treats this passage as a paradigm case: a political philosopher building a putatively universal account of the citizen while reserving a functionally subordinate role for women that the rest of the system then depends upon. Okin's reading has shaped subsequent secondary scholarship.

Kant's account is more complicated and, for that reason, more revealing. In the *Anthropology from a Pragmatic Point of View* (1798) and in the earlier *Observations on the Feeling of the Beautiful and Sublime* (1764), Kant associates women with the "beautiful understanding" rather than the "deep understanding," with taste rather than principle, and he excludes them from active citizenship in the *Metaphysics of Morals* (1797) on the grounds that they lack civil independence. Hannah-Barbara Gerl-Falkovitz, Pauline Kleingeld (1993), and Robin May Schott (ed., 1997) have documented both the exclusions and the interpretive question of whether Kant's transcendental arguments require them or merely express his historical prejudices.

Hegel's *Elements of the Philosophy of Right* (1821), especially §§158–169, locates women in the family as the sphere of "immediate" ethical life and men in civil society and the state. §166 is frequently cited by feminist commentators because Hegel there states that women's "substantial vocation" lies in the family and that if women were at the head of government the state would be in danger. Benhabib (1992), Pateman (1988), and Hutchings (2003) read Hegel as the clearest case of a philosopher who makes the sexual division of labor constitutive of the rational state rather than accidental to it.

Genevieve Lloyd's *The Man of Reason* (1984) is the canonical synthesis of this material. Lloyd argues that from Philo and Augustine through Descartes, Rousseau, Kant, and Hegel, "reason" has been defined by what it excludes, and what it has repeatedly excluded has been coded feminine: body, emotion, sensuous particularity, the domestic. Lloyd is careful: she does not claim reason is "really" masculine, only that its Western symbolic construction has been built through gendered contrast. Her book reframes the question from "were these philosophers sexist?" (trivially yes) to "does the conceptual architecture of modern reason carry that sexing with it?" (a live question).

### 19th-century responses and the hardening of separate spheres

Wollstonecraft's *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman* (1792) is a direct reply to Rousseau. She accepts the Enlightenment premise that reason is the ground of moral personhood and argues that women have been denied the education that would let them exercise it. Her reception was sharply polarized: celebrated in radical circles, vilified after Godwin's posthumous *Memoirs* (1798) exposed her private life, and largely unread in mainstream Anglophone philosophy until the late twentieth century (Taylor 2003; Bergès and Coffee, eds., 2016).

Harriet Taylor Mill's "Enfranchisement of Women" (1851) in the *Westminster Review* and J. S. Mill's *The Subjection of Women* (1869) extended the argument using utilitarian and liberal premises. Mill's claim that "what is now called the nature of women is an eminently artificial thing" anticipates later constructionist arguments by almost a century. Contemporary reception was mixed: the book was translated across Europe and cited by suffrage movements, but was dismissed by much of the academic establishment (Collini 1991; Tulloch 1989).

Alongside these liberal arguments, the nineteenth century produced an elaborate biological and medical apparatus justifying women's confinement to the private sphere. Herbert Spencer's *The Principles of Biology* (1864–1867) and his *Principles of Sociology* (1876–1896) treated the sexual division of labor as an evolutionary adaptation. Cynthia Eagle Russett's *Sexual Science* (1989) documents how "craniometry," energy-conservation arguments, and claims about women's "arrested development" gave nineteenth-century separate-spheres ideology a scientific veneer. Barbara Ehrenreich and Deirdre English's *For Her Own Good* ([1978] 2005) and Carroll Smith-Rosenberg's work on hysteria and neurasthenia trace the parallel medical literature, in which women's attempts to enter public and intellectual life were pathologized as causes of nervous collapse.

### Early and mid-twentieth century

Max Weber's account of rationalization in *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* (1905) and *Economy and Society* (1922) did not thematize gender. Roslyn Wallach Bologh's *Love or Greatness: Max Weber and Masculine Thinking* (1990) argues that Weber's typology of action, especially *Zweckrationalität* (purposive-rational action), implicitly coded the public, bureaucratic, disciplined sphere as masculine and relegated affective, relational, and erotic life to a residual category that Weber himself associated with the feminine and the domestic. Bologh reads Weber with Weber: she uses his own texts on the erotic sphere and on the tension between vocation and private life to show that his sociology requires, but does not acknowledge, a gendered division of labor.

Simone de Beauvoir's *The Second Sex* (1949) is the hinge text of twentieth-century feminist philosophy. Beauvoir argues that "one is not born, but rather becomes, a woman," and frames women's situation through two key oppositions: Self/Other and immanence/transcendence. Woman has been constructed as the Other against which the male subject defines himself, and has been confined to immanence (repetition, biological reproduction, the maintenance of life) while men claim transcendence (project, creation, history). Toril Moi's *What Is a Woman?* (1999) and Sara Heinämaa's *Toward a Phenomenology of Sexual Difference* (2003) have corrected a generation of Anglophone misreadings, arguing that Beauvoir is working in a phenomenological register (Husserl, Merleau-Ponty) rather than advocating that women should become men or escape embodiment.

Hannah Arendt's *The Human Condition* (1958) distinguishes labor (biological necessity, the household), work (fabrication of the durable world), and action (speech and deed in the public realm). Arendt does not foreground gender, but her account of the *oikos* as the sphere of necessity (in which women and slaves were confined in the Greek polis) and her valorization of the public realm have attracted decades of feminist commentary. Adrienne Rich and Mary O'Brien read Arendt as reinscribing the very hierarchy she describes. Linda Zerilli's *Feminism and the Abyss of Freedom* (2005) and Bonnie Honig's edited volume *Feminist Interpretations of Hannah Arendt* (1995) offer a more generative reading: Arendt's category of action, detached from her own ambivalence about the "woman question," is a resource for feminist accounts of political agency.

### Late twentieth-century feminist theory

Carole Pateman's *The Sexual Contract* (1988) reads the canonical social-contract theorists (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Kant) as telling only half the story. The "original contract" that founds civil society, Pateman argues, is also a sexual contract: a prior agreement among men that secures orderly access to women's bodies and labor. The fraternal pact replaces paternal right but does not abolish men's collective authority over women. Pateman's reading has been influential and contested; see Nancy Fraser's qualified endorsement in *Justice Interruptus* (1997) and Charles Mills's parallel argument in *The Racial Contract* (1997), which Pateman and Mills jointly extended in *Contract and Domination* (2007).

Luce Irigaray's *Speculum of the Other Woman* (1974) and *This Sex Which Is Not One* (1977), and Hélène Cixous's "The Laugh of the Medusa" (1975), argue that the Western philosophical tradition operates on a "masculine imaginary" in which the feminine can only appear as lack, mirror, or matter. Their project of *écriture féminine* is frequently read (e.g., by Toril Moi in *Sexual/Textual Politics*, 1985) as a strategic essentialism. The relation to analytic feminist philosophy is uneasy; Nussbaum has criticized Irigaray sharply, while Schor and Whitford have defended her as a diagnostician of symbolic structure rather than a metaphysician of gender.

Nancy Fraser's work moves from her critique of Habermas's "The Structural Transformation of the Public Sphere" in "Rethinking the Public Sphere" (1990) through *Justice Interruptus* (1997) to *Fortunes of Feminism* (2013). Fraser argues that the bourgeois public sphere was constituted by the exclusion of women, workers, and racialized subjects, and that contemporary feminism must combine claims for recognition with claims for redistribution. Her critique of "progressive neoliberalism" in *Fortunes of Feminism* and later essays argues that a certain strand of liberal feminism has been co-opted by capitalism, trading structural demands for representational gains. Fraser's exchange with Judith Butler ("Merely Cultural," *New Left Review*, 1997; Fraser's reply "Heterosexism, Misrecognition, and Capitalism," 1998) is one of the most important intra-feminist debates of the 1990s.

Judith Butler's *Gender Trouble* (1990) and *Bodies That Matter* (1993) argue that gender is performative, constituted through the repetition of stylized acts rather than expressive of a prior substance. Butler draws on Beauvoir, Foucault, and speech-act theory, and extends Lloyd's and Irigaray's claim that the "subject" of philosophy is already sexed. Materialist feminists (Martha Nussbaum's "The Professor of Parody," 1999, is the sharpest example) have argued that Butler's framework evacuates the economic and institutional terrain on which women's subordination is reproduced. The debate is ongoing; Butler's more recent work on precarity and grievability can be read as a partial response.

Silvia Federici's *Caliban and the Witch* (2004) adds a historical-materialist dimension. Federici argues that the gendered division of labor we inherit was not a slow evolution but a violent product of the transition to capitalism: the witch hunts of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, the enclosure of the commons, and the expulsion of women from waged work together constructed the "housewife" and the unpaid reproductive laborer as categories functional to primitive accumulation. Federici extends arguments from the 1970s Wages for Housework campaign (Dalla Costa and James 1972) and anticipates recent social reproduction theory (Bhattacharya, ed., 2017).

### Contemporary scholarship

Martha Nussbaum's *Sex and Social Justice* (1999) uses the capabilities approach to argue for a liberal feminism grounded in universal human functioning. Her position is explicitly opposed to both Butler's anti-essentialism and Irigaray's symbolic analysis, and it provides a useful foil for readers testing where their own commitments sit. Amia Srinivasan's *The Right to Sex* (2021) returns to questions of desire, power, and the limits of liberal autonomy, and is notable for refusing easy synthesis between liberal, Marxist, and post-structuralist feminist traditions.

Recent work on care ethics (Kittay 1999; Tronto 2013) and on social reproduction (Fraser 2016; Bhattacharya 2017) connects the nineteenth-century separate-spheres literature to present debates about the welfare state, unpaid domestic labor, and the gendered composition of "essential work" made visible by the COVID-19 pandemic.

## Gaps and debates

- **Reading charity vs. structural critique.** Does the sexism of Rousseau, Kant, and Hegel contaminate their systems, or can the systems be salvaged by separating "the arguments" from "the prejudices"? Okin and Pateman press the structural reading; Kleingeld and Nussbaum often press the reparative one.
- **Beauvoir's immanence/transcendence.** Does this framework devalue reproductive and bodily life, as Mary O'Brien and some ecofeminists have charged, or is it a phenomenological description of a situation, as Moi and Heinämaa argue?
- **Arendt and the woman question.** Arendt herself was dismissive of organized feminism. Whether her categories can be appropriated against her is an active dispute (Honig 1995; Zerilli 2005).
- **Recognition versus redistribution.** Fraser and Butler's 1997–98 exchange remains the reference debate for whether cultural/symbolic analysis and political-economic analysis are complementary or in tension.
- **Performativity versus materialism.** Butler's influence in the humanities has been enormous, but materialist feminists (Federici, Fraser, Nussbaum in different registers) argue that the wage relation, the witch hunt, and the welfare state do work that discursive analysis alone cannot.
- **Western canon boundaries.** This review, per brief, focuses on Western (US and European) sources. The Black feminist tradition (Hill Collins, Davis, hooks) and postcolonial critiques (Spivak, Mohanty) intersect with every section above and are essential companion reading, not covered here.
