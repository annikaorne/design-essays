# Rousseau

A neutral literature review on Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778), his treatment of women, and the scholarly debate over whether that treatment is structural or separable from his political philosophy.

## Primary texts and what he actually said (with quotations)

Rousseau's treatment of sexual difference is not confined to one text. It appears in the *Discourse on the Origin of Inequality* (1755), the *Letter to d'Alembert on the Theatre* (1758), *Julie, or the New Heloise* (1761), *The Social Contract* (1762), and most systematically in Book V of *Emile, ou de l'éducation* (1762). Each text does different work. The *Discourse on Inequality* gives the anthropological backstory; *Julie* dramatizes the domestic ideal; the *Letter to d'Alembert* defends the exclusion of women from public life; *Emile* sets out the educational program that produces them; and *The Social Contract* describes the polity that requires them while saying almost nothing about them.

In the *Discourse on Inequality*, Rousseau begins from a state of nature in which the sexes are roughly equal because both are solitary and self-sufficient. Women are physically weaker but not subordinate, because there is no family, no property, and no division of labor to enforce dependence. Subordination enters with the formation of the first families and the emergence of property. Commentators disagree on whether Rousseau thinks this is a fall, a necessity, or a convenience; what is not disputed is that once social life begins, Rousseau describes a complete sexual division of labor as the condition of peaceable society (Okin 1979; Schwartz 1984; Lange 1979). Scholars have long flagged the tension: if women and men are equal in nature, the inequalities that follow cannot be read back into nature without contradiction (Lange 1979; Weiss 1993).

*Emile*, Book V contains Rousseau's most quoted and most scrutinized passages. Sophie, Emile's destined wife, is educated under a principle of asymmetry. "The man should be strong and active; the woman should be weak and passive," Rousseau writes; "the one must have both the power and the will; it is enough that the other should offer little resistance" (*Emile* V, Bloom trans. 1979, 358). He grounds this asymmetry in a functional theory of sexual complementarity: because women must please men to secure their protection and the legitimacy of their children, their education is ordered entirely to that end. "The whole education of women ought to be relative to men," Rousseau writes. "To please them, to be useful to them, to make themselves loved and honored by them, to educate them when young, to care for them when grown, to counsel them, to console them, and to make life agreeable and sweet to them, these are the duties of women at all times, and what should be taught them from their infancy" (*Emile* V, Bloom trans. 1979, 365). Sophie's reason is to be "practical" rather than speculative; her virtue is modesty, chastity, and shame; her authority is confined to the home.

The *Letter to d'Alembert on the Theatre* (1758) opposes the establishment of a theatre in Geneva and, in the course of that argument, articulates one of the clearest statements of Rousseau's public/private division. A theatre, Rousseau argues, would mix the sexes in public, corrupt republican mores, and efface the modesty that keeps women at home. He contrasts Paris, where women rule by means of salons and flirtation and thereby enervate male citizens, with Geneva, where women's invisibility in public is what permits men's virtue. The Letter is widely read as a defense of the natural order of traditional sex roles and as a warning that mixing the sexes in public corrupts both (Landes 1988; Elshtain 1981; Schwartz 1984). Joan Landes's *Women and the Public Sphere in the Age of the French Revolution* (1988) treats the Letter as a pivotal document in the eighteenth-century suppression of the salonnières and the construction of a masculinist republican public sphere.

*Julie, or the New Heloise* (1761) translates these prescriptions into narrative. Julie, after an adolescent love affair with her tutor Saint-Preux, marries the older Wolmar and takes up residence at Clarens, a small estate run by the couple as an extended household. Clarens is frequently read as Rousseau's positive vision of domestic order: Wolmar governs from reason, Julie reigns from affection, servants and tenants are treated humanely, pleasures are measured, and nature is tended rather than dominated. The household models a form of life in which women's rule is real but confined to the domestic interior (Shklar 1969; Fermon 1997; Morgenstern 1996). Most structural critics read Clarens as a political allegory: the well-ordered state, like the well-ordered household, depends on a woman who has renounced her passions in favor of duty (Okin 1979; Weiss 1993; Fermon 1997).

*The Social Contract* (1762) is, by contrast, strikingly silent on gender. It elaborates the general will, popular sovereignty, and republican citizenship in a register that appears to be universal. Yet the citizen of *The Social Contract* is, in the language of Rousseau's other works, a man whose domestic life has been organized in advance along the lines set out in *Emile* and *Julie*. Feminist commentators have treated this silence as the central interpretive problem: is the citizen-subject of *The Social Contract* tacitly gendered, and if so, does that gendering contaminate the democratic theory or merely reflect the historical blinders of its author (Pateman 1988; Okin 1979; Lange 1981; Weiss 1993)?

## The structural reading

The dominant line in feminist political theory since the late 1970s has argued that Rousseau's sexism is not a detachable prejudice but a constitutive feature of his system. Susan Moller Okin's *Women in Western Political Thought* (1979) is the founding statement. Okin argues that Rousseau's account of the citizen requires the domestic sphere he constructs in *Emile* and *Julie*: republican virtue is sustained by a particular kind of family, which is in turn sustained by a particular kind of woman. The general will presupposes a citizen who is not distracted by luxury, corrupted by salon manners, or entangled in the competitive sexual politics Rousseau attributes to contemporary Paris. That citizen is produced by Sophie's work. Okin concludes that one cannot simply extract *The Social Contract* from its anthropological and educational scaffolding without dismantling the system.

Lynda Lange's "Rousseau: Women and the General Will," in Clark and Lange's *The Sexism of Social and Political Theory* (1979), sharpens this claim. Lange argues that Rousseau's general will is formally universal but substantively exclusive. Women are not merely absent from the assembly; they are structurally excluded because citizenship as Rousseau conceives it is defined against the dependence, particularity, and affective ties associated with women. Lange's later essay, "Rousseau and Modern Feminism" (*Social Theory and Practice*, 1981), situates Rousseau as the test case for whether modern political theory can be decoupled from its original gendering.

Carole Pateman's *The Sexual Contract* (1988), although broader in scope, places Rousseau at the center of its argument. Pateman reads Rousseau's social contract as the paradigmatic "fraternal pact": the contract that founds civil society is also a prior agreement among men that secures orderly access to women's bodies, labor, and reproduction. The family is not outside the contract but its precondition. Pateman reads Rousseau alongside Hobbes, Locke, and Kant to argue that the canonical theory of modern democracy is a theory of male citizenship underwritten by a sexual subcontract.

Joel Schwartz's *The Sexual Politics of Jean-Jacques Rousseau* (1984) is the first book-length study of the role of sexuality and the family across Rousseau's corpus. Schwartz's reading is less polemical than Okin's or Pateman's, but he arrives at a broadly structural conclusion: sexual difference is not incidental but foundational to Rousseau's political anthropology. Without the asymmetrical complementarity of Emile and Sophie, Rousseau has no account of how natural men become moral citizens or how republics sustain themselves against corruption.

Jean Bethke Elshtain's *Public Man, Private Woman* (1981) reads Rousseau as one of the architects of the modern public/private division and argues that the gendering of that division is not accidental. Elshtain is more ambivalent than Okin: she argues that the private sphere has been systematically devalued and that feminist theory should resist simply assimilating the private to the public. But on the descriptive question—whether Rousseau's political theory requires a gendered division of spheres—Elshtain sides with the structural reading.

Penny Weiss's *Gendered Community: Rousseau, Sex, and Politics* (1993) offers the most sustained case that Rousseau's appeal to sexual difference is strategic rather than naturalistic. Weiss argues that Rousseau does not ground sexual difference in nature (his own anthropology in the Second Discourse forbids this) but constructs it as a political instrument: sexual differentiation, he thinks, produces the mutual dependence that makes community possible. Weiss then argues that this political construction of gender fails on Rousseau's own terms, undermining the community it was meant to found. She sides with the structural reading while refining what "structural" means: the sexism is not a first-order metaphysical claim about women but a second-order functional requirement of Rousseau's account of social cohesion.

Nicole Fermon's *Domesticating Passions: Rousseau, Woman, and Nation* (1997) extends the structural reading into nationalism. Fermon argues that Rousseau's household at Clarens is the model for his nation: both are communities of sentiment that discipline passion through domestic authority, and in both the woman is the affective glue. Fermon treats Rousseau as foundational to modern romantic nationalism and argues that the gendered logic of Clarens is carried forward into the nineteenth-century nation-state.

Mira Morgenstern's *Rousseau and the Politics of Ambiguity* (1996) belongs, by its own account, to a more ambivalent camp. Morgenstern resists the flatly condemnatory reading and emphasizes Rousseau's appreciation of women's mediating role between public and private. But she shares the structural premise: the woman is not accidental to Rousseau's polity, she is constitutive of it. Where Okin reads this as indictment, Morgenstern reads it as a resource for a more nuanced feminist appropriation.

## The separability / charitable reading

A minority tradition argues that Rousseau's democratic theory can be separated from his account of women and that the core of his political thought is salvageable for egalitarian projects. Judith Shklar's *Men and Citizens: A Study of Rousseau's Social Theory* (1969) predates the feminist debate and treats the family chiefly as one of Rousseau's two utopian models (alongside Sparta), not as the key to the general will. Shklar reads Rousseau as primarily concerned with the psychology of modern alienation; the domestic scenes of *Julie* function as therapy for a fractured modern self rather than as political blueprint. Shklar's reading has been taken up by later commentators who argue that Rousseau's democratic theory can be reconstructed without the domestic hierarchy (Cohen 2010; Neuhouser 2008).

Allan Bloom's introduction to his 1979 translation of *Emile* offers a second kind of defense. Bloom reads Rousseau as a diagnostician of the tensions modernity produces between the natural individual and the demanding citizen. On Bloom's reading, Sophie's education is not a normative prescription of women's subordination but a thought experiment about what the completion of Emile's education would require. Bloom's reading has been criticized as apologetic by feminist commentators (Okin 1982; Weiss 1993) and defended as philologically careful by Straussian readers. Its influence in American political theory has been considerable, although it has lost ground to the structural reading since the 1990s.

Rawlsian and deliberative-democratic appropriations constitute a third strand. Rawls himself cites Rousseau sparingly but treats the general will as an ancestor of the original position (Rawls, *Lectures on the History of Political Philosophy*, 2007). Joshua Cohen's *Rousseau: A Free Community of Equals* (2010) offers the most developed Rawlsian reconstruction: Cohen argues that the deep structure of Rousseau's account of freedom, equality, and the general will is defensible on contemporary liberal-democratic grounds without the sexist anthropology, which Cohen brackets as historical residue. Frederick Neuhouser's *Rousseau's Theodicy of Self-Love* (2008) makes a parallel reconstructive argument on the psychological side: the concept of *amour-propre* is, Neuhouser argues, a non-sexist resource for theorizing recognition and inequality. Both Cohen and Neuhouser acknowledge the sexism but argue that it is contingent to the core theory.

A related line of reconstructive work is represented by the contributors to Lynda Lange's edited volume *Feminist Interpretations of Jean-Jacques Rousseau* (2002). Several chapters (notably Elizabeth Wingrove, Lori J. Marso, Rebecca Kukla) argue that Rousseau's texts are internally divided and contain resources that feminists can appropriate against his explicit claims. Wingrove's earlier *Rousseau's Republican Romance* (2000) is an important entry here, reading the political and the erotic as co-constituting in a way that refuses the simple structural-versus-separable binary.

## Reconstruction attempts

Reconstructive feminists have asked whether the general will, the sovereignty of the people, or republican virtue can be prised apart from the domestic hierarchy Rousseau yokes them to. Answers range.

On the no side, Okin (1979) and Pateman (1988) argue that the concepts themselves carry gendered presuppositions that cannot be surgically removed. The general will requires a citizen whose particularity has been disciplined, and Rousseau's only account of how that discipline is produced runs through the family. Strip out *Emile*, and there is no mechanism left to generate the citizen.

On the yes side, Cohen (2010) and Neuhouser (2008) argue that Rousseau's argument for free and equal citizenship does not require his anthropology of sexual difference. Once one accepts Rawlsian reflective equilibrium as the standard, the question is whether Rousseau's normative core can be recovered by a method he did not use; Cohen argues it can. Wingrove (2000) and Marso (in Lange 2002) occupy a middle position: Rousseau's texts are ambivalent enough that they can be read against themselves, and the general will can be recovered as a resource rather than a fixed doctrine.

Penny Weiss's (1993) verdict is that reconstruction is possible only if one is willing to give up Rousseau's politics of community, because sexual differentiation was the mechanism by which he generated the mutual dependence that community requires. A Rousseau shorn of sexual hierarchy is, on Weiss's reading, a different political theorist. This is the strongest version of the structural reading: not that Rousseau was sexist, but that his sexism does the work his theory needs done.

## Institutional and intellectual inheritance

Rousseau's influence on modern institutions is wide and well documented. *Emile* is the foundational text of the progressive education tradition. Johann Heinrich Pestalozzi read Rousseau directly and named his son Jean-Jacques; Pestalozzi's holistic pedagogy of "head, heart, and hands" and his object-teaching method translated Rousseau's child-centered principles into practical schooling (Brühlmeier 2010; Soëtard 1994). Friedrich Froebel studied at Pestalozzi's Yverdon institute and carried the line into the kindergarten movement, centering play, self-activity, and the education of young children (Brosterman 1997; Wollons 2000). Maria Montessori drew on both Froebel and Rousseau in constructing her own method. Historians of education treat this chain as central to the genealogy of modern child-centered schooling (Darling 1994; Brehony 2001).

The question is whether the pedagogical inheritance also carries Rousseau's gender asymmetry. Jane Roland Martin's *Reclaiming a Conversation* (1985) argues that it does: the philosophical tradition of educational theory from Plato through Rousseau to Dewey has either excluded women or relegated them to a separate track, and progressive education inherits a conception of the "educated person" that was built on Emile's template and never seriously revised for Sophie's. Penny Weiss (1987, 1993) and Madeleine Grumet (*Bitter Milk*, 1988) extend this argument to early childhood education and teacher training, both of which have been shaped by the Rousseauian assumption that the mother is the natural first teacher. Feminist historians of education (Allen 2006; Taylor Allen 1988) have documented how Froebel's kindergarten movement both opened professional work to women and codified a maternalist theory of education that limited women's authority to the domestic and paradomestic spheres.

Rousseau's influence on republicanism and on the French Revolution is the second major institutional inheritance. Joan Landes (1988), Lynn Hunt (*The Family Romance of the French Revolution*, 1992), and Madelyn Gutwirth (*The Twilight of the Goddesses*, 1992) argue that the Jacobin exclusion of women from public life, the closure of women's political clubs in 1793, and the revolutionary ideal of the male citizen-soldier carried Rousseau's gender settlement into the architecture of modern republican citizenship. The public/private divide, the ideal of a transparent civic community, and the suspicion of salon sociability trace in large part to Rousseau. Pateman (1988) and Landes (1988) argue that the contemporary public sphere inherits these constraints.

Romantic nationalism is the third inheritance. Fermon (1997) argues that the Clarens model, with its sentimental household presided over by a self-disciplining wife, became the template for nineteenth-century nationalist imaginations of the *Heimat* and the motherland. George Mosse's *Nationalism and Sexuality* (1985) tracks the masculinist and domesticating features of European nationalism that Rousseau helped to articulate.

Direct-democratic theory is the fourth. Benjamin Barber's *Strong Democracy* (1984) and contemporary deliberative democrats draw on Rousseau for an account of participatory citizenship and the general will. Feminist democratic theorists (Young 1990, 2000; Phillips 1995) have argued that these appropriations must confront the gendered exclusions embedded in the Rousseauian model of unanimity, transparency, and civic virtue.

## Live debates

The scholarly consensus since Okin 1979 has been broadly structural: most serious readers concede that Rousseau's treatment of women is pervasive, considered, and connected to the rest of his system. What remains contested is whether the connections can be cut.

Three axes of debate are visible in recent work. First, the nature of Rousseau's gender construction: naturalistic (women really are as Rousseau describes them) or strategic (women are constructed this way because community requires it). Weiss (1993), Wingrove (2000), and Marso (in Lange 2002) have pushed the strategic reading; Okin (1979) and Schwartz (1984) were more inclined to the naturalistic reading. Second, the possibility of reconstruction: Cohen (2010) and Neuhouser (2008) argue that the democratic theory survives surgery; Pateman (1988), Weiss (1993), and Fermon (1997) argue it does not. Third, the interpretive status of ambivalence: Morgenstern (1996) and Wingrove (2000) emphasize Rousseau's internal contradictions as resources; Okin and Pateman emphasize them as symptoms.

Recent reappraisals include Joshua Cohen's *Rousseau: A Free Community of Equals* (2010), which is the most ambitious Rawlsian reconstruction; Ethan Putterman's *Rousseau, Law and the Sovereignty of the People* (2010), which sets the gender question aside in order to recover a technical account of sovereignty; and Helena Rosenblatt's *Rousseau and Geneva* (1997), which contextualizes the *Letter to d'Alembert* within Genevan republican politics in a way that complicates but does not displace the feminist reading. Lori Marso's *Unmanly Citizens* (1999) and *(Un)Manly Citizens* essays treat Rousseau alongside Germaine de Staël and argue that Rousseau's female contemporaries and near-contemporaries already saw and named the structural problem, so that the "feminist reading" is not a late imposition but continuous with eighteenth-century debate.

No contemporary defense of Rousseau in the political-theory literature disputes that his explicit statements on women are sexist by any plausible contemporary standard. The live question is whether those statements do essential theoretical work, and on that question the scholarship remains divided. The structural reading is dominant; the separability reading survives in Rawlsian and Straussian pockets and in reconstructive feminist work.
