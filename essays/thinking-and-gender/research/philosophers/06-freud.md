# Freud

Sigmund Freud (1856-1939) is among the most contested figures in the history of ideas about gender. No philosopher has been more central to twentieth-century accounts of the psyche, and few have been more thoroughly attacked for sexism. The interpretive literature is unusually intricate: feminists have declared Freud irredeemable; other feminists, working with the same texts, have made psychoanalysis the indispensable tool for thinking about patriarchy. What follows maps the scholarly debate rather than resolving it.

## Primary texts and what he actually said

Freud's account of sexual difference developed over thirty years and is scattered across several works. The *Three Essays on the Theory of Sexuality* (1905, with significant revisions through 1924) introduced the infantile sexual theories, the idea that libido is singular and originally "masculine," and the framework of development in which the girl is understood as initially indistinguishable from the boy. His paper "Some Psychical Consequences of the Anatomical Distinction Between the Sexes" (1925) first argued that the sight of male genitals produces in the girl the conviction that she has been castrated, triggering "penis envy" and a turn from mother to father. "Female Sexuality" (1931) extended the argument, focusing on the intensity and duration of the girl's pre-Oedipal attachment to her mother. The fullest statement is Lecture XXXIII, "Femininity," in the *New Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis* (1933), which assembles the mature view: female development is a more complicated, more belated, and less complete version of male development; the girl must accomplish a shift of erotogenic zone from clitoris to vagina and a shift of object from mother to father; penis envy persists as a structuring wish; and because castration is for the girl a fait accompli rather than a threat, the motive to resolve the Oedipus complex is weaker, yielding a less impartial superego. Freud concludes, notoriously, that women "show less sense of justice than men" and contribute less than men to "the interests of civilization" (Freud, 1933/1965).

Three phrases from this corpus recur in the reception. "Anatomy is destiny," taken from "The Dissolution of the Oedipus Complex" (1924) and reiterated in 1925, encapsulates the claim that genital difference drives psychic difference. The phrase "dark continent," drawn from *The Question of Lay Analysis* (1926), frames adult female sexuality as a region psychoanalysis has failed to map. The "weaker superego" passage in "Femininity" is the most cited sentence in the anti-Freud literature. Alongside these run the related concepts of the castration complex, the Electra situation (a term Freud himself resisted but which entered the vernacular), and the treatment of masochism as constitutively feminine in "The Economic Problem of Masochism" (1924).

*Civilization and Its Discontents* (1930) extends the gendered picture into social theory. Freud argues that the work of civilization is carried out by men, who sublimate libido into culture, while women represent the counter-interest of the family and come "into opposition to the trend of civilization and display their retarding and restraining influence." Women are, in this account, simultaneously the origin of love and the antagonists of its cultural elaboration.

Freud himself acknowledged incompleteness. He called femininity a "riddle" and repeatedly conceded that psychoanalysis could not "describe what a woman is" but only "how she comes into being." Whether this marks genuine epistemic humility or rhetorical insulation has been a major locus of dispute.

## Structural / inseparable readings

The most forceful early feminist response treated Freud's sexism not as an incidental prejudice but as constitutive. Simone de Beauvoir's *The Second Sex* (1949) devotes a chapter to the "psychoanalytic point of view," arguing that Freud imports the masculine as norm and then reads female existence as deficiency; the "lack" that organizes his account of femininity is not discovered in the psyche but imposed on it. Shulamith Firestone, in *The Dialectic of Sex* (1970), called Freudianism "the misguided feminism" and argued that Freud registered real power relations in the patriarchal family but encoded them as biology, producing an ideology that pathologizes women. Kate Millett's *Sexual Politics* (1970) took penis envy and female masochism as paradigm cases of the naturalization of patriarchy by a prestige discipline; Millett read the Freudian legacy as a reactionary intervention against first-wave feminism.

The most philosophically demanding structural critique came from Luce Irigaray. *Speculum of the Other Woman* (1974), especially the long essay "The Blind Spot of an Old Dream of Symmetry," subjects Freud's "Femininity" lecture to line-by-line close reading and argues that Freud's apparent discovery of sexual difference is in fact the effacement of difference: woman is representable in his system only as a defective man, a male minus (Irigaray, 1974/1985). Irigaray was expelled from the École Freudienne in the wake of the book, which is often cited as evidence of how structurally threatening her argument was to psychoanalytic institutions. The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy's entry on psychoanalytic feminism treats this tradition as the dominant continental reading well into the 1990s (Stone & Reynolds, 2023).

## The Juliet Mitchell reconstruction and its critics

Against the structural-critique consensus, Juliet Mitchell's *Psychoanalysis and Feminism: Freud, Reich, Laing and Women* (1974) argued that feminism had misread Freud by treating him prescriptively. Freud, Mitchell argued, did not endorse patriarchy; he described its unconscious reproduction in the formation of the sexed subject. Reading Freud through Jacques Lacan, Mitchell held that the Oedipus complex and castration were not biological processes but the subject's entry into a symbolic order of sexual difference, and that psychoanalysis therefore offered feminism its most powerful theory of how patriarchy reproduces itself in psychic life. To reject Freud as misogynist was, on her view, to leave the mechanism of female subordination untheorized (Mitchell, 1974).

The book was received as scandalous. Richard Wollheim, reviewing it sympathetically in the *New Left Review*, called it "highly intelligent and well-informed" but argued Mitchell had overestimated Freud's compatibility with feminism by reading him always through his critics (Wollheim, 1975). Firestone-aligned feminists accused her of apostasy. The Lacanian reception was mixed: Jacqueline Rose, who would later collaborate with Mitchell on the translation and editing of *Feminine Sexuality: Jacques Lacan and the école freudienne* (Mitchell & Rose, 1982), extended the argument in *Sexuality in the Field of Vision* (1986) by pressing the distinction between the anatomical and the symbolic still harder and reading Freud's case studies, especially Dora, as sites where sexual identity fails to close. The Mitchell-Rose position has since become one of two major poles in Anglophone feminist theory; the Stanford Encyclopedia identifies it as the "symbolic" reading set against the "anatomical" reading attributed to Firestone, Millett, and their successors (Stone & Reynolds, 2023).

Critics from within feminism have pressed Mitchell on two fronts. First, that even granting the descriptive reading, Freud's account of development treats the patriarchal outcome as normative: deviations appear as pathology, and the "normal" girl is the one who accepts her castration. Second, that the symbolic reading depends on a Lacanian apparatus which itself recapitulates phallocentrism by making the phallus the master signifier. Irigaray's and Judith Butler's responses both turn on this point.

## Reconstruction from within psychoanalysis (object-relations, Lacanian, relational)

A parallel reconstruction proceeded inside the psychoanalytic profession. Karen Horney's "The Flight from Womanhood" (1926) is the foundational document: Horney argued that the very concepts by which psychoanalysis described women were tied to the assumptions of masculine culture, that penis envy, where it existed, was a reaction to women's cultural subordination rather than its cause, and that Freud's theory underestimated male "womb envy" and the masculine need to depreciate female procreativity (Horney, 1926). "The Denial of the Vagina" (1933) pursued the argument that Freud's insistence on primary clitoral sexuality served a defensive function for male analysts. Clara Thompson extended Horney's culturalist line in the 1940s, and Melanie Klein, while remaining formally within the Freudian tradition, relocated the drama of development to the pre-Oedipal relation with the mother and thereby decentered the father's phallus.

Nancy Chodorow's *The Reproduction of Mothering* (1978) used object-relations theory, especially that of W.R.D. Fairbairn and D.W. Winnicott, to argue that the gender asymmetries Freud posited as intrapsychic universals were produced by a contingent social fact: that women mother. Because girls are mothered by someone of the same sex, their ego boundaries remain more permeable and their capacity for relation more elaborated; because boys must differentiate from the mother to become men, masculinity is constituted defensively. Chodorow's intervention treated Freudian concepts (identification, internalization, the unconscious) as tools while rejecting the biological reductionism; her book became the most influential psychoanalytic feminist text in American sociology (Chodorow, 1978).

Jessica Benjamin's *The Bonds of Love: Psychoanalysis, Feminism, and the Problem of Domination* (1988) pushed further. Drawing on Hegel's dialectic of recognition and on Winnicottian object relations, Benjamin argued that the Freudian Oedipal story presupposed a monadic subject and missed the intersubjective structure of desire. Domination, in her account, was not a consequence of an ineliminable death drive but of the failure of mutual recognition, and feminine masochism was a pathology of this failure rather than its natural expression (Benjamin, 1988). *Like Subjects, Love Objects* (1995) extended the project toward a theory of gender as overinclusive rather than mutually exclusive identification. The broader "relational" school (Stephen Mitchell, Lewis Aron, Adrienne Harris) has since become a dominant current in American psychoanalysis and carries the feminist revision as its default theoretical commitment.

In continental theory, Julia Kristeva, Jacqueline Rose, and Shoshana Felman pursued a Lacanian feminism that kept the symbolic frame while insisting on the instability of sexual identity, the return of the maternal in the "semiotic" (Kristeva), and the disruptive force of female speech in the analytic scene (Felman, 1993). Judith Butler's *Gender Trouble* (1990) engages Freud's "Mourning and Melancholia" and "The Ego and the Id" to argue that gender identity is formed through a foreclosed, ungrievable attachment to the same-sex parent under conditions of compulsory heterosexuality; heterosexual identity thus emerges as a species of melancholia (Butler, 1990, 1995). Leo Bersani's *The Freudian Body* (1986) and Teresa de Lauretis's *The Practice of Love* (1994) offer further queer-theoretic appropriations that treat Freud's destabilizations of object-choice and aim as resources rather than liabilities.

## Empirical evaluation in contemporary psychology

The specific empirical claims most associated with Freud's account of sexual difference have fared poorly. There is no replicated evidence for penis envy as a universal feature of female development, for the Electra complex as a stage, for the shift from clitoral to vaginal orgasm as a maturational achievement, or for female masochism as a normative disposition. Scientific psychology's turn to experimental and statistical methods through the latter half of the twentieth century largely bypassed psychoanalysis; the work of Adolf Grünbaum (*The Foundations of Psychoanalysis*, 1984) and Frederick Crews (*The Memory Wars*, 1995) argued that psychoanalysis failed basic criteria of testability and that its therapeutic claims were overstated. Infant observation studies in the Bowlby-Ainsworth attachment tradition have generally supported relational accounts of development while undercutting Freud's drive-theoretic framework.

Yet psychoanalysis has not disappeared from clinical practice. Meta-analyses of psychodynamic psychotherapy (Shedler, 2010; Leichsenring & Rabung, 2008) report outcomes comparable to those of cognitive-behavioral therapy for many conditions, and core psychoanalytic constructs (unconscious processing, transference, defense) have been partially operationalized and supported in contemporary cognitive and social psychology (Westen, 1998). The consensus position among historians and philosophers of the field is that Freud's specific theories of female development have been discredited while a more general psychodynamic framework, heavily revised, retains empirical and clinical viability. Scholars reconcile the tension by distinguishing Freud the theorist of sexual difference from Freud the theorist of the dynamic unconscious; the first is largely rejected, the second partially preserved.

## Institutional and intellectual inheritance

The historical question of Freud's influence is distinct from the question of whether his theories are true. Ellen Herman's *The Romance of American Psychology: Political Culture in the Age of Experts* (1995) traces how psychological expertise, with a strongly psychoanalytic inflection, became central to postwar American government, military, and popular culture; the book documents the diffusion of Freudian vocabulary into domains far removed from the clinic (Herman, 1995). Nathan Hale's two-volume *Freud and the Americans* (1971, 1995) provides the longer institutional narrative, showing how the American psychoanalytic establishment enforced medical-only training and thereby gave Freudian theory unusual prestige and unusual distance from academic psychology.

The gendered costs of that diffusion have been extensively documented. Hysteria, Freud's originating diagnostic category, functioned through the first half of the twentieth century as a catchall for female distress; Elaine Showalter's *The Female Malady* (1985) and Mark Micale's *Approaching Hysteria* (1995) trace its career. The "refrigerator mother" theory of autism and schizophrenia, associated with Leo Kanner and Bruno Bettelheim and drawing loosely on psychoanalytic frameworks, blamed mothers for their children's developmental disorders and was not fully displaced until the 1980s. The DSM's first two editions (1952, 1968) were heavily psychoanalytic in orientation and encoded categories (including frigidity and various "neurotic" diagnoses disproportionately applied to women) that subsequent editions removed. Jonathan Metzl's *Prozac on the Couch* (2003) and *The Protest Psychosis* (2009) have documented how psychiatric categories have carried gendered and racialized assumptions across the shift from psychoanalytic to psychopharmacological paradigms. Ruth Leys's *Trauma: A Genealogy* (2000) traces how the psychoanalytic concept of trauma was reworked and carried into late-twentieth-century clinical and cultural discourse, with complicated consequences for the politics of victimhood.

Beyond the clinic, Freudian motifs saturated mid-century advertising (through Edward Bernays and the Ernest Dichter school of motivation research), literary criticism (from Lionel Trilling to the psychoanalytic Shakespeareans), film theory (Laura Mulvey's "Visual Pleasure and Narrative Cinema," 1975, built on Lacanian-Freudian premises), and the everyday language of the unconscious, repression, and projection. The "pop Freud" of common parlance is in many respects more durable than the technical apparatus.

## Live debates

The field has not converged. The Mitchell-Rose line remains influential in cultural theory, gender studies, and relational psychoanalysis; it treats Freud as indispensable, properly read. The Irigaray-Butler line treats Freud as both indispensable and irretrievably compromised, to be worked through critically rather than either accepted or discarded. The Firestone-Millett line, updated in more recent feminist philosophy of science (for example, Naomi Weisstein's classic "Psychology Constructs the Female" and contemporary work by Cordelia Fine and others), treats psychoanalysis as an obstacle that empirical psychology and feminist theory are better off without. Within clinical psychology the question is largely settled against Freud's specific gender theory but remains open with respect to the broader psychodynamic framework.

For an essay on gendered modes of thought, Freud presents the clearest possible case of the author's problem. His empirical claims about women are largely wrong; his framework nonetheless generated some of the most sophisticated feminist theory of the twentieth century; his sexism is not incidental to his system but is also not obviously separable from the parts of that system that have proven generative. The question whether Freud is recoverable for feminism has been answered yes, no, and "only by going through him" by careful readers working in good faith. The scholarly consensus, to the extent that one exists, is that the terms of the debate (prescriptive vs. descriptive, anatomical vs. symbolic, structural vs. contingent) are themselves the yield of the encounter, and that no responsible engagement with gender theory in the twentieth century can avoid them.
