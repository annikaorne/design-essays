# Proudhon

Pierre-Joseph Proudhon (1809-1865) is the only canonical nineteenth-century European socialist whose anti-feminism is so explicit, sustained, and central to his published work that it cannot plausibly be treated as incidental. He coined the slogan "property is theft," authored the first sustained theory of mutualist anarchism, gave the federalist tradition its modern shape, and was the most influential French socialist of his generation. He also wrote, late in life, a book-length attack on women's emancipation entitled *La Pornocratie*, and embedded a theory of female inferiority in the same work, *De la justice dans la Révolution et dans l'Église* (1858), in which he set out his mature ethical philosophy. The scholarly literature, in both French and English, has therefore been forced to confront a more pointed version of the question that troubles other figures in this series: not whether Proudhon's gender views can be quietly bracketed, but whether the social theory itself can be reconstructed without them.

This review surveys what Proudhon actually wrote about women; the structural and separability readings in the secondary literature; the contemporary opposition led by Jenny d'Héricourt and Juliette Adam; and the institutional inheritance through Bakunin, Marx, the First International, and twentieth-century anarchist and syndicalist traditions.

## Primary texts and what he actually said

Proudhon's writings on women are unusually unambiguous. Four bodies of text matter.

First, *Système des contradictions économiques, ou Philosophie de la misère* (1846). The famous formula appears here: *"Courtisane ou ménagère (ménagère, dis-je, et non pas servante), je n'y vois pas de milieu"* — "Courtesan or housekeeper (housekeeper, I say, and not servant), I see no middle ground." The line, embedded in his discussion of property and the family, fixes the binary that runs through all his later work: woman is constitutively either a wife within a patriarchal household or a sexually disordered figure outside it. Hal Draper, in his survey of nineteenth-century socialist anti-feminism, treats this passage as the inaugural moment of what he calls Proudhon's "phobic anti-feminism" (Draper and Lipow, 1976).

Second, *De la justice dans la Révolution et dans l'Église* (1858), Proudhon's longest single work and his attempt at a comprehensive moral philosophy. Volumes 3 and 4 contain the "Amour et mariage" study in which he derives the inferiority of women from a calculation of the relative "physical, intellectual, and moral force" of the sexes, arriving at the now-notorious ratio of roughly 3:2 in favour of men across all three dimensions. He argues that the conjugal couple is the elementary unit of justice, that the husband's authority is necessary for the moral integrity of the family, and that women's exclusion from public life follows from their nature rather than from circumstance. K. Steven Vincent (1984) and Edward Castleton (in his recent editorial work on the unpublished Proudhon manuscripts) both treat the *Justice* as the architectural centre of Proudhon's mature thought, which makes the location of these claims significant: they sit not at the periphery but at the foundation.

Third, *La Pornocratie ou les femmes dans les temps modernes*, written around 1858-1860 in response to d'Héricourt and others, never published in his lifetime, and brought out posthumously by his literary executors in 1875. The work is what Linda Nochlin called "the most consistent anti-feminist tract of its time, or perhaps any other." Proudhon argues that the visibility of women in modern public life amounts to a "pornocracy" — rule by courtesans — and includes the line that women do not entirely hate being treated with violence. The text is short, polemical, and uneven, but it is unambiguously his, and twentieth-century scholars (Daniel Guérin, Castleton, Shawn Wilbur) treat it as the logical extension of the *Justice*, not as an aberration.

Fourth, the silence of *Qu'est-ce que la propriété?* (1840). Proudhon's foundational text says almost nothing about women, and what it does say assumes the patriarchal household as the natural basis of possession and use-rights. Vincent (1984) and Iain McKay (in his 2011 anthology *Property is Theft!*) read this silence as itself revealing: the early mutualism is built on the workshop and the household, both already gendered, without that fact being made into an object of analysis.

The correspondence with his wife, Euphrasie Piégard (1822-1900), whom he married in 1849, is more ambiguous. Recent biographical scholarship by French historians on Piégard (Chaïbi, 2023) emphasises that Euphrasie was barely literate, that the marriage matched Proudhon's stated ideal of a "working girl, simple, gracious, naïve, devoted to work and her duties," and that the surviving letters show genuine affection alongside an explicitly hierarchical conception of marital roles. The letters do not contradict the published anti-feminism; they enact the model it prescribes.

## The structural reading

The dominant reading in serious scholarship over the past forty years is structural: Proudhon's misogyny is not a personal prejudice grafted onto an otherwise emancipatory theory but a constitutive element of the theory itself.

K. Steven Vincent's *Pierre-Joseph Proudhon and the Rise of French Republican Socialism* (Oxford, 1984) is the standard English-language scholarly biography. Vincent argues that Proudhon's mutualism rests on two productive units — the workshop and the family — and that federalism builds upward from these. Because the family in Proudhon's account is patriarchal by design, with the wife restricted to the household and the husband functioning as both producer and political subject, the gender hierarchy is load-bearing rather than decorative. Strip out the patriarchal family and the chain from individual producer to commune to federation loses one of its two anchor points.

Aaron Noland's mid-century essays in the *Journal of the History of Ideas* and the *American Journal of Economics and Sociology* (Noland, 1967a, 1967b, 1970) had already pressed a version of this argument. Noland reads Proudhon as a moralist whose central category is *justice* understood as balance, and shows that the justification for male authority in the family is structurally identical to the justification for the federalist principle of balanced and limited authority elsewhere: in both cases Proudhon claims to be reading a natural order off the world. If the gender claim is wrong, Noland implies, the larger method is in trouble.

Edward Castleton, the principal contemporary editor of Proudhon's unpublished manuscripts at the Maison des Sciences de l'Homme in Besançon, has emphasised in his recent work the depth and consistency of Proudhon's gender views across published and unpublished texts. Castleton's editorial scholarship makes it harder to read *La Pornocratie* as a late aberration: similar formulations recur in notebooks from across his career.

In the Marxist critical tradition, Hal Draper (Draper and Lipow, 1976) made the strongest version of the structural case: that Proudhon's anti-feminism is "phobic" rather than merely conventional and that it shapes his account of property, the wage, and the workshop from *Qu'est-ce que la propriété?* forward. Joan Landes, in *Women and the Public Sphere in the Age of the French Revolution* (Cornell, 1988), provides the broader context: French republican and socialist thought in the post-revolutionary decades systematically reconstructed the public sphere as masculine, and Proudhon represents the most explicit theorisation of that exclusion within the socialist tradition.

## The separability or charitable reading

A weaker tradition, mostly older and mostly Anglophone, treats the misogyny as a serious flaw but ultimately separable from the political and economic theory.

George Woodcock's *Pierre-Joseph Proudhon: A Biography* (Macmillan, 1956) is the foundational English-language treatment. Woodcock is broadly sympathetic to Proudhon's federalism and mutualism and treats him as a major figure unfairly eclipsed by Marx. He calls the misogyny out by name — he describes the views in *La Pornocratie* as indefensible — but his interpretive stance is that these are personal failings rooted in Proudhon's peasant background and temperament, not theoretical commitments. Critics have noted that Woodcock's own remarks about women's intellectual capacities in the same biography reproduce some of what he is criticising (see the libcom commentary on Woodcock, n.d.).

Robert L. Hoffman's *Revolutionary Justice: The Social and Political Theory of P.-J. Proudhon* (Illinois, 1972) takes a related line. Hoffman treats the *Justice* as Proudhon's most important work but reads its gender chapters as an embarrassing application of an otherwise valuable ethical framework. The implicit claim is that the framework — justice as balance, mutual respect among equals, federalist limits on authority — could in principle be re-derived without the patriarchal premises Proudhon happened to attach to it.

In the contemporary anarchist scene, Iain McKay (editor of *An Anarchist FAQ* and the Proudhon anthology *Property is Theft!*, AK Press, 2011) and Shawn Wilbur (curator of the Libertarian Labyrinth digital archive and translator of much of *Justice*) represent the most serious effort to rescue Proudhon for present-day use. Both acknowledge the misogyny in unsparing terms — Wilbur in particular has translated and posted the contemporary feminist responses to Proudhon alongside his work — and both argue that the mutualist framework can be reconstructed without the gender theory. Wilbur's posts on "Proudhon, women, and the 'organ of justice'" explicitly stage the tension: the same Proudhon who claims to derive justice from the experience of mutual recognition fails to extend that recognition to half of humanity, and the question is whether that failure is a contradiction internal to his theory (in which case the theory contains its own corrective) or a structural feature (in which case it does not).

The honest version of the separability argument, then, is not that Proudhon's misogyny is minor but that the rest of the theory contains resources to overturn it. Whether that is true is the live question.

## Jenny d'Héricourt and contemporary opposition

The most striking feature of the Proudhon case is that the structural critique was made in his lifetime, by a woman he debated directly, and that he refused to answer her on the explicit ground of her sex.

Jenny P. d'Héricourt (1809-1875) — physician-midwife, Saint-Simonian, Fourierist, and 1848 republican — exchanged a series of articles with Proudhon in the *Revue Philosophique et Religieuse* in 1856-1857. She then published *La Femme affranchie: réponse à MM. Michelet, Proudhon, É. de Girardin, A. Comte et aux autres novateurs modernes* (Brussels, 1860; banned in France; English translation as *A Woman's Philosophy of Woman*, New York, 1864). D'Héricourt's argument has three layers. First, an empirical refutation: she draws on contemporary anatomy and phrenology to argue that there is no physiological basis for the claim that women are intellectually inferior. Second, a logical refutation: she shows that Proudhon's own principles — the equal dignity of producers, justice as mutual recognition, federation as the political form of equality — are flatly inconsistent with his gender claims. The famous formulation is hers: "you contradict your own principles." Third, a political argument: women's exclusion from civic life is the cause of the moral and political weaknesses Proudhon attributes to them.

Proudhon's response, captured in his correspondence and recapitulated in *La Pornocratie*, was to refuse further debate on the ground that d'Héricourt's intellectual posture itself demonstrated her natural inferiority. The exchange is, as Karen Offen put it in her rediscovery essay (Offen, "A Nineteenth-Century French Feminist Rediscovered: Jenny P. d'Héricourt, 1809-1875," *Signs*, 1987), one of the cleanest cases on record of a male thinker confronted in real time with the contradiction his critic identifies and choosing to entrench rather than respond.

Juliette Lamber (later Adam) published *Idées anti-proudhoniennes sur l'amour, la femme et le mariage* (1858) at the age of twenty-two, a year after Proudhon's *Justice* appeared. Adam, like d'Héricourt, accepts much of Proudhon's economic and political critique while rejecting his account of sexual difference; her argument is that masculine and feminine qualities are real but not bound to biological sex, and that political equality follows from the requirement of moral autonomy. Recent French scholarship (Krier, 2019, on Adam's feminism in the *Revue d'histoire de la pensée économique*) has restored Adam's intervention to view as an important part of mid-century French feminist political economy.

The broader scholarship on French nineteenth-century feminism (Offen, *European Feminisms*, 2000; Felicia Gordon and Máire Cross, *Early French Feminisms*, 1996; Caroline Arni's work on conjugal contract debates) reads the d'Héricourt-Adam-Proudhon exchange as a defining moment for the French tradition: it is the point at which republican-socialist and feminist projects, briefly aligned in 1848, decisively split.

## Institutional and intellectual inheritance

Proudhon's gender theory did not die with him. Three lines of inheritance matter.

First, the First International. Proudhon's followers — the French and Belgian mutualists — dominated the early International Workingmen's Association and successfully passed resolutions opposing women's wage labour outside the home, on explicitly Proudhonian grounds. This was the substantive issue (alongside the Bakuninist-Marxist split over centralism) on which the International divided. Marx's *La Misère de la philosophie* (1847), written in direct response to Proudhon's *Système des contradictions économiques*, attacks the economics rather than the gender theory, but Marx's later interventions in the International on women's labour are pitched explicitly against the Proudhonian position.

Second, Bakunin. Mikhail Bakunin admired Proudhon and acknowledged his federalism as foundational, but explicitly rejected the gender theory. Bakunin advocated "complete equality between men and women," opposed the patriarchal juridical family, and called out the contradiction in male revolutionaries who fought for liberty in public and tyrannised at home. The split is significant: it shows that within nineteenth-century anarchism itself, the structural and separability readings had partisans, with Bakunin in effect arguing that Proudhonian federalism could and should be detached from its patriarchal foundation.

Third, the syndicalist and mutualist labour traditions of the late nineteenth and early twentieth century — particularly in France — inherited a gendered conception of the worker as male breadwinner that has been traced by labour historians (Joan Wallach Scott, *Gender and the Politics of History*, 1988) directly to the Proudhonian formation of the French left. The recurring critique that left-wing movements have reproduced sexism internally — what is sometimes called the "patriarchal left" — has, in the French case, an unusually clear textual genealogy.

Joan Landes's broader argument in *Women and the Public Sphere* connects this back to the structure of post-revolutionary republicanism: the public sphere was constituted as masculine through a series of intellectual moves of which Proudhon's is among the most explicit. The exclusion of women from civic life was not an oversight to be corrected but a constitutive condition that the republican-socialist tradition built into its foundations and that subsequent generations have had to dismantle from within.

## Live debates

Three questions remain live in the secondary literature.

First, is the structural reading correct? The strongest version (Vincent, Castleton, Draper, Landes) holds that Proudhon's mutualism cannot be reconstructed without its patriarchal foundation; the weaker version (Hoffman, McKay, Wilbur) holds that the framework contains internal resources for its own correction, as d'Héricourt herself argued. The dispute is not about what Proudhon believed — that is settled — but about how tightly his beliefs are integrated.

Second, what is the status of d'Héricourt's intervention? Recent scholarship (Offen, Arni, French historians associated with the *Clio* journal) treats it as a major work of nineteenth-century feminist political theory rather than a footnote to Proudhon. The recovery is ongoing and the English-language literature lags the French.

Third, how should the contemporary anarchist tradition handle Proudhon? The dominant move in recent decades — visible in McKay, Wilbur, and the broader anarcha-feminist literature — has been to publish the misogyny in full, alongside the contemporary feminist responses, rather than to bracket or apologise for it. Whether this constitutes a sustainable rescue or a delayed admission that the theory needs to be rebuilt from the ground up is itself contested.

What is not contested, in any serious recent treatment, is that Proudhon's anti-feminism is real, central, and unusually well documented in his own hand. The interpretive question is what follows from that fact.
