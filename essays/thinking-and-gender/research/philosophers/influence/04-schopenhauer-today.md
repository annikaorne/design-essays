# Schopenhauer today

Arthur Schopenhauer's contemporary influence is unusual in shape. He is not, like Kant, a foundation for liberal political institutions, and he is not, like Hegel, a reference point for constitutional theory. His inheritance is instead dispersed across four uneven domains: a steady but relatively small academic Schopenhauer industry, a thin legal and institutional footprint, a significant clinical and ethical influence through psychoanalysis and antinatalism, and a surprisingly large pop-cultural presence that runs from cosmic horror fiction to online misogyny. This review surveys all four and flags, at each turn, how the "On Women" essay and the "Metaphysics of Sexual Love" chapter travel alongside the rest of the inheritance. It is a companion to the existing file on Schopenhauer's sexism and structural misogyny (`../04-schopenhauer.md`); the focus here is on what contemporary readers do with Schopenhauer, and with what distribution of engagement.

## Academic and intellectual

Anglophone Schopenhauer scholarship is organized, as of the mid-2020s, around a recognizable group of specialists. Christopher Janaway's *Self and World in Schopenhauer's Philosophy* (1989) and *Schopenhauer: A Very Short Introduction* (1994) remain the standard entry points, and Janaway is general editor of the Cambridge Edition of Schopenhauer's works (Norman, Welchman, and Janaway, trans., *The World as Will and Representation*, vols. 1 and 2, 2010 and 2018; Del Caro and Janaway, trans., *Parerga and Paralipomena*, vol. 2, 2015), which has effectively replaced the Payne translations as the scholarly reference. Julian Young's *Schopenhauer* (2005) is the fullest single-volume philosophical study in English. Robert Wicks's *Schopenhauer* (Blackwell, 2008) and his *Stanford Encyclopedia* entry (Wicks 2021) function as reference pieces. Dale Jacquette edited *Schopenhauer, Philosophy, and the Arts* (1996) and wrote *The Philosophy of Schopenhauer* (2005). Bernard Reginster's *The Affirmation of Life: Nietzsche on Overcoming Nihilism* (Harvard, 2006), though nominally a Nietzsche book, is one of the most influential reconstructions of the Schopenhauerian pessimism Nietzsche was responding to, and is regularly cited in Schopenhauer journals. David E. Cartwright's *Schopenhauer: A Biography* (Cambridge, 2010) and *Historical Dictionary of Schopenhauer's Philosophy* (2005) are the standard biographical and reference works.

Three live research programs sit inside this academic infrastructure. The first is comparative philosophy. Schopenhauer read the Latin *Oupnek'hat* translation of the Upanishads in 1814 and cited "Indian wisdom" throughout his career, which makes him the nineteenth-century European philosopher most often paired with South Asian thought. Peter Abelsen's "Schopenhauer and Buddhism" (*Philosophy East and West* 43, 1993) and Urs App's *Schopenhauer's Compass* (2014) are widely cited reconstructions; Stephen Cross's *Schopenhauer's Encounter with Indian Thought* (Hawaii, 2013) treats the Upanishadic engagement specifically. The Schopenhauer-Buddhism pairing has become a standing reference point in contemporary comparative philosophy of mind, with Janaway himself contributing to the conversation in essays on suffering and the denial of the will.

The second program is the ethics of compassion. Cartwright's biography and his earlier papers on *Mitleid* (compassion) have reframed Schopenhauer's *On the Basis of Morality* (1840) as a serious rival to Kantian and utilitarian ethics rather than as a curiosity. Sandra Shapshay's *Reconstructing Schopenhauer's Ethics* (Oxford, 2019) offers the most ambitious recent reconstruction, defending what she calls a "compassionate moral realism" and explicitly arguing that Schopenhauer's ethical core can be extended to cases he himself refused to extend it to. Shapshay's book is notable because it confronts the tension Warnock flagged (Warnock 1996): the same philosopher who grounded morality in compassion for all sentient beings also wrote that women lack a proper sense of justice. Shapshay treats this as an internal failure to apply the ethic, not as a reason to abandon it.

The third program is aesthetics, particularly music and the sublime. Schopenhauer's claim that music is the direct expression of the Will, not a representation of it, remains the most-cited passage of *World as Will and Representation* in musicology. Lydia Goehr's *The Quest for Voice: Music, Politics, and the Limits of Philosophy* (Oxford, 1998) and several chapters in Jacquette (ed.) 1996 are the anchor references. Bryan Magee's *Aspects of Wagner* (1968; rev. 1988) and *Wagner and Philosophy* (2000) extend the aesthetic conversation into opera studies; the Schopenhauer-Wagner link is treated at book length in Mark Berry's *Treacherous Bonds and Laughing Fire* (2006) and in Julian Young's *The Philosophies of Richard Wagner* (Lexington, 2014).

Feminist engagement, as noted in the companion file, is comparatively thin. There is no *Feminist Interpretations of Schopenhauer* volume in Penn State's Re-Reading the Canon series, despite volumes on Kant, Hegel, Nietzsche, Plato, and Aristotle. Patricia Matthews's work in Gjesdal and Nassar (eds.), *The Oxford Handbook of Nineteenth-Century Women Philosophers in the German Tradition* (Oxford, 2024), is the most current corrective.

## Legal and institutional

The direct legal footprint is small. Schopenhauer did not write constitutional theory, and his political remarks are scattered and reactionary. One area where his ethics has had measurable institutional uptake is animal ethics. *On the Basis of Morality* extends compassion to nonhuman animals and is one of the earliest sustained European arguments for the moral status of animals; contemporary animal ethicists cite him. Mark Rowlands engages Schopenhauer in *Animal Rights: Moral Theory and Practice* (2nd ed., Palgrave, 2009), and David DeGrazia references him in *Taking Animals Seriously* (Cambridge, 1996) and in his subsequent handbook entries. Schopenhauer's influence here is background rather than foundational (Singer, Regan, and Korsgaard carry more weight in the field), but he is part of the genealogy that contemporary animal ethics cites when tracing its pre-utilitarian roots.

A related but smaller current runs through environmental ethics, where Schopenhauerian pessimism is sometimes invoked as a counterweight to progress narratives. This literature is scattered and has not cohered into a named research program.

## Clinical and medical

The clinical inheritance is substantial and runs through two channels. The first is psychoanalysis. Freud claimed to have read little Schopenhauer directly, but the conceptual overlaps are extensive enough that Christopher Young and Andrew Brook's "Schopenhauer and Freud" (*International Journal of Psychoanalysis* 75, 1994) and Marcel Zentner's *Die Flucht ins Vergessen: Die Anfänge der Psychoanalyse Freuds bei Schopenhauer* (1995) are now standard references for the pre-history of psychoanalytic theory. The Will as blind drive, the role of repression and sublimation, the structure of sexual motivation as species-operation rather than individual choice, and the clinical emphasis on unconscious determination all have Schopenhauerian antecedents. Contemporary histories of psychoanalysis (George Makari's *Revolution in Mind*, 2008; Paul-Laurent Assoun's earlier French work) treat the connection as settled.

The second channel is philosophical antinatalism and the ethics of suffering. David Benatar's *Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming Into Existence* (Oxford, 2006) is the most cited contemporary work, and Benatar explicitly positions his asymmetry argument as a formalization of Schopenhauerian pessimism about the balance of pleasure and suffering in a human life. Benatar's follow-up, *The Human Predicament* (Oxford, 2017), extends the argument into questions about meaning and death. Frederick Beiser's *Weltschmerz: Pessimism in German Philosophy, 1860 to 1900* (Oxford, 2016) is the authoritative historical study of the pessimist tradition Benatar is drawing on. In bioethics, Schopenhauerian pessimism about the value of continued existence occasionally surfaces in end-of-life and assisted-dying debates, though usually at one remove; the direct citations are to Benatar rather than to Schopenhauer.

Shapshay (2019) is also relevant here, because her reconstruction of Schopenhauer's compassion ethic has been picked up in medical-ethics conversations about suffering, palliative care, and the moral weight of negative welfare.

## Pop culture and political rhetoric

The largest contemporary audience for Schopenhauer is probably not academic. Three currents deserve naming.

The first is the pessimist revival in literary and screen culture. Eugene Thacker's *In the Dust of This Planet: Horror of Philosophy, Vol. 1* (Zero Books, 2011) popularized a cosmic-pessimist reading that draws heavily on Schopenhauer and became widely cited after Nic Pizzolatto borrowed its vocabulary for the first season of *True Detective* (HBO, 2014). Thomas Ligotti's *The Conspiracy Against the Human Race: A Contrivance of Horror* (Hippocampus, 2010) is the other touchstone; Ligotti cites Schopenhauer and Zapffe directly and was the acknowledged source for Pizzolatto's Rust Cohle monologues. These works have carried Schopenhauerian pessimism into a broad readership that has little contact with academic philosophy, and they have seeded a "philosophical pessimism" subculture online, often organized around small presses and forums.

The second is the Wagner inheritance. Bryan Magee's *Aspects of Wagner* (1968) and *Wagner and Philosophy* (2000), Berry (2006), and Young's *The Philosophies of Richard Wagner* (Lexington, 2014) continue to frame Wagner reception around a Schopenhauerian metaphysics. This matters culturally because Wagner's gender politics, the woman as erotic lure and as the site of the night-world of Will, reaches a mass audience through opera, film scoring, and the long afterlife of *Tristan*, and that coding is inherited from Schopenhauer.

The third is the manosphere. "On Women" ("Über die Weiber") has had a second life in red-pill, incel, and manosphere contexts, where it circulates as a short, quotable, "based" historical text that ostensibly legitimates contemporary misogyny through philosophical pedigree. Donna Zuckerberg's *Not All Dead White Men: Classics and Misogyny in the Digital Age* (Harvard, 2018) documents the broader pattern of classical and post-classical philosophy being strip-mined in this way; Angela Nagle's *Kill All Normies* (Zero Books, 2017) maps the online subculture; and Debbie Ging's "Alphas, Betas, and Incels" (*Men and Masculinities* 22, 2019) traces the rhetorical strategies. Dilek Cakmak's work on Schopenhauer's reception in contemporary antifeminist discourse (e.g., in edited volumes on digital misogyny) is also relevant. What the manosphere takes from Schopenhauer is not the metaphysics or the ethics of compassion but precisely the short essay that Magee and Young most want to quarantine.

## Where gender travels with the inheritance

The distribution of gendered uptake across the four domains is uneven, and the pattern is diagnostic. In the academic domain, the gender material is treated either as a historical embarrassment (Magee, Janaway, Cartwright) or as structurally revealing (Kofman, Higgins, Matthews), but it is not typically read as the core of the inheritance. In the legal-institutional domain, gender barely surfaces because the inheritance itself is small. In the clinical domain, the gender coding travels forward intact through Freud's theory of female sexuality (Young and Brook 1994; Zentner 1995) and, less directly, through the antinatalist framing of reproduction as harm, which rests on a Schopenhauerian picture of sexual love as species-manipulation that his own text genders asymmetrically.

It is in the pop-cultural domain that the gender inheritance is most visible and most consequential. Three observations are worth flagging. First, the manosphere's rediscovery of "On Women" is a case of reception rather than endorsement: Schopenhauer did not design his work for this audience, but the short, portable, quotable form of the essay made it unusually well-suited to meme-era circulation. Second, Wagner's gender politics reaches mass audiences in a form that preserves the Schopenhauerian coding of the feminine as seductive surface and metaphysical lure; the Magee, Berry, and Young literature treats this as a live interpretive question in contemporary opera staging. Third, philosophical pessimism as a subculture, from Thacker and Ligotti outward, is male-coded in its authorship, readership, and online communities in a way that is empirically documented (Ligotti's readership is tracked in small-press interviews; the pessimist forums are men-dominated) but has not been subjected to sustained feminist analysis. There is no feminist-pessimist counter-tradition of comparable visibility; what exists (work by Eugenie Brinkema, Robin James, and parts of the afro-pessimist conversation) is adjacent rather than continuous.

The companion file argues that Schopenhauer's feminist reception is thinner than Kant's or Hegel's because the incentive to rehabilitate him is lower. The contemporary-influence picture reinforces that: the audiences that most actively consume Schopenhauer today, opera-goers, pessimist readers, antinatalists, and manosphere participants, are not audiences with strong internal pressure to re-examine the gender material. The academic specialists who could do that work are a small community, and, as Matthews and Shapshay both note, the work is only recently getting underway.
