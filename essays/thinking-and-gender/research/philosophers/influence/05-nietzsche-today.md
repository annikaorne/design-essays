# Nietzsche today

Of the nineteenth-century philosophers treated in this essay, Nietzsche has by far the largest and most unruly contemporary footprint. He is the most-cited philosopher of the twentieth and twenty-first centuries across humanities databases (Leiter 2015, 1 to 3), the organizing figure for continental philosophy after Heidegger, and simultaneously the most quoted philosopher in self-help, manosphere, and far-right online discourse. This review maps the inheritance across four domains and flags where gender travels with it.

## Academic and intellectual

Continental philosophy after 1960 is substantially a set of readings of Nietzsche. Gilles Deleuze's *Nietzsche and Philosophy* (1962) is the pivot. Deleuze rewrites the will to power as a typology of active and reactive forces and reclaims Nietzsche from the Heideggerian reading as a "metaphysician of presence"; the book is credited with single-handedly reversing Nietzsche's postwar reputation in France and making the French reception possible (Schrift 1995, chapter 3). Michel Foucault's method is explicitly Nietzschean. "Nietzsche, Genealogy, History" (1971) formalizes the procedure that organizes *Discipline and Punish* (1975) and *The History of Sexuality* (1976 onward): not a search for origins but a tracing of descent and emergence, of the contingent power-laden conditions under which a practice, a concept, or a subject comes to appear necessary. Jacques Derrida's *Spurs* (*Eperons*, 1978) uses the figure of woman in Nietzsche to argue for the undecidability of the sign, and the book is central both to deconstruction and to the "woman as truth" reading treated in the companion file.

The analytic reception is the other main scholarly current. Brian Leiter's *Nietzsche on Morality* (2002; revised 2015) is the standard reference for analytically trained readers. Leiter reconstructs Nietzsche's critique of morality as a naturalistic research program: moralities are type-facts about the psycho-physical constitutions of those who hold them, and the task of philosophy is to identify which morality suits which type. Maudemarie Clark's *Nietzsche on Truth and Philosophy* (1990) set the terms for the analytic debate on perspectivism by arguing that the mature Nietzsche abandons the early "falsification" thesis and holds a defensible, non-self-undermining view. Alexander Nehamas's *Nietzsche: Life as Literature* (1985) reads Nietzsche as constructing his own life as a literary work and is the most cited single monograph in the field. John Richardson's *Nietzsche's System* (1996) defends the systematic reading, and Bernard Reginster's *The Affirmation of Life* (2006) reconstructs the eternal return and the will to power as an integrated response to nihilism. Together these five works constitute the analytic Nietzsche canon and are assigned in virtually every graduate seminar.

Genealogy has escaped Nietzsche studies and become a general philosophical method. Bernard Williams's *Truth and Truthfulness* (2002) offers a "genealogical" vindication of the virtues of truth that is explicitly modeled on Nietzsche and on Hume while rejecting the deflationary conclusions of some Nietzschean readers. Miranda Fricker's *Epistemic Injustice* (2007) uses a genealogical frame to argue that credibility deficits tracked to social identity are a distinctive wrong, and the book has become foundational for contemporary social epistemology. Colin Koopman's *Genealogy as Critique* (2013) codifies the method across Nietzsche, Foucault, and Williams. The upshot is that genealogy is now a mainstream option in anglophone moral and political philosophy, and its Nietzschean provenance is explicitly acknowledged.

## Legal and institutional (brief)

Direct legal influence is slight. Nietzschean perspectivism is occasionally invoked in constitutional interpretation debates, typically as a foil for originalism or as a loose label for anti-foundationalist positions (Schlag 1997). The serious legal inheritance is indirect and travels through Foucault. Critical legal studies, critical race theory, and the Foucauldian turn in law and society scholarship rest on the Nietzschean-Foucauldian thesis that legal categories are effects of power rather than discoveries of antecedent right (Kennedy 1997; Golder and Fitzpatrick 2009). Administrative law's "governmentality" literature (Rose and Miller 1992; Dean 2010) is Foucault's Nietzschean toolkit applied to regulatory practice. These are substantial bodies of work, but the Nietzsche in them is filtered through a generation of French theory.

## Clinical and medical

The clinical reception is modest and concentrated in existential and humanistic traditions. Irvin Yalom's *Existential Psychotherapy* (1980) is the standard reference text for the existential school; Yalom's later novel *When Nietzsche Wept* (1992) dramatizes a fictional therapeutic encounter between Nietzsche and Josef Breuer and has been widely used in training programs as a portal into the tradition. Nietzsche's critique of pity as a weakening emotion, his ethic of amor fati, and the self-overcoming motif surface in logotherapy (Frankl 1946 cites Nietzsche directly, most famously "he who has a why to live for can bear almost any how") and in the broader existential-humanistic literature (May 1969). Positive psychology invokes Nietzsche episodically, typically through the maxim "that which does not kill us makes us stronger" from *Twilight of the Idols*, which has migrated into the post-traumatic growth literature (Tedeschi and Calhoun 2004); the maxim is usually cited without its Nietzschean context, which is a critique of compassion rather than a hopeful endorsement of resilience. Outside these pockets, Nietzsche has little purchase in evidence-based clinical psychology, cognitive behavioral therapy, or psychiatry, and the mainstream reception is closer to literary citation than to theoretical use.

## Pop culture, self-help, manosphere, political rhetoric

This is where the inheritance is loudest and most contested. In self-help, Ryan Holiday's Stoic revival (*The Obstacle Is the Way*, 2014; *Ego Is the Enemy*, 2016) cross-pollinates with a popular Nietzscheanism of self-overcoming even where the explicit sources are Marcus Aurelius and Epictetus. Jordan Peterson's *Maps of Meaning* (1999) and *12 Rules for Life* (2018) are the most consequential popular transmission: Peterson weaves Nietzsche with Jung, Dostoevsky, and biblical hermeneutics into a therapeutic-political program that has sold millions of copies. Peterson's Nietzsche is heavily filtered; scholarly readers have criticized the selective quotation, the flattening of the critique of Christianity, and the use of Nietzsche's anti-egalitarian passages in service of a socially conservative argument Nietzsche himself would likely have rejected (Burgis et al. 2020).

The manosphere and alt-right reception is more direct and more textually aggressive. Ronald Beiner's *Dangerous Minds: Nietzsche, Heidegger, and the Return of the Far Right* (2018) is the principal scholarly treatment. Beiner's claim, which has been controversial but well received by historians of political thought, is that Nietzsche's anti-egalitarianism, his contempt for Christian and democratic morality, and his rhetoric of hierarchy are genuinely present in the texts and are not simply distorted by Richard Spencer, Aleksandr Dugin (*The Fourth Political Theory*, 2009), or Curtis Yarvin. Malcolm Bull's *Anti-Nietzsche* (2011) and Domenico Losurdo's *Nietzsche, the Aristocratic Rebel* (2002; English 2019) are the most thorough philosophical critiques on the left: Bull proposes reading Nietzsche "like a loser" rather than identifying with the strong, and Losurdo undertakes a 1000-page historical reconstruction that locates Nietzsche firmly in the anti-socialist, anti-democratic, anti-feminist reaction of his period. Together these form the revisionist pole against the postwar Kaufmann-Deleuze-Foucault rehabilitation, and the post-2016 political climate has amplified their audibility.

Popular culture supplies the connective tissue. Wagnerian Nietzscheanism in film (Kubrick's use of *Also sprach Zarathustra* in *2001*, Malick's use of Nietzschean motifs in *The Thin Red Line*) and the prevalence of "that which does not kill us" as a t-shirt slogan make Nietzsche a diffusely available cultural resource. The pathway from a Kelly Clarkson chorus to a Peterson lecture to a 4chan thread is shorter than the scholarly reception often allows (Nagle 2017).

## Where gender travels with the inheritance

The inheritance bifurcates by gender in predictable ways. The continental and analytic academic receptions have been substantially degendered. Foucault's Nietzsche feeds feminist theory directly through Judith Butler (*Gender Trouble*, 1990), Sandra Bartky, and Susan Bordo. Clark, Nehamas, Leiter, and Reginster work with a Nietzsche from whom the misogynist aphorisms have been largely bracketed as embarrassing biographical residue. Feminist Nietzsche scholarship (Oliver 1995; Schutte 1984; Diethe 1996; the Burgard 1994 and Oliver and Pearsall 1998 collections) is a substantial subfield that has argued explicitly over whether this bracketing is defensible.

The popular inheritance, by contrast, carries gender forward. Peterson's Nietzsche-Jung synthesis imports a gendered archetypal scheme (order as masculine, chaos as feminine) into mainstream self-help and explicitly ties it to anti-feminist political positions (Peterson 2018, rule 11). The manosphere reception is inseparable from its misogyny; Nietzsche's aphorisms on women circulate on red-pill forums as textual warrant, and the master-slave schema is regularly mapped onto gender politics. Beiner (2018, chapter 4) and Nagle (2017) both argue that this is a real continuity with the texts rather than a pure distortion, which places pressure on the academic bracketing strategy.

The honest description is that Nietzsche's afterlife has split into two inheritances that share textual roots. The academic left has inherited the method (genealogy, perspectivism, the critique of morality) and discarded the gender material. The popular right has inherited the rhetoric (hierarchy, mastery, contempt for the herd) and kept the gender material intact. The companion file on Nietzsche's sexism argues that this split is not as clean as the first inheritance wants it to be; the present file's more limited claim is that both inheritances are live, both are large, and any assessment of Nietzsche's contemporary influence that attends to only one misses the phenomenon.
