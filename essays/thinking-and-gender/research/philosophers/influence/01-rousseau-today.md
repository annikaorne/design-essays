# Rousseau today

A companion literature review to the main file on Rousseau's treatment of women. This one maps the shape of his contemporary influence across academic political philosophy, legal and institutional practice, clinical and educational science, and popular political rhetoric. The aim is descriptive rather than polemical: to show where Rousseau is actually at work in 21st-century life and, where the gender inheritance travels with him, to flag it.

## Academic and intellectual

Rousseau is the single most continuously influential figure in Anglo-American political philosophy since the late 1960s, with three waves of reception visible in the literature.

The first is Rawlsian. John Rawls acknowledged Rousseau as one of his principal teachers in the history of modern political philosophy, devoting a substantial set of lectures to *The Social Contract* in his Harvard course; these were published posthumously as *Lectures on the History of Political Philosophy* (Rawls 2007). Rawls treats the general will as the direct ancestor of the original position and reads *The Social Contract* as working out a conception of autonomy in which free and equal persons legislate for themselves. Joshua Cohen's *Rousseau: A Free Community of Equals* (Cohen 2010) is the most developed Rawlsian reconstruction: Cohen argues that the deep structure of Rousseau's account of freedom, equality, and the general will is defensible on contemporary liberal-democratic grounds. Cohen brackets the sexist anthropology of *Emile* as historical residue rather than load-bearing structure, a move that structural feminist readers contest (see Pateman 1988; Weiss 1993 in the companion file).

The second wave is the republican revival. Philip Pettit's *Republicanism: A Theory of Freedom and Government* (Pettit 1997) and *On the People's Terms: A Republican Theory and Model of Democracy* (Pettit 2012) reframe freedom as non-domination rather than non-interference and position Rousseau as one of the tradition's central modern exponents, alongside Machiavelli, Harrington, and the American founders. Pettit's reading is ambivalent about Rousseau: he takes the general will as a precursor to contestatory democracy but criticizes the "communitarian" strand in Rousseau as a source of the populist pathologies republicanism ought to avoid. Quentin Skinner's parallel work on liberty before liberalism (Skinner 1998) treats Rousseau as a pivot between the neo-Roman tradition and modern democratic theory. The Pettit-Skinner revival has been institutionally consequential: it animated a generation of political theorists (Frank Lovett, Cécile Laborde, John McCormick) and has fed into legal scholarship on non-domination, deliberative accountability, and constitutional design (Laborde and Maynor 2008).

The third wave is reconstructive rather than Rawlsian or republican. Frederick Neuhouser's *Rousseau's Theodicy of Self-Love* (Neuhouser 2008) and *Rousseau's Critique of Inequality* (Neuhouser 2014) reconstruct *amour-propre* as a concept for theorizing recognition and inequality, positioning Rousseau as a forerunner of Hegel and a resource for contemporary work on status, esteem, and social pathology. Arthur Melzer's *The Natural Goodness of Man: On the System of Rousseau's Thought* (Melzer 1990) gives the most systematic Straussian-inflected account of Rousseau's underlying philosophical anthropology and has been the touchstone for readers who treat the *oeuvre* as internally unified. Christopher Bertram's *Rousseau and the Social Contract* (Bertram 2004) is the standard contemporary Anglophone introduction and has shaped how the text is taught. Patrick Riley, across a long career (Riley 1982, 1986, 2001), treated the general will as the nodal concept linking Rousseau to Kant and thence to the whole of modern moral philosophy; Riley's edited *Cambridge Companion to Rousseau* (Riley 2001) remains the standard reference. These reconstructive projects vary in their relationship to the gender critique: Neuhouser and Bertram acknowledge it and proceed; Melzer and Riley largely set it aside.

## Legal and institutional

Rousseau's institutional afterlife is best tracked in three places: participatory and direct democracy, civic republicanism as a political-legal tradition, and French constitutional thought.

Direct-democracy theory draws on Rousseau both philosophically and iconographically. Benjamin Barber's *Strong Democracy* (Barber 1984) defended a Rousseauian participatory politics against thin proceduralism and has been a touchstone for practitioners of town-hall democracy, citizen assemblies, and participatory budgeting. The Porto Alegre participatory budgeting model, now adopted in more than 3,000 municipalities worldwide, has been theorized by Archon Fung and Erik Olin Wright as "empowered participatory governance" and traced back to a Rousseauian commitment to the self-legislating community (Fung and Wright 2003). The Swiss referendum tradition and the New England town meeting are both standardly cited, in comparative-politics textbooks, as living institutional legacies of the Rousseauian insistence that sovereignty cannot be represented (Kriesi and Trechsel 2008; Bryan 2004). The empirical political science of deliberation (James Fishkin's deliberative polls, the Citizens' Assemblies used in Ireland for the 2018 abortion referendum and the 2015 marriage-equality vote) draws explicitly on Rousseau via Cohen (1989) and Habermas (Fishkin 2009; Farrell, Suiter, and Harris 2019).

Civic republicanism as a political-legal tradition is the second site. Pettit's framework of freedom as non-domination has been taken up in constitutional theory, in labor law (on workplace domination), and in international-relations theory (on hegemony and domination among states) (Pettit 2014; Gourevitch 2015). Cécile Laborde's *Republicanism and Political Theory* (Laborde and Maynor 2008) and *Liberalism's Religion* (Laborde 2017) extend Pettit's model into the law of church-state relations and minority rights. The Cambridge "neo-Roman" school (Skinner, Pocock, Viroli) has shaped how 18th-century constitutional settlements, including the American and French, are now read in legal history.

French constitutional thought is the third site and the one with the longest institutional pedigree. Rousseau is the ideological grandfather of Article 6 of the 1789 Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen ("Law is the expression of the general will"), which remains in force today as part of the preamble to the 1958 Constitution of the Fifth Republic (Morange 1988; Rials 1988). French public-law scholarship continues to debate whether the Conseil constitutionnel's evolving doctrine on popular sovereignty represents a faithful or attenuated version of *la volonté générale* (Rosanvallon 2011; Brunet 2004). Pierre Rosanvallon's *Democratic Legitimacy* (Rosanvallon 2011) treats Rousseau as the still-operative theorist of French democratic self-understanding, against which contemporary institutions of counter-democracy (judicial review, independent agencies, protest) have to justify themselves.

Where gender travels with this inheritance it does so via the public sphere. Anne Phillips's *The Politics of Presence* (Phillips 1995) and Iris Marion Young's *Justice and the Politics of Difference* (Young 1990) and *Inclusion and Democracy* (Young 2000) argue that the Rousseauian model of transparent civic unanimity, inherited by deliberative democrats, systematically screens out the embodied, affective, and particular forms of voice that women and minorities bring to politics. Joan Landes (Landes 1988) has argued, at the historical-institutional level, that the Jacobin closure of women's political clubs in 1793 and the revolutionary construction of a masculinist public sphere carry Rousseau's *Letter to d'Alembert* forward into the structure of modern republican citizenship.

## Clinical and scientific

Rousseau's presence in clinical science is indirect but real, and it runs through developmental psychology and progressive education.

Jean Piaget repeatedly acknowledged Rousseau as a precursor of the developmental idea that the child's cognition unfolds in stages and must be understood on its own terms rather than as a deficient adulthood. The Institut Jean-Jacques Rousseau in Geneva, which Piaget joined in 1921 and eventually directed, was explicitly named for Rousseau and framed its research program as a scientific continuation of *Emile*'s child-centered pedagogy (Vidal 1994). Piaget's constructivism and, downstream, Lev Vygotsky's socio-cultural psychology, Jerome Bruner's work on scaffolding, and the whole field of developmental and educational psychology have a Rousseauian ancestor in the premise that children are not miniature adults and that learning must follow the child's developmental readiness (Bruner 1996; Darling 1994).

The translation into clinical-pedagogical practice runs through Pestalozzi, Froebel, and Montessori, as detailed in the companion file, and is continued today in Reggio Emilia, Waldorf, and Montessori early-childhood programs, in the progressive-school tradition (Dewey's heirs), and in the contemporary homeschooling and unschooling movements, which often cite *Emile* directly as their charter text (Gray 2013; Holt 1977). The American child-centered reform tradition (John Dewey, Francis Parker, the Bank Street College of Education) is genealogically Rousseauian, mediated by Froebel and Pestalozzi (Cremin 1961; Darling 1994).

Jane Roland Martin's *Reclaiming a Conversation: The Ideal of the Educated Woman* (Martin 1985) is the key text for tracing how the gender inheritance travels with this clinical-pedagogical chain. Martin shows that progressive education absorbed Rousseau's developmental principles while retaining, in attenuated form, the Emile/Sophie split: the "educated person" in modern pedagogy is still substantially Emile, while the domestic and caring virtues Sophie was trained in are either devalued or naturalized as women's unpaid labor. Madeleine Grumet's *Bitter Milk* (Grumet 1988) extends the argument to teacher training. Ann Taylor Allen's work on the European kindergarten movement shows how Froebelian pedagogy, carried by women into the professions, simultaneously opened paid educational work to women and codified a maternalist theory that limited their authority to the domestic and paradomestic (Allen 1988, 2006). Contemporary debates about gender and early-childhood-education workforces, the invisibility of care work in developmental research, and the gender composition of elementary teaching are downstream of this settlement.

## Pop culture, self-help, political rhetoric, manosphere

Rousseau is a surprisingly active presence in contemporary popular and political discourse, usually via three channels.

The first is the nature-versus-civilization dichotomy. Rousseau's *Discourse on Inequality*, with its conjectural history of the corruption of an originally innocent species by society, underwrites a large share of contemporary "back to nature" writing. The deep-ecology tradition (Arne Næss, George Sessions) explicitly names Rousseau as an ancestor (Taylor 2010). Popular nonfiction from Jon Krakauer's *Into the Wild* (1996) to Sebastian Junger's *Tribe* (2016) to Yuval Noah Harari's *Sapiens* (2014) reanimates the Rousseauian thesis that something essential was lost in the transition to settled and then industrial society. The "paleo" and "ancestral-health" movements, survivalism, and parts of the wellness industry operate on a recognizably Rousseauian anthropology of corruption and recovery (Crist 2004).

The second channel is communitarian and populist political rhetoric. Michael Sandel's *Democracy's Discontent* (Sandel 1996, revised 2022) and *The Tyranny of Merit* (Sandel 2020) mobilize a civic-republican idiom that is, through Pettit and Arendt, Rousseauian in pedigree: the claim that liberal proceduralism has hollowed out the common life and that self-government requires shared civic formation. Alasdair MacIntyre's *After Virtue* (MacIntyre 1981) does not rely on Rousseau directly but shares the diagnosis that modern liberal individualism is parasitic on traditions of virtue it cannot itself sustain. This family of arguments has been influential well beyond the academy, shaping both left-communitarian and right-communitarian politics. Yael Tamir's *Why Nationalism* (Tamir 2019) argues that contemporary populism, rather than being a pathology to be explained away, is continuous with a Rousseauian insight that liberal democracy depends on a dense sense of shared fate. Tamir is unusually explicit that Rousseau is the ancestor of the position she is defending.

The third channel, more fragmentary, is appropriation of the "general will" in online political rhetoric. Integralist and post-liberal writers on the Catholic right (Adrian Vermeule, Patrick Deneen) invoke the common good in ways that echo Rousseau, usually as filtered through Catholic natural-law traditions (Deneen 2018; Vermeule 2022). Scattered alt-right and "dissident right" writers have attempted to recruit Rousseau's critique of luxury and inequality, and the general will, into majoritarian and ethno-nationalist projects; scholarly responses have emphasized that such appropriations misread Rousseau's insistence on the universality of citizenship and his explicit rejection of forcing particular wills to count as general (see Lovett 2022 for a corrective). In the manosphere proper, Rousseau is less commonly named than Nietzsche or Weininger, but the nature/civilization dichotomy and the romantic-domestic ideal both circulate in its discourse in recognizably Rousseauian form.

## Where gender travels with the inheritance

Three channels are worth flagging because they carry Rousseau's gender settlement forward more or less intact.

First, progressive education. As Martin (1985), Grumet (1988), and Allen (2006) document, the Rousseauian pedagogy that Pestalozzi, Froebel, Montessori, and Dewey carried into the modern school was only partially revised for Sophie. The gendered division between the "educated person" (Emile) and the mother-teacher (Sophie) persists in the gender composition of early-childhood teaching, in the low status of care-oriented pedagogy, and in the residual assumption that the first educator is the mother.

Second, the gendered public sphere. Landes (1988), Phillips (1995), and Young (1990, 2000) argue that the Rousseauian model of transparent civic deliberation, inherited by contemporary deliberative and republican democrats, carries forward a preference for the rational, dispassionate, universalizing voice and a corresponding suspicion of the affective, particular, and embodied. This is not a dead inheritance: it shapes how legislative speech, civic associations, and political media treat women's voices today (see Mansbridge 1999; Karpowitz and Mendelberg 2014).

Third, romantic nationalism. Fermon (1997) and Mosse (1985) argue that the Rousseauian household at Clarens became a template for 19th-century nationalist imaginations of the nation as an extended, self-disciplining family. Tamir (2019) is explicit that contemporary liberal nationalism needs the sentimental-domestic register Rousseau articulated; critics (Yuval-Davis 1997) argue that the register is not separable from its gendered structure, in which women carry the biological and cultural reproduction of the nation while men carry its political agency.

The pattern across all three is the same as in the academic reception: the structure Rousseau articulated is load-bearing in contemporary institutions, and whether his gender settlement can be detached from it is, in practice, still an open question.
