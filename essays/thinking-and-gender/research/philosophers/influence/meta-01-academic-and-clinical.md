# Academic and clinical reception today

A cross-cutting literature review of how eight historically gender-freighted thinkers (Rousseau, Kant, Hegel, Schopenhauer, Nietzsche, Freud, Proudhon, Weininger) are engaged in contemporary academic philosophy, adjacent academic disciplines, law schools, and clinical psychology and psychiatry. The aim is not to summarize any individual's system but to map where each is actually at work in the 21st-century university and clinic, and to flag where the gender inheritance travels with that work. Companion files treat the thinkers individually; this file asks, across them, which institutions still depend on their categories.

## Academic philosophy

Within anglophone academic philosophy, the eight sort into a clear core-periphery pattern. Kant is dominant, Nietzsche and Hegel are resurgent, Freud is marginal to the discipline even where he is central to the humanities, and Rousseau, Schopenhauer, Proudhon, and Weininger occupy successively narrower positions.

Kant is the most-taught single figure in analytic ethics and political philosophy. Christine Korsgaard's constructivist reading (Korsgaard 1996, 2009), Onora O'Neill's work on practical reason (O'Neill 1989, 2000), and Allen Wood's extensive reconstruction (Wood 1999, 2008) anchor an active Kantian paradigm; PhilPapers survey data consistently place Kantianism among the top two or three live positions in normative ethics alongside consequentialism and virtue ethics (Bourget and Chalmers 2014, 2023). Jay Garfield and Bryan Van Norden's intervention in *Taking Back Philosophy* (Van Norden 2017; Garfield and Van Norden 2016 in the *New York Times*) explicitly takes Kant as the exemplar of a canon that has become parochial by calling itself universal, and argues that philosophy departments that teach only the Western canon should rename themselves accordingly. The APA's demographic reports and the hiring-pattern studies compiled by Eric Schwitzgebel (Schwitzgebel and Jennings 2017) document both the ongoing Kantian dominance and the growing institutional pressure to diversify.

The analytic Hegel revival associated with Robert Pippin, Robert Brandom, Terry Pinkard, and John McDowell (Pippin 2008; Brandom 2019; Pinkard 1994) has pulled Hegel back into anglophone metaethics and philosophy of mind after decades of neglect, and the Honneth-Fraser recognition debate (Fraser and Honneth 2003; Honneth 1995) keeps the *Philosophy of Right* at the center of critical theory. Nietzsche's anglophone reception has grown steadily, led by Brian Leiter's naturalistic reading (Leiter 2002, 2015), Maudemarie Clark's work (Clark 1990; Clark and Dudrick 2012), Alexander Nehamas on the self as literary production (Nehamas 1985), John Richardson's systematic reconstructions (Richardson 1996, 2004), and Bernard Reginster's *The Affirmation of Life* (Reginster 2006). Continental reception adds Schopenhauer as a significant but smaller figure, reanimated by Christopher Janaway's Cambridge Companion and monographs (Janaway 1999, 2007) and David Cartwright's biography and guide (Cartwright 2010). Rousseau occupies a steady position in political philosophy, treated as an analytic predecessor by Joshua Cohen (Cohen 2010) and as the neo-Roman republican pivot by Philip Pettit and Quentin Skinner (Pettit 1997; Skinner 1998). Freud, Proudhon, and Weininger are effectively absent from mainstream analytic syllabi; they survive in continental philosophy and in disciplinary history, not in ethics or political philosophy proper.

Citation data, while crude, confirm the rank order. Google Scholar citation counts for the last two decades (to 2024) show Kant and Hegel each with tens of thousands of secondary-literature citations per year, Nietzsche close behind, Rousseau and Freud an order of magnitude lower, and Schopenhauer, Proudhon, and Weininger two orders lower still. Bibliometric work on *The Philosophical Quarterly*, *Ethics*, *Journal of Philosophy*, and *Mind* (Healy 2015; Kieran Healy's gender-and-citation analyses) shows Kant is the single most-cited historical figure in these journals.

The canon debate is live. Van Norden's argument, Garfield's broader push on Buddhist and Indian philosophy (Garfield 2015, 2022), and Kristie Dotson's work on the exclusionary norms of professional philosophy (Dotson 2012) all name the eight, and Kant in particular, as cases where the discipline's self-understanding conflicts with its demographic and geographic parochialism. The feminist side of this debate, running through Genevieve Lloyd's *The Man of Reason* (Lloyd 1984) and the subsequent scholarship gathered in *A Companion to Feminist Philosophy* (Jaggar and Young 1998), overlaps with the decolonial critique but is not identical to it: what Lloyd documents is that the same figures who are accused of parochialism are also the ones whose systems were built with gendered reason at their core. Where Kant's autonomy framework is criticized by decolonial scholars for its provincial universality, it is criticized by care ethicists (Held 2006; Kittay 1999) for what it leaves out, namely dependency relations and the labor that sustains them.

## Adjacent academic disciplines

Beyond philosophy, the eight thinkers distribute across adjacent disciplines along predictable lines. Political theory, as practiced in political science departments and public-affairs schools, takes Rousseau, Kant, Hegel, and Proudhon as central reference points. Honneth and Fraser's recognition-redistribution exchange is taught in graduate political theory; Pettit's non-domination republicanism has reshaped constitutional theory and political economy (Gourevitch 2015; Laborde 2017). Proudhon survives in political theory mostly through labor and anarchist-studies scholarship (Chambost 2004; Vincent 1984), and through intellectual-history treatments of nineteenth-century socialism; he is peripheral to mainstream political theory but sustained by specialist communities.

In literary studies, Nietzsche, Freud, and Weininger have had the deepest uptake through the study of modernism. Nietzsche runs through the New Historicism and through the Deleuze-Foucault line. Freud remains indispensable to psychoanalytic literary criticism even as departments that were once wholly Freudian have broadened to Lacan, Kristeva, and Bersani (Bersani 1986; Rose 1986). Weininger persists as a reference point for scholarship on modernist masculinity, antisemitism, and sexology, mostly in the wake of Chandak Sengoopta's *Otto Weininger: Sex, Science, and Self in Imperial Vienna* (Sengoopta 2000) and Jacques Le Rider's biography (Le Rider 1993).

Cultural studies takes Freud, Nietzsche, and Hegel as the standard theoretical triad, mediated by the Frankfurt School, Foucault, and the Birmingham tradition. The history of science and medicine treats Freud and Weininger as major case studies: Freud as the subject of an enormous rehabilitation-or-debunking literature (Crews 2017; Borch-Jacobsen and Shamdasani 2012) and Weininger as a key case in histories of turn-of-the-century sexology (Harrowitz and Hyams 1995; Sengoopta 2000). Women's and gender studies engages all eight critically, as the companion files document; the field treats them less as resources than as archaeological sites of gendered reason.

## Law schools

Legal academic engagement with these thinkers is narrower than philosophical engagement, and it concentrates on four threads.

First, Kantian dignity-based reasoning is the backbone of modern bioethics (Beauchamp and Childress 2019, now in its eighth edition) and of much international human rights theory. Seyla Benhabib's *The Rights of Others* (Benhabib 2004) is the most cited contemporary articulation of a Kantian cosmopolitan framework for refugee and citizenship law; her *Dignity in Adversity* (Benhabib 2011) extends it. Jeremy Waldron (Waldron 2012) and George Kateb (Kateb 2011) have built parallel Kantian dignity arguments in constitutional theory. Care ethicists and disability scholars (Kittay 1999; Nussbaum 2006) have pressed back on the autonomy presumption, arguing it cannot accommodate dependency, which is a direct descendant of the Kantian gender critique.

Second, Rousseau's general will persists in democratic theory and in the law of direct democracy, referendum procedure, and deliberative assemblies (Fishkin 2009; Rosanvallon 2011). Legal scholarship on participatory budgeting, citizens' assemblies, and jury theory routinely cites Rousseau as the foundational figure.

Third, Hegelian recognition has reshaped identity-based legal claims. Charles Taylor's "The Politics of Recognition" (Taylor 1992) and Will Kymlicka's *Multicultural Citizenship* (Kymlicka 1995) are the central references in Canadian, Australian, and European jurisprudence on group rights, indigenous claims, and religious accommodation. Patchen Markell's *Bound by Recognition* (Markell 2003) is the sharpest internal critique and is widely taught.

Fourth, Freud's footprint in criminal law has been shrinking for decades. Historically, psychoanalytic theory supported an expansionary understanding of the insanity defense (Model Penal Code §4.01, pre-Hinckley); the retrenchment after United States v. Hinckley (1982) tracked the wider academic-psychology retreat from Freud. Contemporary forensic psychiatry is Kraepelinian and DSM-based rather than Freudian; Stephen Morse's extensive law and psychiatry work (Morse 2011) represents the mainstream position.

The remaining four figures have limited direct influence in law schools. Schopenhauer and Nietzsche appear mostly filtered through Foucault in critical legal studies, law and society, and punishment theory (Garland 1990). Proudhon persists in labor-law historiography and in property-theory scholarship that traces the critique of absolute ownership (Pottage and Mundy 2004). Weininger is essentially absent.

## Clinical psychology and psychiatry

The clinical situation is dominated by the Freud question. Academic psychology has substantially abandoned Freud as a scientific theorist. Adolf Grünbaum's *The Foundations of Psychoanalysis* (Grünbaum 1984) is the philosophically decisive critique of Freud's claim to empirical support; Frederick Crews's long polemical campaign, culminating in *Freud: The Making of an Illusion* (Crews 2017), is the historical version. Undergraduate psychology textbooks in the United States typically present Freud as a historical figure rather than a live theoretical option, and empirically-minded clinical psychology has moved to cognitive-behavioral, third-wave, and evidence-based frameworks.

Clinical practice tells a different story. Jonathan Shedler's meta-analytic review of psychodynamic therapy in *American Psychologist* (Shedler 2010) documents effect sizes comparable to CBT and durable benefits after treatment ends; the APA's Division 39 (Psychoanalysis) and the journal *Psychoanalytic Psychology* sustain an active clinical tradition. Object relations, relational analysis, and self psychology (the Winnicott-Klein-Fairbairn line, the Kohut tradition, and the Mitchell-Stern relational school) carry Freudian language forward in modified form (Mitchell and Aron 1999). Jonathan Lear's work (Lear 2005, 2014) is the most philosophically serious defense of psychoanalysis as a live practice.

Mark Solms's neuropsychoanalysis is the most empirically ambitious contemporary rehabilitation of a Freudian framework. Solms's work on dreaming and on the neural basis of affect (Solms 2000; Solms 2021) argues that specific Freudian hypotheses can be tested against neuroscience, and the journal *Neuropsychoanalysis* has become the institutional home of this program. It is a minority position, but it is the most serious attempt to reintegrate Freud into empirical science.

Outside the Freudian center, Nietzsche is alive in existential and humanistic psychotherapy through Irvin Yalom (Yalom 1980) and through the Rollo May tradition; Schopenhauer sits behind David Benatar's antinatalism (Benatar 2006) and behind a small philosophical-psychotherapy literature; Rousseau appears in developmental psychology's genealogy via Piaget; Kant, Hegel, Proudhon, and Weininger have no meaningful clinical presence.

## Gender threads in the contemporary academic reception

The gender inheritance travels with this reception in four places worth naming.

First, the Kantian autonomy framework that anchors bioethics and human rights law is the target of an established care-ethics critique. Virginia Held, Joan Tronto, Eva Kittay, and Martha Nussbaum (Held 2006; Tronto 1993; Kittay 1999; Nussbaum 2006) argue that the autonomous rights-bearing subject inherits a gendered division between reason and dependency that Kant articulated explicitly in the *Anthropology* and that reconstructive Kantians have been slow to address.

Second, the recognition turn in Hegelian political theory inherits §166's division of love, rights, and solidarity. Nancy Fraser's critique in *Redistribution or Recognition?* (Fraser and Honneth 2003) and Amy Allen's subsequent work (Allen 2008) press the point that the family-civil-society split Honneth uses is precisely the one feminist political theory has most consistently criticized.

Third, object-relations and relational-psychoanalytic clinical traditions use a feminine-coded vocabulary of holding, containing, and mothering (Winnicott, Bion, Klein) that Nancy Chodorow's *The Reproduction of Mothering* (Chodorow 1978) and Jessica Benjamin's *The Bonds of Love* (Benjamin 1988) treat as both diagnostic and problematic: the clinical language maps onto a cultural assignment of care to women that it neither invented nor dissolves.

Fourth, the Freud-Jung-Nietzsche synthesis visible in popular clinical writing (most prominently Jordan Peterson's *Maps of Meaning* and *12 Rules for Life*) travels outside the academy and into a popular-psychology market. The content of that synthesis replays gender material from all three thinkers with little modification; Donna Zuckerberg's *Not All Dead White Men* (Zuckerberg 2018) traces the analogous phenomenon for Stoicism and classical philosophy in the manosphere.

Across the four domains the pattern is the one the companion files document: the systems the 21st-century academy and clinic still use were built with gendered content at load-bearing positions, and the reconstructive work to detach that content is real but incomplete.
