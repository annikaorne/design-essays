# Freud today

Sigmund Freud (1856 to 1939) is in the strange position of being simultaneously discredited and inescapable. A hundred years after *The Ego and the Id*, serious clinical psychology rarely cites him; yet his vocabulary saturates ordinary speech, his concepts anchor much of the contemporary humanities, and a refurbished psychodynamic tradition quietly retains clinical ground against cognitive behavioral therapy. This review surveys four domains where that contradictory legacy plays out, and flags where gendered assumptions travel with the inheritance.

## Academic and intellectual

The dominant academic verdict on Freud the scientist is negative and has been for roughly forty years. Adolf Grünbaum's *The Foundations of Psychoanalysis* (1984) is still the canonical epistemological indictment. Grünbaum argued that psychoanalytic inference rests on what he called the "Tally Argument," a circular reliance on clinical data contaminated by suggestion, and concluded that classical psychoanalysis fails as a causal science of the unconscious (Grünbaum, 1984). Frederick Crews extended that critique into biography with *Freud: The Making of an Illusion* (2017), a hostile historiography marshaling archival evidence to portray Freud as a careerist who manipulated cases and invented data. Reviews in *The New Yorker* and elsewhere described it as polemical but thoroughly documented (Crews, 2017; Menand, 2017).

Rehabilitation efforts persist. Mark Solms's neuropsychoanalysis project, consolidated in *The Hidden Spring* (2021), tries to ground Freudian affect theory in brainstem anatomy, locating the "source of consciousness" in subcortical arousal systems rather than cortex. Reviewers have treated Solms's broader metaphysics skeptically while granting that the affect-first framework has empirical traction (Solms, 2021; Fernyhough, 2021). Jonathan Lear's *Freud* (2005), in the Routledge Philosophers series, offers the most careful philosophical defense: he reads Freud not as a failed natural scientist but as a theorist of self-understanding whose account of unconscious motivation remains indispensable to the philosophy of mind (Lear, 2005). Drew Westen's *Psychological Bulletin* essay "The Scientific Legacy of Sigmund Freud" (1998) had earlier argued that many Freudian propositions, once stripped of drive metapsychology, survive in mainstream cognitive and social psychology as claims about unconscious process, ambivalence, and affect regulation (Westen, 1998).

In literature, cultural studies, film theory, and continental philosophy, Freud remains load-bearing. Lacanian psychoanalysis, transmitted in the United States largely through Bruce Fink's clinical and translational work, continues to furnish a vocabulary for subject formation, desire, and the signifier (Fink, 1997). Slavoj Žižek's ideology critique is structurally Lacanian and therefore genealogically Freudian. Judith Butler's *Gender Trouble* (1990) depends on a Freudian account of melancholic identification to explain how prohibited attachments become sedimented as gender (Butler, 1990). The humanities have not moved past Freud; they have metabolized him.

## Legal and institutional

Freud's legal influence peaked mid-century and has receded, but residues persist. Psychoanalytic psychiatry underwrote the post-war expansion of the insanity defense and the liberalization of psychiatric expert testimony; Durham v. United States (1954) and the American Law Institute test reflected a psychodynamic view of agency as overdetermined by unconscious conflict. The backlash, including the Insanity Defense Reform Act of 1984 after the Hinckley verdict, tightened standards and restricted expert testimony, in part as a reaction against perceived psychoanalytic overreach (Melton et al., 2018).

Child custody law carries a more durable psychoanalytic trace. Goldstein, Freud (Anna), and Solnit's *Beyond the Best Interests of the Child* (1973) introduced the "psychological parent" and "least detrimental alternative" doctrines that reshaped family courts across common-law jurisdictions; both concepts sit inside an object-relations picture of the child's developmental needs (Goldstein, Freud, and Solnit, 1973). Contemporary custody frameworks have largely migrated to attachment theory, but attachment itself shares object-relations ancestry via Bowlby, so the inheritance is attenuated rather than severed.

## Clinical and medical

The clinical domain is the most contested. Mainstream evidence-based clinical psychology, driven by randomized trials, insurance reimbursement, and manualized treatment, has largely displaced psychoanalysis; cognitive behavioral therapy is now the default first-line treatment for most common disorders. Biological psychiatry, anchored in DSM diagnosis and pharmacotherapy, has absorbed the institutional authority psychoanalysis held from the 1940s through the 1970s.

The more interesting story is a partial comeback. Jonathan Shedler's 2010 *American Psychologist* paper "The Efficacy of Psychodynamic Psychotherapy" synthesized meta-analyses showing that psychodynamic therapy produces effect sizes comparable to or exceeding those of CBT for depression, anxiety, personality disorders, and somatic conditions, with gains that grow rather than fade at follow-up (Shedler, 2010). Leichsenring and Rabung's *JAMA* meta-analysis of long-term psychodynamic psychotherapy reached similar conclusions for complex presentations, particularly personality and chronic disorders (Leichsenring and Rabung, 2008). These findings have not restored psychoanalysis to dominance, but they have stabilized psychodynamic therapy as an empirically respectable alternative.

Institutionally, the APA's Division 39 (Society for Psychoanalysis and Psychoanalytic Psychology) remains active; psychoanalytic training institutes still credential hundreds of analysts a year. The live theoretical traditions are largely post-Freudian: Otto Kernberg's integration of ego psychology and object relations underpins Transference-Focused Psychotherapy for borderline personality disorder, which has its own evidence base (Kernberg, 1984; Clarkin, Yeomans, and Kernberg, 2006). Heinz Kohut's self psychology reframed narcissism as a deficit in early empathic mirroring rather than a libidinal fixation (Kohut, 1977). The relational and intersubjective school, associated with Stephen Mitchell, Jessica Benjamin, and Lewis Aron, displaced drive theory in favor of a two-person psychology of mutual recognition (Mitchell, 1988; Benjamin, 1988). What survives clinically is therefore not Freud's metapsychology but a distributed network of successor theories that still take the unconscious and transference seriously.

## Pop culture and political rhetoric

Freud's popular influence is both diffuse and deep. "Freudian slip," "repressed," "projection," "in denial," "anal retentive," "ego," "id," "superego," and "unconscious" are ordinary English. Pop psychology books routinely invoke Freudian motifs even when disavowing him. Adam Curtis's BBC documentary *The Century of the Self* (2002) is the definitive treatment of the line from psychoanalysis to consumer capitalism, tracing how Edward Bernays (Freud's nephew) imported psychoanalytic theories of irrational desire into American public relations, advertising, and eventually political consulting (Curtis, 2002). Curtis's argument, that twentieth-century democracies learned to govern through manufactured desire rather than rational appeal, has become the standard critical narrative about mass persuasion.

In the contemporary manosphere and adjacent political rhetoric, Freud reappears largely through Jungian mediation. Jordan Peterson's *Maps of Meaning* (1999) and *12 Rules for Life* (2018) fuse Freud, Jung, and Nietzsche, treating archetypes, the "shadow," and dominance hierarchies as anthropological universals. Critics have pointed out that Peterson's reading of both Freud and Jung is idiosyncratic, leaning on Jung to escape Freudian sexual materialism while retaining a depth-psychological frame (Burgis et al., 2020). The clinical authority granted by a depth-psychology vocabulary is doing substantial rhetorical work in that project.

## Where gender travels with the inheritance

Gender rarely travels silently with Freud. Four patterns recur.

First, Peterson's synthesis packages Jung-Freud depth psychology with gender essentialism: order as masculine, chaos as feminine, Logos as male, Eros as female. The philosophical vocabulary lends scientific-sounding authority to what is, on inspection, a metaphysics of sexual difference (Burgis et al., 2020).

Second, the "feminine masochism" trope survives. Freud's 1924 essay, which folded women's purported passivity into a drive-theoretic frame, has been reworked by queer theorists and feminist critics (Butler's melancholic identification, Deleuze's account of masochism) but also persists unreconstructed in popular sexology and some strands of pornography studies (Butler, 1990; Deleuze, 1991).

Third, feminist object relations is Freud's most successful gender afterlife and its most double-edged. Nancy Chodorow's *The Reproduction of Mothering* (1978) used object-relations theory to argue that exclusive female mothering reproduces gendered psychic structures across generations; Jessica Benjamin's *The Bonds of Love* (1988) extended this into a theory of mutual recognition and the gendered pathologies of domination (Chodorow, 1978; Benjamin, 1988). Both are indispensable feminist theorizations, and both leave in place a feminine-coded "mothering" register that naturalizes particular divisions of care.

Fourth, Freud's own image of femininity as a "dark continent" continues to circulate as a critical trope, marking a recurring move in which theorists either disown it (Firestone, Millett) or reinhabit it to expose its workings (Mitchell, Rose, Butler). In either direction, gender is not a peripheral feature of Freud's legacy. It is the seam along which much of the contemporary argument still runs.
