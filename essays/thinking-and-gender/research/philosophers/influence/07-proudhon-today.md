# Proudhon today

Pierre-Joseph Proudhon (1809 to 1865) occupies an unusual position in the history of modern political thought. He is the first self-described anarchist, a founding theorist of mutualism and federalism, and the target of Marx's most sustained polemic against a contemporary socialist. Yet his contemporary reception in English is thinner than that of Marx, Bakunin, or Kropotkin. This review surveys Proudhon's presence across four domains today, flagging where his gender politics travel with his inheritance.

## Academic and intellectual

English-language Proudhon scholarship has been carried by a small set of specialists. K. Steven Vincent's *Pierre-Joseph Proudhon and the Rise of French Republican Socialism* (Oxford University Press, 1984) remains the standard English monograph, situating Proudhon within the republican and associationist currents of the 1840s rather than treating him as a freestanding anarchist eccentric. Vincent's reading has shaped how subsequent anglophone historians of socialism place Proudhon in the pre-1848 intellectual field.

The most significant recent scholarly development is Edward Castleton's editorial and interpretive project recovering the full archive. Proudhon left thousands of pages of unpublished manuscripts in the Besancon municipal library, and Castleton has argued across a series of articles and edited volumes that the published corpus gives a misleading picture of his thought. Castleton's essays in *History of European Ideas*, *Modern Intellectual History*, and edited collections such as *The European Anarchist Tradition* have pressed for a Proudhon who is more conservative, more religiously preoccupied, and more entangled with nineteenth-century French republicanism than the anarchist canon admits.

Iain McKay's *Property Is Theft! A Pierre-Joseph Proudhon Anthology* (AK Press, 2011) is the most important anglophone source for non-specialists. McKay translated or re-edited key texts, wrote a long introduction, and has since published essays defending Proudhon's relevance while criticizing his misogyny (see "Proudhon, Property, and Possession" and his *Anarchist Studies* and *Anarchist Writers* essays). The anthology made Proudhon teachable again in English.

Within anarchist historiography, Proudhon retains a foundational but contested place. George Woodcock's *Anarchism: A History of Libertarian Ideas and Movements* (1962; reissued) gave him a long biographical chapter and framed him as the originator. Peter Marshall's *Demanding the Impossible: A History of Anarchism* (1992; Harper Perennial, 2008) sustains this framing while noting the misogyny directly. Both remain reference points, though specialists now treat them as narrative rather than analytical.

In political economy and intellectual history more broadly, Warren Breckman's work on French socialism and post-Hegelianism, and Mark Leier's biography of Bakunin (*Bakunin: The Creative Passion*, 2006), situate Proudhon within the broader nineteenth-century left. Gareth Stedman Jones's writings on Marx engage Proudhon primarily through the lens of *The Poverty of Philosophy* and the 1840s Paris milieu.

Overall the academic footprint is real but small. The English-language Proudhon literature numbers in the dozens of monographs and articles, not the thousands that attend Marx, and much of it circles the same canonical texts.

## Legal and institutional

Proudhon's direct institutional footprint is diffuse but real in three areas.

First, mutualism as a framework for alternative economic arrangements. Credit unions, mutual insurance companies, and cooperative banks have historical genealogies that intersect with Proudhonian mutualism, though the direct theoretical lineage is often loose. His proposal for a People's Bank (*Banque du Peuple*, 1849) failed in execution but is regularly cited in histories of cooperative finance and in contemporary mutualist writing.

Second, federalism in political theory. *Du principe federatif* (1863) is the text most cited by contemporary political theorists interested in subsidiarity, plurinational federation, and non-state forms of political association. Scholars working on European federalism and on Catalan and Basque political thought have periodically reclaimed Proudhon, and Castleton's editorial work has made the late federalist writings more accessible.

Third, land-use and usufruct debates. Proudhon's distinction between property and possession, and his defense of usufruct, surface intermittently in legal scholarship on commons, on indigenous land claims, and on housing. The engagement is usually gestural rather than sustained.

## Clinical and medical

Minimal direct influence. Proudhon has no meaningful presence in clinical psychology, medicine, or the health sciences. Occasional references in medical humanities or in histories of nineteenth-century French social thought do not constitute a research program.

## Pop culture and political rhetoric

Proudhon's contemporary visibility outside the academy is carried almost entirely by a single slogan, "Property is theft," and by a small set of anarchist and cooperativist scenes.

The anarchism revival of the 2000s and 2010s brought him back into circulation. David Graeber occasionally cited Proudhon but was substantively closer to Kropotkin and to ethnographic anarchism; *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (2011) references Proudhon in passing. Occupy Wall Street and post-Occupy movements drew selectively on Proudhonian themes of federation, mutual aid, and anti-property rhetoric without systematic engagement.

In crypto, peer-to-peer, and decentralization discourse, Proudhonian mutualism is invoked with varying fidelity. Some cyberlibertarian writers have claimed a mutualist lineage for distributed ledgers and platform decentralization; Shawn Wilbur, who runs the Libertarian Labyrinth translation project and is the most prolific contemporary translator of Proudhon into English, has repeatedly pushed back on these readings in his online essays and in *Anarchist Studies*. Wilbur's corrective work, along with McKay's, is the contemporary filter through which serious readers encounter Proudhon in English.

Platform cooperativism is the clearest contemporary movement to cite mutualist lineage explicitly. Nathan Schneider (*Everything for Everyone*, 2018) and Trebor Scholz (*Uberworked and Underpaid*, 2016; *Own This!*, 2023) name Proudhon and the mutualist tradition as forebears of worker-owned platforms and cooperative digital economies.

In France, Proudhon's influence on syndicalism, via Georges Sorel and the *Confederation Generale du Travail*, continues to shape French labor politics in diffuse ways. Historians of French labor, including Michelle Perrot and more recently Xavier Vigna, treat Proudhonian and syndicalist inheritances as constitutive of a distinctively French labor culture.

The manosphere rarely engages Proudhon directly. He is not a canonical reference for contemporary anti-feminist online writing in the way Nietzsche or evolutionary psychology figures are. His "housewife or harlot" dichotomy (*La Pornocratie*, published posthumously in 1875) surfaces occasionally in anti-feminist contexts, usually filtered through secondary paraphrase rather than read.

## Where gender travels with the inheritance

Proudhon's misogyny is severe and well documented: the "housewife or harlot" formulation, the hierarchy of strength that justifies male domestic authority, the 1858 polemics against Jenny d'Hericourt and Juliette Adam. Three strands of contemporary scholarship track how this gender politics has traveled with Proudhon's broader inheritance.

The first is the patriarchal-left critique. Joan Wallach Scott's *Gender and the Politics of History* (Columbia University Press, 1988; revised 1999) and her essays on French labor history argue that nineteenth-century French socialism and syndicalism inherited a male-breadwinner model whose intellectual scaffolding is substantially Proudhonian. Joan Landes's *Women and the Public Sphere in the Age of the French Revolution* (Cornell University Press, 1988) positions Proudhon within a longer French republican tradition that constructed citizenship as masculine and confined women to a domestic sphere. Later French labor historiography (Laura Levine Frader, Judith Coffin) extends this analysis into the twentieth century.

The second is the anarchist reckoning with the founder's misogyny. McKay's introduction to *Property Is Theft!* addresses the misogyny directly and argues it is separable from the mutualist economics, a position Wilbur has also defended in more qualified form on the Libertarian Labyrinth site. Contemporary anarchist feminists, including writers associated with *Perspectives on Anarchist Theory* and *Anarchist Studies*, have been less forgiving, treating the misogyny as constitutive rather than incidental.

The third is the rediscovery of Jenny d'Hericourt. Karen Offen's 1987 *Signs* essay, "A Nineteenth-Century French Feminist Rediscovered: Jenny P. d'Hericourt, 1809 to 1875," and Caroline Arni and other subsequent feminist historians, have placed d'Hericourt's 1860 *La femme affranchie* back in the record as a direct and devastating contemporary response to Proudhon. This rediscovery has reframed the 1850s debate: where earlier historiography treated Proudhon's misogyny as a personal eccentricity, Offen's recovery shows that it was publicly contested at the time by a feminist interlocutor whom the tradition then forgot.

Taken together, the three strands complicate any clean separation of Proudhon's economic from his gender theory. The federalism and mutualism travel; so does the household hierarchy, and the latter has had its own institutional afterlife in the male-breadwinner politics of French and European labor.
