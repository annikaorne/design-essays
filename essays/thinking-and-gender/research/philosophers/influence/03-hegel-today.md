# Hegel today

Hegel's contemporary influence is unusually wide. Two centuries after his death, his categories still organize how social and political theorists talk about recognition, civil society, the state, freedom, and history; his dialectical method supplies the frame for a live Anglophone and continental reception; and his name has migrated into public intellectual life through Slavoj Žižek and into alt-political rhetoric through garbled invocations of "dialectics." This review maps the inheritance across four domains (academic, legal and institutional, clinical and medical, and pop cultural and political) and flags the places where gender travels with the inheritance. The companion file `03-hegel.md` documents what Hegel actually wrote about women and the structural versus reconstructive debate about whether that content is separable from the system. The question here is what happens to those same architectural choices when the system continues to do work in contemporary thought.

## Academic and intellectual

The most consequential twentieth and twenty-first century academic uptake of Hegel is the recognition tradition. Axel Honneth's *The Struggle for Recognition* (1995, German 1992) rebuilds Hegel's early Jena writings into a social theory in which moral development and political conflict both turn on the three spheres of love, rights, and solidarity. Honneth's *Freedom's Right* (2014, German 2011) then returns to the *Philosophy of Right* as a model for a "normative reconstruction" of contemporary liberal democracies, treating the family, civil society, and the democratic public as institutional expressions of social freedom. Charles Taylor's "The Politics of Recognition" (1992) independently brought Hegel's master/slave dialectic into liberal political theory as the scaffolding for multicultural claims, arguing that misrecognition is a form of harm and that identity is constitutively dialogical. Taylor's earlier *Hegel* (1975) had already positioned him as the anglophone mediator of Hegel for post-1960s political philosophy.

Nancy Fraser's exchange with Honneth in *Redistribution or Recognition?* (2003) is the internal critique that matters most for present purposes. Fraser accepts that recognition is indispensable but argues that a recognition-first paradigm risks occluding economic maldistribution and political misrepresentation, and that Honneth's Hegelian monism, which treats all injustices as ultimately misrecognition, cannot hold. Amy Allen's *The Politics of Our Selves* (2008) and her later work press an adjacent feminist critique, arguing that Honneth's account of the family as a sphere of love rather than power reproduces a Hegelian split the feminist critique of Hegel should have foreclosed (Allen 2008). The Fraser and Allen critiques both attack Hegel's contemporary uptake at the place where his §166 division (family as feeling, civil society and state as universality) most cleanly survives.

A parallel strand is the analytic Hegel revival. Robert Pippin's *Hegel's Practical Philosophy* (2008) reads the *Philosophy of Right* as a theory of rational agency realized in social institutions, explicitly treating Hegel as a resource for contemporary liberal theory rather than a period figure. Robert Brandom's *A Spirit of Trust* (2019) is the most ambitious recent anglophone reconstruction, recasting the *Phenomenology* as a theory of normative commitment and mutual recognition in which rational inference is itself a social practice. Terry Pinkard's *Hegel's Naturalism* (2012), which develops arguments from his earlier *Hegel's Phenomenology: The Sociality of Reason* (1994), pushes back on purely linguistic readings by rooting Hegel's account of freedom in a non-reductive naturalism about human life form. These three authors, sometimes called the Pittsburgh Hegelians together with John McDowell, have put Hegel at the center of post-analytic philosophy of mind and ethics.

On the continental side, Slavoj Žižek's *Less Than Nothing* (2012) and *Absolute Recoil* (2014) offer an explicitly Lacanian Hegel, in which negativity, failure, and self-relating contradiction are read as the real content of dialectics, not as moments to be sublated into a harmonious totality. Catherine Malabou's *The Future of Hegel* (French 1996, English 2005), developed from her doctorate under Derrida, reintroduces the category of "plasticity" and argues that Hegel's system is self-transforming rather than closed, a reading with downstream influence in neuroscience-adjacent philosophy. Frederick Beiser's historical work, notably *Hegel* (2005), has anchored the academic reception by insisting Hegel's system must be read in its post-Kantian context rather than as a prophet of the present.

## Legal and institutional

In legal and political theory, Hegel's influence runs most clearly through two channels. The first is civil society theory. Jean Cohen and Andrew Arato's *Civil Society and Political Theory* (1992) is the canonical post-1989 reformulation, and it is explicitly Hegelian: they take Hegel's tripartite distinction between family, civil society, and state as the analytic grid for a non-Marxist theory of democratic legitimacy, revised via Habermas and Gramsci. The Cohen and Arato framework has become standard reference in democratization scholarship, human rights NGOs, and European Union institutional theory. It also imports Hegel's specific arrangement of the family as a sphere distinct from civil society, a choice that is analytically convenient and genealogically freighted.

The second channel is recognition-based legal and political theory. Will Kymlicka's *Multicultural Citizenship* (1995) works with a different philosophical vocabulary than Taylor but engages his Hegelian argument directly, and Kymlicka's framework has shaped minority rights jurisprudence in Canada and beyond. Patchen Markell's *Bound by Recognition* (2003) offers the sharpest internal critique of the Hegelian turn in identity-based legal claims, arguing that the politics of recognition inherits a sovereign-subject picture that misrepresents the dependencies and vulnerabilities it was meant to address. Markell's argument is important because it shows the continuing legal-theoretical stakes of getting the Hegelian inheritance right.

Hegelian strands also persist in Marxist legal theory, including in the work of the Frankfurt School tradition that reached Honneth, and in readings of commodity form and state form in Evgeny Pashukanis's general theory of law, itself a Marxist reworking of Hegelian categories that continues to be reissued and debated in critical legal studies. Susan Marks and others have developed this lineage into critiques of international legal universalism.

## Clinical and medical

Direct clinical influence is limited. Hegel has no analog to Freud's role in clinical psychology. There are, however, two indirect transmission routes worth naming. The first runs through Alexandre Kojève's *Introduction to the Reading of Hegel* (1947, English 1969), whose Paris lectures in the 1930s taught the master/slave dialectic to a generation that included Jacques Lacan, Maurice Merleau-Ponty, Georges Bataille, and Simone de Beauvoir (Roth 1988). Lacan's account of the mirror stage and of desire as "the desire of the Other" carries the Kojèvean Hegel directly into psychoanalysis, and Lacanian clinical traditions, particularly in France, Argentina, and Slovenia, retain that vocabulary. The second route is the organicist strand in philosophy of medicine and in holistic accounts of the person. Contemporary philosophers of medicine drawing on Hegelian and post-Hegelian continental resources, including Havi Carel's phenomenology of illness, retain a broadly holistic picture that shares family resemblance with Hegel's philosophy of organism without direct filiation. The clinical footprint is real but modest relative to the academic and political footprint.

## Pop culture and political rhetoric

Hegel's most visible pop-cultural afterlife is Žižek, whose books, lectures, documentaries, and interviews have placed Hegel into mainstream discourse in a way no other twentieth or twenty-first century philosopher has managed. For a non-academic reader, Žižek is often the vector for the claim that "Hegel is back." The scholarly verdict on this vector is mixed; Žižek's Hegel is idiosyncratic, but the reception effect is real.

Francis Fukuyama's *The End of History and the Last Man* (1992), drawing openly on Kojève, recast the post-Cold War moment as the Hegelian consummation of the struggle for recognition in liberal democracy. The argument was attacked from every direction after 2001 and again after 2016, including by Fukuyama himself, but its framing language, recognition, *thymos*, and end-state, remains in political commentary. On the right, self-consciously Hegelian rhetoric circulates through Mencius Moldbug (Curtis Yarvin) and adjacent neo-reactionary writers who invoke dialectics as a pretense of depth; the scholarly consensus, including Ronald Beiner's *Dangerous Minds* (2018), is that this is not a serious engagement with Hegel but a genealogical costume. Richard Spencer has similarly appropriated Hegel's name in public self-presentation. The point is sociological rather than philosophical: "Hegelian" has become a signaling term in segments of online political rhetoric.

Identity politics debates, both academic and journalistic, routinely invoke "recognition" as the analytic frame, and the frame descends through Taylor and Honneth from Hegel whether or not that genealogy is named. Susan Buck-Morss's *Hegel, Haiti, and Universal History* (2009), expanding her *Critical Inquiry* article of 2000, has become a widely cited reassessment that re-enters Hegel into discussions of race, empire, and universal history. Buck-Morss shows that Hegel followed the Haitian Revolution in real time and that the master/slave dialectic of the *Phenomenology* is legible against that historical backdrop; the argument has been both embraced and contested but has shifted the public face of Hegel meaningfully.

## Where gender travels with the inheritance

Three places in the contemporary uptake carry Hegel's gender architecture forward.

First, the recognition turn inherits the §166 division in practice even when it disavows it in principle. Honneth's first sphere in *The Struggle for Recognition* is "love," exemplified by the family and by affective primary relations; his second sphere is legal-cognitive respect in civil society; his third is esteem in solidaristic community. Fraser's critique that recognition is insufficient without redistribution and representation is in part a critique of this division, and Amy Allen and Kimberly Hutchings have argued that feminist claims are only partially captured by a recognition paradigm built on love-rights-solidarity because the paradigm reproduces the public-private split Hegel bequeathed to it (Fraser and Honneth 2003; Allen 2008; Hutchings 2003). The Fraser and Honneth debate is therefore legible as a continuation of the feminist critique of Hegel inside contemporary political philosophy.

Second, Hegelian civil society theory, as in Cohen and Arato, retains the family as a prior sphere of intimate life distinct from civil society. Feminist scholarship on care work, especially after Nancy Folbre and the feminist economists, has argued that this separation is precisely what produces the invisibility of reproductive labor in standard political economy. The Hegelian frame does not cause the invisibility, but it supplies the categorical backing for keeping it there.

Third, Kimberly Hutchings' *Hegel and Feminist Philosophy* (2003) makes the case that Hegel remains methodologically important for feminism, despite the content of §§165-166, because the dialectic supplies resources for theorizing how gender is socially constituted through mediated opposition rather than natural given. Catherine Malabou's *Changing Difference: The Feminine and the Question of Philosophy* (French 2009, English 2011) extends a similar argument from within continental feminism: Hegel's plasticity, read against Hegel's own conclusions, is a resource for rethinking sexual difference without essentialism. These are explicitly reconstructive uses, and their presence is evidence for Benhabib and Hutchings' standing position that the system is partly recoverable. They also illustrate the cost of recovery: each such project does visible argumentative work to detach categories from the gendered assignments Hegel gave them.
