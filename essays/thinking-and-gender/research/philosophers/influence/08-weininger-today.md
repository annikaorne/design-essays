# Weininger today

Otto Weininger (1880 to 1903) is the limit case of the series. His contemporary influence is almost entirely historical-scholarly, and where it reaches beyond the academy it reaches as a symptom rather than a resource. The book that made him famous, *Geschlecht und Charakter* (1903), fuses a neo-Kantian metaphysics of will, a Schopenhauerian pessimism, a hereditarian sexology, and an eliminationist antisemitism into a single system in which "Woman" and "the Jew" function as structurally identical failures of moral personhood. That architecture is why Weininger is unusually difficult to partition. The misogyny is not a detachable blemish on an otherwise interesting epistemology; it is the epistemology. Every serious scholar of Weininger since the 1980s has made some version of that point, and it shapes how his contemporary afterlife has to be read.

## Academic and intellectual

The modern scholarly recovery of Weininger begins with Jacques Le Rider's *Le cas Otto Weininger* (1982; English translation *The Case of Otto Weininger*, 1993), which treats Weininger as a diagnostic of fin de siècle Viennese identity crisis rather than as a philosopher to be reconstructed. Chandak Sengoopta's *Otto Weininger: Sex, Science, and Self in Imperial Vienna* (University of Chicago Press, 2000) is the definitive contextual study; Sengoopta situates Weininger inside the sexual science of Krafft-Ebing, Fliess, and early endocrinology, and shows how scientific respectability gave the misogyny and antisemitism their carrying power. Nancy Harrowitz and Barbara Hyams's edited volume *Jews and Gender: Responses to Otto Weininger* (Temple University Press, 1995) remains the central collection, with essays by Sander Gilman, John Hoberman, and others tracking the entanglement of the two prejudices in Weininger's system and in the responses it provoked.

Feminist historiography has treated Weininger as a revealing rather than a serious object. Susan C. Anderson's "Otto Weininger's Masculine Utopia" (*German Studies Review* 19:3, 1996) reads the book as a paranoid fantasy about male boundary-dissolution; Chandak Sengoopta's articles in *History of Science* and *Central European History* in the 1990s map the same terrain from the history of science side. What unifies this literature is the refusal to extract a usable Weininger. He is studied as a case, not followed as a thinker.

The one place where that cleaner refusal becomes complicated is Wittgenstein studies. Ludwig Wittgenstein named Weininger in his 1931 list of influences in *Culture and Value*, and recommended *Geschlecht und Charakter* to friends. David G. Stern and Béla Szabados's edited volume *Wittgenstein Reads Weininger* (Cambridge University Press, 2004) is the major treatment; Allan Janik's *Wittgenstein's Vienna Revisited* (Transaction, 2001), building on Janik and Toulmin's earlier *Wittgenstein's Vienna* (1973), argues that Weininger's ethical seriousness, not his gender theory, is what Wittgenstein took. Brian McGuinness's *Wittgenstein: A Life* (1988) and Ray Monk's *Ludwig Wittgenstein: The Duty of Genius* (1990) both note the influence without resolving it. Steven Burns and others in the Stern and Szabados volume push harder, asking whether the "genius" and "character" vocabulary Wittgenstein used can really be separated from the misogyny and antisemitism it carried in its source text. The honest reading of that literature is that the field has not resolved the question and has mostly declined to let it damage Wittgenstein's standing.

Literary modernism is the other domain where Weininger is still actively cited. Marilyn Reizbaum's *James Joyce's Judaic Other* (Stanford, 1999) traces Weininger's presence in *Ulysses*, particularly in the construction of Leopold Bloom as "feminine Jew"; Marilyn Reizbaum and others have shown that Joyce owned a copy and worked against it. David S. Luft's *Robert Musil and the Crisis of European Culture, 1880 to 1942* (California, 1980), and his later *Eros and Inwardness in Vienna: Weininger, Musil, Doderer* (Chicago, 2003), make Weininger constitutive for Musil's *The Man Without Qualities*. Ritchie Robertson's *Kafka: Judaism, Politics, and Literature* (Oxford, 1985) reads Kafka's self-conception partly through Weininger's Jewish self-hatred schema. Beno Weiss's work on Italo Svevo traces the same lines in *La coscienza di Zeno*. In all these cases Weininger is the intellectual atmosphere the writer had to metabolize, not a thinker they endorsed.

## Legal and institutional (minimal)

Weininger has no meaningful contemporary legal or institutional footprint. He is not cited in family-law, sex-discrimination, or educational-policy contexts in any Western jurisdiction. The closest historical residue is the use of Weininger-adjacent sex-difference arguments in early twentieth century debates about women's suffrage and professional entry, all of which are dead letters. He is a scholarly object, not a policy one.

## Clinical and medical (brief)

Weininger's live clinical influence is likewise zero. Historically, he was a participant in the first generation of sexology: Magnus Hirschfeld engaged and rebutted him, Havelock Ellis cited him, and the Fliess plagiarism dispute over bisexuality embedded Weininger in early psychoanalytic prehistory (Sengoopta 2000; Peter Heller, "A Quarrel over Bisexuality," in Berkley and Edinger, eds., *The Turn of the Century*, 1981). Contemporary sexology, gender medicine, and psychiatric nosology do not cite him. Where his bisexuality construct survives, it survives through Freud, who absorbed and transformed it, not through Weininger directly.

## Pop culture and political rhetoric

The politically consequential afterlife is on the right. George L. Mosse's *Nationalism and Sexuality: Respectability and Abnormal Sexuality in Modern Europe* (Howard Fertig, 1985) and *The Image of Man: The Creation of Modern Masculinity* (Oxford, 1996) document how Weininger's schema of virile spirit against feminine and Jewish dissolution was absorbed into Nazi sex theory; Klaus Theweleit's *Male Fantasies* (two volumes, 1977 to 1978; English 1987 to 1989) shows the same structure in the Freikorps imaginary. Andrew Hewitt's *Political Inversions: Homosexuality, Fascism, and the Modernist Imaginary* (Stanford, 1996) tracks the specific uptake of Weininger in proto-fascist and fascist aesthetic theory. Hitler's reported remark that Weininger was "the only decent Jew" because he killed himself is, as Sengoopta notes, the cleanest summary of how the right received him.

The contemporary manosphere and incel ecosystem is where Weininger resurfaces as a living citation, though at low volume. Angela Nagle's *Kill All Normies* (Zero Books, 2017) and Debbie Ging's "Alphas, Betas, and Incels: Theorizing the Masculinities of the Manosphere" (*Men and Masculinities* 22:4, 2019) note that self-styled philosophical wings of the online anti-feminist right cite Weininger alongside Schopenhauer, Nietzsche (badly read), and Evola as "scientific" or "philosophical" backing for sex essentialism. The Weininger tropes that travel cleanest are the ones most structurally compatible with red-pill discourse: man as active principle and woman as mere sexuality, a hierarchy of "higher" and "lower" humanity indexed to rationality, and the framing of female agency as ontological lack. Donna Zuckerberg's *Not All Dead White Men* (Harvard, 2018) and Michael Kimmel's *Angry White Men* (Nation Books, 2013) situate this citation practice inside the broader pattern of canonical name-laundering on the online right.

The structural point, following Sengoopta and Mosse, is that Weininger is a durable case study in how the vocabulary of science and philosophy launders prejudice. The vocabulary is what keeps the work legible a hundred and twenty years later; the prejudice is what it was always carrying.

## Where gender travels with the inheritance

Weininger is the philosopher in this series where the gender question is not a travel problem but an identity problem. Nothing in his system is usable once the misogyny and antisemitism are subtracted, because there is nothing left: the metaphysics of genius, the theory of bisexual constitution, and the ethics of "character" are all built out of the ranking of "M" over "W" and of Aryan over Jew. Harrowitz and Hyams, Sengoopta, and Le Rider all converge on this. Scholars who want to defend an inheritance, as in the Wittgenstein case, end up arguing that Wittgenstein read Weininger *against the grain*, taking the seriousness about ethics and refusing the content; the Stern and Szabados volume is an extended argument about whether that kind of reading is possible and how to evaluate it when it is offered as biographical fact. The unresolved state of that literature is itself the finding: one of the most important analytic philosophers of the twentieth century cited one of the most openly misogynist antisemitic writers of his time as formative, and the profession has largely proceeded as if this did not need to be metabolized.

The second place gender travels is the contemporary incel and red-pill recapitulation of Weininger-shaped claims, most of them made without any awareness of the source. The active-male, merely-sexual-female schema, the "higher" versus "lower" humanity frame, and the treatment of female desire as a threat to male self-possession are all present in the 1903 text and in 2020s manosphere discourse. That is not a claim of direct lineage in most cases; it is a claim that the same structural moves keep reappearing once you take sex to be metaphysically prior to personhood. Weininger matters today mostly as the cleanest historical specimen of what that move looks like when a young philosopher, at the height of scientific prestige, writes it down in full.
