# Kant today

Immanuel Kant (1724-1804) is not only a canonical figure studied in seminar rooms. His conceptual apparatus, above all the idea that each rational agent is an end in itself and the thought that valid moral and political principles must be ones each agent could will for all, furnishes the working vocabulary of several of the dominant normative systems of contemporary life. This companion review surveys that contemporary influence across four domains: academic philosophy, legal and institutional practice, clinical and medical ethics, and popular and political rhetoric. It then flags the points where the gendered assumptions discussed in the companion file on Kant's sexism travel with the inheritance. The survey is descriptive, not evaluative. The goal is to establish how wide the footprint is and where the live questions lie.

## Academic and intellectual

Kantian ethics is arguably the dominant research program in contemporary analytic moral and political philosophy, and Kant is among the most-taught philosophers in Anglo-American ethics curricula. The revival began with John Rawls's *A Theory of Justice* (1971), whose section 40, "The Kantian Interpretation of Justice as Fairness," recast the original position as a procedural model of Kantian autonomy, in which free and equal rational beings legislate principles of justice under conditions that mirror the categorical imperative (Rawls 1971, sec. 40). Rawls was careful to say his theory was not an interpretation of Kant but an attempt to recover the "real force" of the Kantian project on a less metaphysical footing. That move set the template for a generation of work.

Christine Korsgaard's *The Sources of Normativity* (1996), originally the 1992 Tanner Lectures at Cambridge, argues that the Kantian account of normativity as springing from our own autonomy is a synthesis of the major alternative traditions and is the strongest contender for explaining why moral claims bind us (Korsgaard 1996). Her *Self-Constitution* (2009) extends the view, treating agency itself as an achievement of rational self-legislation. Onora O'Neill's *Constructions of Reason* (1989) and *Towards Justice and Virtue* (1996) develop a constructivist reading of Kantian practical reason oriented around what policies all affected agents could in principle share, applied to problems of hunger, trust, and global justice (O'Neill 1989, 1996). Allen Wood's *Kantian Ethics* (2008) defends a more text-historical Kantianism, arguing that the Formula of Humanity, rather than the Formula of Universal Law, is the substantive heart of Kant's ethical theory and grounds a wide range of duties (Wood 2008). Thomas E. Hill Jr.'s *Dignity and Practical Reason in Kant's Moral Theory* (1992) and *Respect, Pluralism, and Justice* (2000), together with Barbara Herman's *The Practice of Moral Judgment* (1993) and Tamar Schapiro's work on moral character and adolescence, round out a research program that treats Kantian resources as the default tools for addressing contemporary normative questions.

What matters for the present review is less the internal disputes than the scale of the program. Dissertations, handbooks, and journal symposia on Kantian ethics and political philosophy are produced continuously, and a professional philosopher working on normative theory is expected to know the Kantian literature in a way that has no parallel for, say, Hegel or Schopenhauer.

## Legal and institutional

The juridical inheritance is arguably even larger. The core formulation of Article 1 of the German *Grundgesetz*, "Human dignity shall be inviolable," has been interpreted by the Federal Constitutional Court through what is called the *Objektformel*, the object formula, which holds that no person may be treated merely as an object of state action. Commentators consistently trace this formula to Kant's prohibition on treating humanity merely as a means (Eberle 2012; Enders 2010). The influence is not a matter of surface citation but of doctrinal architecture: a substantial body of German constitutional case law, including decisions on privacy, aviation security, and life-prolonging treatment, has been decided on the object formula.

At the international level, Kantian dignity is the standard genealogy invoked for the UN Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948), although historians caution that the actual drafting process drew on multiple and partly competing traditions, including Catholic personalism and earlier Latin American constitutional texts (Moyn 2010). Even granting the genealogical complexity, the modern human rights vocabulary of inherent dignity, universal and equal rights, and persons as ends in themselves is unmistakably Kantian in idiom.

In constitutional theory, Ronald Dworkin's *Justice for Hedgehogs* (2011) makes the Kantian inheritance explicit. Dworkin's "Kant's principle" holds that proper self-respect entails parallel respect for the lives of all human beings, and he builds his account of legal interpretation and political morality on two principles of dignity, self-respect and authenticity (Dworkin 2011). In international political theory, Seyla Benhabib's *Another Cosmopolitanism* (2006) takes Kant's doctrine of cosmopolitan right and his essay "Perpetual Peace" as her starting point for an account of hospitality, sovereignty, and what she calls democratic iterations, arguing that Kantian cosmopolitan norms are now operative in the supranational human rights order (Benhabib 2006). Kantian cosmopolitanism is the shared idiom for much post-1989 work in international relations theory, including contributions from Habermas, Kleingeld, and Held.

## Clinical and medical

The most consequential institutional diffusion of Kantian vocabulary is probably into biomedicine. Tom L. Beauchamp and James F. Childress's *Principles of Biomedical Ethics*, first published in 1979 and now in its eighth edition, codified the now-standard four-principle framework of autonomy, nonmaleficence, beneficence, and justice. The principle of respect for autonomy is explicitly and repeatedly grounded by Beauchamp and Childress in Kant, as is the dignitarian framing of personhood (Beauchamp and Childress 2019). In practice this has meant that the doctrine of informed consent, the institutional architecture of Institutional Review Boards, the confidentiality and privacy norms codified in HIPAA in the United States and in data-protection regimes elsewhere, and much of the clinical-ethics-committee case literature, all speak a recognizably Kantian language.

O'Neill's *Autonomy and Trust in Bioethics* (2002) sharpens that picture from within the tradition, arguing that contemporary bioethics has flattened Kantian autonomy into a thin patient-preference concept divorced from the original idea of self-legislation according to principles one could will for all. Her worry is that the clinical inheritance has kept the Kantian word "autonomy" but lost the Kantian thought. Debates about paternalism in medicine, public health, and psychiatry continue to be conducted largely in Kantian terms, with arguments about when overriding a patient's expressed preferences is compatible with respect for that patient as an end in herself.

The disability rights literature contains some of the sharpest contemporary challenges to this framework. Eva Feder Kittay's *Love's Labor* (1999), written in part from her experience as the mother of a daughter with profound cognitive disabilities, argues that a conception of personhood built around rational autonomy systematically under-theorizes dependency and the unpaid, gendered care work that sustains it (Kittay 1999). Martha Nussbaum's capabilities approach, developed in *Frontiers of Justice* (2006), similarly presses the point that Kantian and contractarian frameworks struggle to include people with severe cognitive impairments as full subjects of justice rather than beneficiaries of charity. Neither Kittay nor Nussbaum concludes that the Kantian framework must be jettisoned; both press for modification. The debate is live, and it matters in disability policy, long-term-care financing, and the design of supported-decision-making regimes.

## Pop culture and political rhetoric

The categorical imperative has entered ordinary moral language. "What if everyone did that?" is a schoolyard and dinner-table formulation of Kant's universalizability test, and appeals to treating people as ends in themselves rather than as mere means appear regularly in editorial writing, in workplace ethics training, and in the rhetoric of liberal democratic politics. Kantian idioms of dignity, rights, and universal human worth saturate the rhetorical repertoire of mainstream liberal politicians in both Europe and North America.

The uses are not all on the left. Jordan Peterson has made sustained if unsystematic appeals to Kant in his lectures and writings, typically positioning his "rules for life" as updated categorical imperatives and citing Kant alongside Kierkegaard in support of a deontological moral seriousness (Peterson 2018). Alt-right writers including Richard Spencer have attempted Kantian appropriations, usually trading on Kant's eighteenth-century remarks on race and national character rather than on his moral theory. These appropriations have been contested from within academic Kantianism. Charles Mills's "Black Radical Kantianism" (2018) and his earlier *The Racial Contract* (1997) argue that any contemporary Kantianism has to reckon with the actual historical Kant's racism, and that the normative force of the framework has been secured only by the work of excluded groups who demanded inclusion on its own terms (Mills 1997, 2018). The broader academic canon-reckoning conversation, including symposia in *Kantian Review* and *Hypatia* and Kleingeld's 2019 essay "On Dealing with Kant's Sexism and Racism," is the disciplinary counterpart of the public argument about whom to teach and how.

## Where gender travels with the inheritance

Several points of contact connect this contemporary influence back to the questions of gendered thought that organize the essay.

First, feminist critique of Kantian autonomy as implicitly masculine is now a mature body of scholarship. Kittay's *Love's Labor* (1999) argues that the idealized autonomous subject of liberal theory abstracts from the conditions of dependency under which all humans begin and many end, and that the labor of supplying those conditions is gendered female in both paid and unpaid form (Kittay 1999). Sarah Clark Miller's *The Ethics of Need* (2012) is explicitly hybrid, arguing that Kantian beneficence supplies the formal ground of obligation to meet others' needs but that feminist care ethics supplies the content and method, because the Kantian framework on its own is not equipped to specify what dignifying care looks like (Miller 2012). These are critiques from within the normative neighborhood, not repudiations.

Second, Carol Hay's *Kantianism, Liberalism, and Feminism* (2013) makes the reconstructive case. Hay argues that a Kantian duty to respect one's own rational nature generates a prima facie duty on the oppressed to resist their own oppression, and that the liberal framework of autonomy is indispensable for, rather than opposed to, feminist aims. Her treatment of stereotype threat and adaptive preferences is an attempt to show that the harms contemporary feminism most wants to name are precisely the harms a properly formulated Kantianism can name (Hay 2013). The book was recognized with the APA's Kavka Prize in political philosophy in 2015.

Third, the disciplinary pattern itself is worth noting. Analytic ethics and political philosophy, in which Kantian frameworks dominate, are among the least gender-balanced subfields in philosophy, which is itself among the least gender-balanced fields in the humanities. The continued dominance of Kantian frameworks in fields with low female representation is not by itself evidence of a causal link, but it is part of the pattern this essay is tracing. Benhabib, O'Neill, Korsgaard, Herman, Schapiro, Hay, Varden, Kittay, Nussbaum, and Miller are all significant women working on Kant or through Kantian materials, and their work is disproportionately the work that presses the tradition on questions of embodiment, dependency, care, and gender.

Fourth, the bioethical inheritance is the place where the gendered stakes are most concrete. The patient whose autonomy must be respected, the research subject whose informed consent must be secured, the cognitively impaired person whose personhood is in legal question, and the family member whose unpaid care work sustains the clinical encounter are all situated differently by gender in most contemporary health systems. The Kantian vocabulary the system speaks does not, on most feminist readings, correctly track those differences.
