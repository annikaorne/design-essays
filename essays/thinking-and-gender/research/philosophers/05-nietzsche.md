# Nietzsche

Friedrich Nietzsche (1844-1900) is the most contested case in feminist philosophy. No major figure has produced both more virulent passages on women and more sustained, ambivalent feminist engagement. This review maps what he actually wrote, then sets the three principal feminist responses (irredeemable, rhetorical, affirmative) alongside one another, before turning to contemporary reception in alt-right and "manosphere" milieus and the question of whether the misogyny is detachable from the inheritance.

## Primary texts and what he actually said

The most concentrated misogynist material is in *Beyond Good and Evil* (1886), Part VII, "Our Virtues," sections 231 to 239. Section 231 opens with the disclaimer that what follows are "only, of course, my truths," which Nietzsche himself italicizes. Section 232 attacks women who seek "enlightenment" about themselves, claiming such self-knowledge ruins the feminine instinct. Section 233 dismisses Madame Roland, Madame de Stael, and George Sand as "three comical women." Section 238 announces that "to be mistaken on the fundamental problem of 'man and woman'" is a sign of shallowness, and that the right thinker must grasp the "abysmal antagonism" between the sexes. Section 239 ends with the famous "seven little maxims" or "seven little sayings" about women, including the lines that woman's "first and last profession" is "to bear strong children" and that emancipation represents the "deterioration" of the female type.

In *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* (1883-85), Part I, "On Little Old and Young Women," Zarathustra recounts being given counsel by an "old little woman." She asks him to share what he knows of woman, and after he speaks she rewards him with "a little truth": "Thou goest to women? Do not forget thy whip!" The narrative frame matters and is regularly underplayed in popular citation: the line is given to Zarathustra by an old woman, and Zarathustra does not himself utter the maxim. The famous 1882 photograph of Lou Salome holding a whip over Nietzsche and Paul Ree adds a biographical inversion that several commentators (Diethe, Kofman) treat as relevant.

*The Gay Science* (1882), sections 68 through 72, contains epigrams on "women and their action at a distance," shame, mothers, and pregnancy. Section 339, "Vita femina," treats truth itself as a veiled woman whose beauty is glimpsed only at rare moments. The figure of truth as woman is announced earlier in the Preface to the second edition (1886): "Supposing truth is a woman, what then?" This trope is reprised at the opening of *Beyond Good and Evil*, where dogmatic philosophers are mocked as awkward suitors who failed to win her.

*Human, All Too Human* (1878), sections 377 through 437 ("Woman and Child"), is the longest single block on the subject, written in the more aphoristic, "free spirit" period. The tone is colder and more sociological than the later texts; many aphorisms read as conventional nineteenth-century European bourgeois generalization (women as actresses, as instruments of reproduction, as incapable of friendship in the male sense).

In *Ecce Homo* (written 1888, published 1908), "Why I Write Such Good Books" section 5, Nietzsche claims to "know" women: "May I here venture the surmise that I know women?" He then describes this knowledge as part of his "Dionysian dowry" and asserts that woman is "incalculably more evil than man." The same section attacks emancipation as an attempt by botched women to revenge themselves on the well-formed.

The private correspondence complicates rather than softens the picture. Nietzsche's relationships with his mother Franziska and sister Elisabeth were freighted; the late letters to Elisabeth are alternately tender and contemptuous, and the famous late note refers to "mother and sister" as belonging to the "abysmal" against him. After Lou Salome rejected his proposal in 1882, his published writing on women coarsens noticeably (Diethe 1996; Kaufmann 1974). Elisabeth's later editorial control over the *Nachlass* and her marriage to the antisemite Bernhard Forster shaped the early reception in ways Nietzsche himself opposed in print.

## The structural / irredeemable reading

Kelly Oliver's *Womanizing Nietzsche* (1995) is the most sustained argument that the misogyny is structural rather than ornamental. Oliver reads the maternal body as the disavowed ground of Nietzsche's metaphors of pregnancy, birth, self-overcoming, and the eternal return. The philosopher who claims the productive, generative position must, on her reading, repeatedly expel and devalue actual women in order to occupy a position of birth that is not theirs. The "woman as truth" tropes are not, for Oliver, a destabilizing performance but a colonization: woman becomes a metaphor precisely so that women can be excluded from the scene of philosophy.

Ofelia Schutte's *Beyond Nihilism: Nietzsche Without Masks* (1984) reads the misogyny as continuous with an authoritarian and hierarchical strain in the will to power. Schutte argues that Nietzsche's contempt for the "herd," for democratic equality, and for women is one position, not three; and that attempts to retrieve his philosophy by bracketing the politics underestimate how tightly the elements are bound.

Luce Irigaray's *Marine Lover of Friedrich Nietzsche* (French 1980; English 1991) addresses Nietzsche in the second person from the position of a feminine voice he never acknowledged. Irigaray's charge is ontological: Nietzsche's philosophy of becoming, flux, and self-overcoming still depends on the suppression of the feminine element (water, the maternal, the elemental other) as the unspeakable ground from which the male philosopher launches himself. *Marine Lover* is not a refutation in the analytic sense; it is a sustained performance of what Nietzsche's text excludes.

Adjacent to these are Tina Chanter, Rosalyn Diprose, and (in different keys) Jean Graybeal, all of whom argue that the maternal and the feminine function in Nietzsche as the constitutive outside of his project rather than as detachable rhetorical decoration.

## The rhetorical / "woman as truth" reading

Jacques Derrida's *Spurs: Nietzsche's Styles* (*Eperons*, 1978; English 1979) is the inaugural text of this camp. Derrida fastens on the figure of woman in Nietzsche as the figure of the undecidable. Because Nietzsche says contradictory things about women (woman as truth, woman as untruth, woman as the philosopher's distance from truth), Derrida argues that "there is no such thing as the truth of woman" in Nietzsche, and that woman names the very impossibility of stable predication. On this reading, Nietzsche's misogynist propositions and his philo-feminine propositions are equally undermined by the trope, and the trope is what matters philosophically.

Sarah Kofman's *Nietzsche et la metaphore* (1972) and *Le respect des femmes* (1982; portions translated as "Baubo: Theological Perversion and Fetishism") and her later essays collected as *The Enigma of Woman* read Nietzsche as a writer whose misogynist surface conceals a more complex affirmation of an "affirmative" woman against the "reactive" woman of resentment. Kofman's Baubo essay treats the figure of the lifted skirt in *The Gay Science* preface as an affirmation of life as appearance, not as obscenity. Kofman is more sympathetic to Nietzsche than Irigaray and more attentive to the textual specificity than Oliver.

Alan D. Schrift (in *Nietzsche's French Legacy*, 1995, and various essays in the Burgard volume) and David Farrell Krell (*Postponements: Woman, Sensuality, and Death in Nietzsche*, 1986) extend the Derridean line. Krell focuses on the deferred, postponed quality of the feminine in Nietzsche, the way woman is always announced and never arrived at. Schrift situates the readings in the French uptake of Nietzsche by Deleuze, Foucault, and the post-structuralist generation.

The strength of this camp is textual fidelity to the contradictions; the standard objection from the structural camp (Oliver makes it sharply) is that "woman as undecidable trope" is exactly the move that lets male philosophers continue not to read women.

## The affirmative feminist reading

Maudemarie Clark's "Nietzsche's Misogyny" (in *International Studies in Philosophy*, 1994; revised in the Oliver and Pearsall volume, 1998) is the most cited analytic-feminist position. Clark concedes that the content of the misogynist passages is offensive and biographically driven (she emphasizes the Salome rejection) but argues that the substantive philosophy (perspectivism, will to power, the critique of morality, eternal return) does not depend on any claim about women and survives the excision of the misogynist material. Clark treats the seven maxims as embarrassing rather than as load-bearing.

Kathleen M. Higgins (*Comic Relief: Nietzsche's Gay Science*, 2000, and earlier essays) emphasizes the playful, ironic, self-undermining tone of much of the misogynist material and reads many passages as parody of nineteenth-century male posturing. Ellen Kennedy and Susan Mendus's edited *Women in Western Political Thought* (1987) places Nietzsche in a tradition where the misogyny is conventional and the philosophy distinctive.

Carol Diethe's *Nietzsche's Women: Beyond the Whip* (1996) is the most thorough biographical and contextual study. Diethe documents Nietzsche's actual relationships with educated women of his circle (Malwida von Meysenbug, Lou Salome, Cosima Wagner, Helene Druskowitz, Meta von Salis) and shows that his behavior toward individual women was often supportive of their intellectual ambitions in ways that the published aphorisms flatly contradict. Diethe also tracks Elisabeth Forster-Nietzsche's role in shaping the posthumous image. Ruth Abbey's *Nietzsche's Middle Period* (2000) makes a related case from the *Human, All Too Human* texts: the middle Nietzsche is markedly less misogynist than the late Nietzsche, which suggests the late misogyny is biographical rather than philosophical bedrock.

The two principal edited collections that present the split are Peter J. Burgard, ed., *Nietzsche and the Feminine* (1994), and Kelly Oliver and Marilyn Pearsall, eds., *Feminist Interpretations of Friedrich Nietzsche* (1998). Paul Patton, ed., *Nietzsche, Feminism and Political Theory* (1993) is the third standard volume. Reading these three together is the standard graduate introduction to the controversy.

## Does the philosophy depend on the misogyny?

The three camps answer this question differently and the answers track their broader commitments. Clark and the affirmative camp say no: perspectivism is a thesis about knowledge, will to power is a thesis about valuation and force, eternal return is an existential test, genealogy is a method. None require gendered premises. Oliver and the structural camp say yes, at least in part: the metaphors of pregnancy and birth that organize the productive, life-affirming pole of Nietzsche's thought trade on the appropriation of female generativity, and removing them removes a great deal of his rhetoric of self-overcoming. The Derridean camp says the question is malformed: the misogynist text is part of the destabilizing performance, and excising it would be a different kind of dishonesty.

A reasonable observation, made in different forms by Patton (1993) and by Babette Babich (*Nietzsche's Philosophy of Science*, 1994), is that the philosophy of science and epistemology in Nietzsche is more separable from the gender material than the philosophy of culture, art, and the body, where the metaphors are denser and more entangled.

## Contemporary manosphere reception

Nietzsche has been heavily and selectively cited in alt-right, "red pill," and adjacent online milieus since the mid-2010s. Ronald Beiner's *Dangerous Minds: Nietzsche, Heidegger, and the Return of the Far Right* (2018) is the principal scholarly treatment. Beiner argues that the alt-right reception is not a simple distortion: Nietzsche's anti-egalitarianism, his contempt for democratic and Christian morality, and his rhetoric of hierarchy and mastery are genuinely there in the texts, and the fact that Richard Spencer and others cite him is not purely a misreading. Beiner's position is that the academic left has under-attended to the political danger of these elements precisely because the postmodern reception (Foucault, Deleuze) made Nietzsche safe for left-wing critique.

Angela Nagle's *Kill All Normies* (2017) documents the circulation of Nietzschean motifs in the 4chan, manosphere, and alt-right ecosystem of the period, focusing more on cultural transmission than on textual fidelity. Nagle treats Nietzsche less as a source than as a set of rhetorical resources (master/slave morality, the last man, the herd) that travel easily into anti-feminist and anti-progressive discourse.

Shadia Drury's *Alexandre Kojeve* (1994) and *Leo Strauss and the American Right* (1997), and her earlier work on the Straussian reception, supply the longer genealogy: a strain of twentieth-century right-wing political philosophy already metabolized Nietzsche in an anti-democratic, anti-feminist register, and the present-day online reception inherits this filter as much as it inherits Nietzsche himself.

The standard scholarly response (Bull 2011, *Anti-Nietzsche*; Losurdo 2002, *Nietzsche, il ribelle aristocratico*) is that Nietzsche genuinely is an aristocratic radical, that the rehabilitation as a proto-postmodern liberal is partial, and that the manosphere reception is reading something real even where it strips context.

## Institutional and intellectual inheritance

Nietzsche is the single most influential nineteenth-century source for twentieth-century continental philosophy. Genealogy as a critical method (Foucault, *Discipline and Punish*, 1975; *History of Sexuality* vol. 1, 1976) is explicitly Nietzschean. Perspectivism feeds directly into the postmodern critique of universal reason. The "death of God" announced in *The Gay Science* section 125 organizes a great deal of twentieth-century theology and post-theology. Will to power and the critique of morality run through Deleuze (*Nietzsche and Philosophy*, 1962), Bataille, Klossowski, and the French reception that returns to American theory through Derrida and Foucault.

Whether the misogyny travels with the inheritance is a live question. Foucault's reception of Nietzsche almost entirely brackets the gender material, and feminist Foucauldians (Sandra Bartky, Susan Bordo, Judith Butler) have used Foucauldian Nietzscheanism for explicitly feminist purposes without obvious distortion. This is a piece of evidence for the affirmative camp: the methods do appear to be portable. On the other hand, the manosphere reception is also part of the inheritance, and it carries the gender material forward intact. The honest description is that Nietzsche's afterlife has bifurcated, and both branches are continuous with something in the texts.

## Live debates

Three questions remain unsettled in the current literature.

First, the biographical-versus-philosophical question. Diethe and Clark treat the late misogyny as substantially driven by the Salome rejection and the deteriorating relationships with his mother and sister; Oliver and Schutte treat this framing as itself a form of apologetics. The textual evidence allows both readings.

Second, the question of whether the "woman as truth" trope is liberating or colonizing. Derrida and Kofman read it as destabilizing the very category of philosophical truth; Irigaray and Oliver read it as a fresh appropriation of the feminine for male philosophical use. The disagreement is methodological as much as textual.

Third, the question of political inheritance. The post-2016 reassessment (Beiner, Bull, Losurdo) has put pressure on the postwar liberal-postmodern Nietzsche associated with Walter Kaufmann's translations and with the French reception. Whether this reassessment will reshape the feminist debate, by making the "structural" reading look more politically prescient, is an open question.
