# Weininger

Otto Weininger (1880-1903) is the limit case for any debate about whether the misogyny of a philosophical system can be separated from its arguments. His one published book, *Geschlecht und Charakter: Eine prinzipielle Untersuchung* (*Sex and Character: A Principled Investigation*), appeared in May 1903; he shot himself in the room where Beethoven had died on October 4, 1903, at the age of twenty-three. The book had been his rewritten doctoral dissertation, defended at the University of Vienna under Friedrich Jodl and Laurenz Mullner the previous summer (Sengoopta 2000, 12-19). Within a decade of his death the book had run through more than twenty German editions, been translated into English (1906), French, Italian, Polish, Russian, Hungarian, Swedish, and Japanese, and been read or cited approvingly by Wittgenstein, Karl Kraus, August Strindberg, James Joyce, Italo Svevo, Franz Kafka, Robert Musil, D. H. Lawrence, and, eventually and toxically, by ideologues of the Nazi state (Le Rider 1993, 3-7; Harrowitz and Hyams 1995, 3-20). The scholarship's task has been to explain how a book this systematically hateful became a cultural phenomenon, what intellectual milieu produced it, and what, if anything, contemporary readers can or should do with it.

## Primary text and what he actually said

*Sex and Character* is divided into two parts. Part I, "Sexual Diversity," presents an empirical-biological framework. Weininger asserts that all living matter is bisexual: every cell contains both masculine ("M") and feminine ("W") "plasms" or *idioplasms*, in varying proportions. No actual human is purely M or purely W; each is a mixture. Sexual attraction, on his account, follows a law of complementarity: a person who is, say, three-quarters M is maximally attracted to a person who is three-quarters W (Weininger [1903] 1906, 28-37; Sengoopta 2000, 56-72). Part I presents itself as a contribution to the new sciences of sex and embryology, citing Steinach, Halban, and the chromosomal cytology of the 1890s, and intersecting with the bisexuality theories of Wilhelm Fliess, which Sigmund Freud was simultaneously borrowing (Sulloway 1979, 184-194; Sengoopta 2000, 47-54).

Part II, "The Sexual Types," abandons the empirical pose and constructs ideal types. The "absolute woman" (W) is figured as nothing but sexuality: she has no soul, no ego, no relation to logic or to the moral law, no genuine memory, no continuity of self, no capacity for genius, no relation to truth as such. She exists as either "Mother" or "Prostitute," and these are not social roles but metaphysical types differing in the direction of their sexual orientation (Weininger [1903] 1906, 186-249). The "absolute man" (M), by contrast, is the bearer of the Kantian transcendental ego, of logic, of ethics, and of the possibility of genius. Genius, for Weininger, is the maximization of the masculine principle: it is universal apperception, the capacity to hold all of consciousness in view at once, and is therefore by definition unavailable to W (Weininger [1903] 1906, 110-138; Stern 1995).

The book then makes two further moves that have shaped its reception. First, in chapter 13, Weininger argues that the "Jew" stands to the "Aryan" as W stands to M. Jewishness, for Weininger (himself Jewish-born and baptized into Protestantism the day he received his doctorate), is a "tendency of mind" that any person of any descent can have, characterized by lack of soul, lack of genius, materialism, and a feminine relation to the world (Weininger [1903] 1906, 301-330; Le Rider 1993, 175-208; Gilman 1986, 244-250). Second, in the closing chapter, Weininger draws an ethical conclusion: because sexuality is the medium of W and the corruption of M, the ethical task of the man is sexual abstinence and the eventual extinction of the species. The book ends in a register of explicit ascetic mysticism (Weininger [1903] 1906, 343-347; Sengoopta 2000, 97-105).

The structural argument matters because it is what makes Weininger a singular case. The misogyny is not a passage one can excise. The book is a deductive structure whose axiom is that femininity equals nullity and whose theorems are the rest of his ethics, epistemology, and metaphysics. As Sengoopta puts it, *Sex and Character* is "a treatise on the unity of self, sex, and reason" in which "the denial of selfhood to woman" is the "load-bearing premise" (Sengoopta 2000, 6, 117). Janik makes the same point in *Wittgenstein's Vienna*: Weininger's book is best read as a Kantian ethical treatise whose subject is sex because, for Weininger, sex is the place where the Kantian ego is constituted or destroyed (Janik and Toulmin 1973, 71-74; Janik 1985).

## Context: fin-de-siecle Vienna

The historiography of Weininger is dominated by the question of context. Read out of context, *Sex and Character* looks like the rantings of a uniquely disturbed twenty-three-year-old; read in context, it looks like a particularly extreme but recognizable distillation of the intellectual currents of fin-de-siecle Vienna. Three contextualizing accounts are central.

Allan Janik and Stephen Toulmin's *Wittgenstein's Vienna* (1973) made the case that Vienna around 1900 was a coherent intellectual milieu, organized around the crisis of liberal Kultur, the assimilated Jewish bourgeoisie, the language scepticism of Mauthner and Kraus, and a generalized search for ethical foundations after the apparent collapse of Habsburg liberalism. Weininger, on their reading, belongs alongside Wittgenstein, Kraus, Loos, Schoenberg, and Hofmannsthal as a participant in a shared problem: the relation of ethics to language and form (Janik and Toulmin 1973, 67-91).

Jacques Le Rider's *Le cas Otto Weininger: Racines de l'antifeminisme et de l'antisemitisme* (1982; English translation 1993) and his subsequent *Modernity and Crises of Identity* (1993) extended the analysis. Le Rider argues that Weininger is the symptom of a generalized crisis of masculine identity in late-Habsburg central Europe. The decline of the liberal political project, the rise of mass politics including Karl Lueger's antisemitic Christian Social movement, the entry of women into universities and the professions, the new sexology, and the visibility of Jewish assimilation produced, in a particular cohort of young, educated, often Jewish-born men, a panic about the dissolution of stable selfhood. Weininger's book, on Le Rider's reading, takes that panic and constructs a metaphysical defence: the self is masculine, the masculine is Aryan, and both are under siege from W and the Jew (Le Rider 1993, 67-92, 175-208).

Chandak Sengoopta's *Otto Weininger: Sex, Science, and Self in Imperial Vienna* (2000) is the most thorough English-language scholarly monograph and the standard reference. Sengoopta reconstructs the scientific sources Weininger drew on (Steinach's hormone research, Halban's gonadal grafting, Fliess's bisexuality, Krafft-Ebing's *Psychopathia Sexualis*, the Mendelian rediscovery) and the philosophical sources (Kant's transcendental idealism, Schopenhauer's metaphysics of will, Wagner's redemption mythology, Mach's sensationalism, which Weininger violently rejected). His core argument is that Weininger's project is best understood as an attempt to rescue the Kantian moral subject from the dissolution that Mach's psychology and the new sexology threatened to inflict on it; the price of the rescue is the consignment of half of humanity to nullity (Sengoopta 2000, 31-72, 117-148). Sengoopta is also sharp on the falsehood of the popular image of Weininger as a "self-hating Jew" pure and simple: Weininger's antisemitism is real but is neither original nor confined to Jewish-born thinkers, and his misogyny is structurally deeper than his antisemitism in the architecture of the book (Sengoopta 2000, 99-135; see also Beller 1989, 217-237).

Steven Beller's *Vienna and the Jews 1867-1938* (1989) provides the broader social-historical frame: the disproportionately Jewish composition of Viennese liberal high culture, the fragility of assimilation, and the cultural conditions under which a thinker like Weininger could simultaneously be a product of that milieu and a participant in its undoing. Beller's later *Antisemitism: A Very Short Introduction* (2007) places Weininger within the wider European antisemitic discourse of the period.

Other useful contextualizations include Carl Schorske's *Fin-de-Siecle Vienna* (1980), which provides the broader cultural-political backdrop without focusing on Weininger; William Johnston's *The Austrian Mind* (1972), which gives an early synthetic treatment; and Sander Gilman's *Jewish Self-Hatred* (1986) and *The Jew's Body* (1991), which trace the specific iconography of the feminized male Jew that Weininger inherited and radicalized.

## Influence and reception

The reception literature is now substantial and the basic story is well established. *Sex and Character* was widely read across European intellectual life in the decade after Weininger's suicide, in part because the suicide itself, in Beethoven's death-room, gave the book a charge of authenticity that critics like Karl Kraus actively cultivated.

Wittgenstein listed Weininger among his major influences in a 1931 notebook entry, alongside Boltzmann, Hertz, Schopenhauer, Frege, Russell, Kraus, Loos, Spengler, and Sraffa (Wittgenstein 1980, 19; McGuinness 1988, 39-43). The nature of the influence has been debated. Janik (1985, 1995) argues that what Wittgenstein took from Weininger was not the misogyny but a certain conception of the relation between ethics and the limits of meaningful language, and the model of philosophy as ethical task. Stern (2001) and Monk (1990, 19-25) take similar positions, with Monk noting that Wittgenstein recommended the book to G. E. Moore with the warning that "you have to negate the whole book to get an important truth" (Monk 1990, 313). Brian McGuinness's biography (1988) and Ray Monk's (1990) both argue that Wittgenstein's lifelong asceticism, his conception of genius as ethical achievement, and his agonized relation to his own Jewish ancestry all bear Weininger's imprint, even where Wittgenstein resisted Weininger's conclusions.

Karl Kraus, the editor of *Die Fackel*, was Weininger's most influential public defender. Kraus's posthumous championing of the book, combined with his own brand of misogynistic and self-critical Jewish satire, did much to keep Weininger in circulation in German-language letters until 1933 (Timms 1986, 75-83; Le Rider 1993, 7-12). Edward Timms's two-volume biography of Kraus is the standard treatment.

August Strindberg wrote to Weininger in July 1903, three months before the suicide, calling *Sex and Character* a book that had "probably solved the most difficult of all problems" (cited in Le Rider 1993, 5-6; Sengoopta 2000, 17). Strindberg's late misogynistic plays show the imprint, although the influence runs alongside Strindberg's own pre-existing convictions.

The literary uptake is well documented. Joyce knew the book and Bloom's characterization in *Ulysses* (1922) borrows from Weininger's typology of the "feminine male Jew," as Marilyn Reizbaum (1999) and others have shown. Italo Svevo's *La coscienza di Zeno* (1923) reads in places as a parodic engagement with Weininger; Beno Weiss (1987) and Saccone (1973) trace the references. Kafka encountered the book in 1903 and there are echoes in his diaries and in *The Castle*; Robertson (1985) and Anderson (1992) develop this. Robert Musil's *The Man Without Qualities* (1930-1942) engages Weiningerian sexual metaphysics critically; Luft (1980) and Hickman (1984) read this thread.

The fascist inheritance is the most contested part of the reception story. Hitler is sometimes quoted, on the authority of Dietrich Eckart, as saying of Weininger that he was "the only decent Jew, who killed himself when he realised he had been pulling the world to pieces" (cited in Hyams 1995, 155-160). The provenance of the quotation is uncertain, but the broader claim that Weininger's biological-typological misogyny and antisemitism prefigure and feed Nazi racial-sexual theory is defended by Mosse (1985), Theweleit (1987 [1977]) in *Male Fantasies*, and, more recently, by Hewitt (1993) in *Political Inversions*. The careful version of the claim, advanced by Hyams and Harrowitz (1995, introduction) and Le Rider (1993), is that Weininger did not directly cause Nazi ideology but provided one of the most intellectually respectable templates for the racialisation and gendering of metaphysical categories that Nazi theorists subsequently exploited.

The single most important reception volume in English is Nancy A. Harrowitz and Barbara Hyams, eds., *Jews and Gender: Responses to Otto Weininger* (1995), which collects essays by Steven Beller, Sander Gilman, Allan Janik, John M. Hoberman, Susan C. Anderson, Robert S. Wistrich, and others. The volume is the standard scholarly entry point. David Abrahamsen's earlier psychobiography *The Mind and Death of a Genius* (1946) is now mostly of historical interest; Leon Botstein's essays on Vienna and modernism (e.g., Botstein 1991) provide musical-cultural context. More recent work includes Maurice Weaver's articles on Weininger and modernist masculinity, Andrew Sartori's reception studies, and ongoing reconsiderations in the *Journal of the History of Ideas* and *Modern Intellectual History*.

The contemporary tail of the reception is its own subject. Weininger circulates today in some "manosphere" and "incel" forums as an authority on the supposed metaphysical unworthiness of women; Nagle (2017) and more recent work on the online radical right note the reappearance of Weiningerian tropes (the woman as pure sexuality without ego, the Jew as feminine corrupter) in twenty-first-century reactionary discourse. The scholarly consensus is that this is a genuine and not merely accidental inheritance, given the structural fit between Weininger's framework and contemporary essentialist sex-difference rhetoric.

## The "sexism is the philosophy" limit case

The Weininger scholarship is unusually direct on the question of separability. The reason is that the book makes the question almost trivial. There is no Weiningerian epistemology, ethics, or metaphysics that is not built from the premise that W is nullity and M is selfhood. To "separate the misogyny from the philosophy" is to delete the philosophy.

Sengoopta states this most plainly: "the architecture of *Sex and Character* is such that the misogyny cannot be lifted off the system because the misogyny is the system" (Sengoopta 2000, 6, 148). Le Rider makes the same point in different language, calling the book a "delirium of pure structure" in which sexual difference is the organising principle of every claim (Le Rider 1993, 92). Janik, who is friendlier to Weininger than most contemporary commentators, argues that the philosophically interesting kernel (a Kantian conception of the self as ethical achievement, a critique of the dissolution of the subject in Machian sensationalism) is real, but that Weininger's own way of presenting it is inseparable from the gendering that he uses to dramatise it (Janik 1985, 1995).

This makes Weininger a useful test case for the broader debate about reading historical philosophers. Critics of "charitable reading" sometimes argue that Kant's, Hegel's, or Rousseau's sexism is excisable because their systems do not formally depend on it. Defenders of the structural reading (Okin 1979; Pateman 1988; Lloyd 1984) argue that the dependence is deeper than it appears. Weininger sits at the limit: the dependence is total and explicit. As Hyams and Harrowitz put it in their introduction, "Weininger forces the reader to see what is usually hidden in the canon: that the construction of the rational subject as masculine is not incidental to the philosophical project but constitutive of it, and that when this construction is made fully explicit it produces a monster" (Harrowitz and Hyams 1995, 19).

The methodological upshot, defended by Sengoopta, Le Rider, and most contributors to the Hyams-Harrowitz volume, is that Weininger should be read as evidence of what the canonical tradition contains in attenuated form, not as an aberration from it. The interest of the case is precisely that the misogyny becomes visible as architecture rather than as decoration.

## Why engage with Weininger at all

Two scholarly positions structure the contemporary debate.

The first, advanced most fully by the Hyams-Harrowitz volume, by Sengoopta, by Le Rider, and by Gilman, is that Weininger is a necessary case study. He shows what happens when the gendered and racialized categories that lurk beneath modern philosophy are stated as axioms and pursued to their conclusions. Reading Weininger is therefore a form of intellectual hygiene: it makes the implicit explicit. It also shows how the language of science and the language of Kantian ethics can be used to launder bigotry into respectability, which is a recurring danger and not only a historical one (Sengoopta 2000, 1-12, 187-194; Harrowitz and Hyams 1995, 1-20).

The second position, less often stated in print but visible in curricular practice, is that Weininger should be excluded from the philosophical conversation altogether: he is bad philosophy, his arguments are not good arguments by any standard, and continued attention to him keeps a noxious text in circulation. Some contributors to the Hyams-Harrowitz volume note that this is the dominant institutional posture (he is rarely taught), and worry that exclusion has the perverse effect of leaving Weininger as the property of his contemporary admirers on the radical right (Hyams 1995, 155-176; Harrowitz 1995, 73-90).

The synthesis position, which most current scholarship endorses, is that Weininger should be read as a primary source for intellectual history rather than as a live philosophical interlocutor. He is studied not because his arguments are correct or even interesting in themselves but because he is the clearest available evidence of how a particular configuration of European thought, around 1900, organized sex, race, science, ethics, and selfhood. Janik (1995), Sengoopta (2000), and Le Rider (1993) all take versions of this position.

## Institutional and intellectual inheritance

The Weininger lineage runs through several distinct channels.

The first is the early twentieth-century "scientific" misogyny tradition. Weininger's appropriation of hormone research, embryology, and bisexuality theory provided a model for the rhetorical use of biological language to ground metaphysical claims about sexual difference. Russett's *Sexual Science* (1989), although focused on the Anglophone Victorian tradition, documents the wider pattern; Sengoopta (2000) and Mosse (1985) trace the central European version. The pattern recurs in mid-twentieth-century popular sex theory and in some strands of evolutionary psychology, although the direct chain of citation thins out after the Second World War.

The second is the Nazi inheritance. The careful version, as above, is that Weininger provided a template, not a cause. George Mosse's *Nationalism and Sexuality* (1985) and Klaus Theweleit's *Male Fantasies* ([1977] 1987) trace the resonances. Andrew Hewitt's *Political Inversions* (1996) develops the more specific argument that fascist sexual theory drew on Weininger's framework for thinking sexual deviance as metaphysical pollution.

The third is literary modernism. The Joyce, Svevo, Kafka, Musil, and Lawrence inheritance is well documented (Reizbaum 1999; Robertson 1985; Anderson 1992; Luft 1980). Modernist writers used Weininger sometimes affirmatively, more often parodically or critically, but his typology gave them a vocabulary for thinking the dissolution of stable gendered selfhood that the period as a whole was trying to think.

The fourth is the popular-psychology tradition of "masculine and feminine principles." The Jungian framework of *animus* and *anima*, the popular literature of personality typology, and the contemporary self-help genre of "masculine and feminine energy" all bear a structural resemblance to Weininger's M and W, although the direct lineage runs more often through Jung and through the broader fin-de-siecle theosophical milieu than through Weininger personally (Noll 1994; Le Rider 1993, 248-262).

The fifth, as already noted, is the contemporary online reactionary milieu. The reappearance of Weiningerian tropes in red-pill, manosphere, and incel discourse is now documented (Nagle 2017; Ging 2019), although the citation is often indirect, mediated by interwar and postwar reactionary writers who themselves drew on Weininger.

The pattern across all five channels is the one that Sengoopta, Le Rider, Hyams, and Harrowitz emphasize: Weininger himself is rarely the proximate cause of any specific later development, but his book is the single most concentrated articulation of a structure of thought that recurs whenever the project of distinguishing the rational, ethical, sovereign self from a feminized and racialized other is attempted with explicit metaphysical seriousness. That is what makes the book a permanent reference point and what makes it, by the same measure, philosophically useless and historically indispensable.
