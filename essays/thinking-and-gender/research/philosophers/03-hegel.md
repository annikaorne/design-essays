# Hegel

G.W.F. Hegel (1770-1831) stands among the most consequential modern thinkers on ethical life, the state, family, civil society, recognition, and history. He also produced some of the most explicit philosophical claims in the canon about the different "vocations" of men and women. This review maps what he actually wrote about women, the principal feminist readings that treat these claims as structural to his system, the reconstructive readings that attempt to separate them, the divergent feminist uses of his Antigone, and the institutional afterlives through which his gendered framework is said to travel. The aim is neutral cartography of a live scholarly debate.

## Primary texts and what he actually said

Hegel's most systematic treatment of sexual difference appears in the *Elements of the Philosophy of Right* (1821), in the section on the family within "Ethical Life" (*Sittlichkeit*). In section 165 he argues that the "natural determinacy" of the two sexes acquires, through ethical life, "an intellectual and ethical significance." In section 166 he sets out the consequences: one sex "is therefore spirituality which divides itself up into personal self-sufficiency with being for itself and the knowledge and volition of free universality," while the other is "spirituality which maintains itself in unity as knowledge and volition of the substantial in the form of concrete individuality and feeling." Men are therefore said to belong to the state, science, and the struggles of the external world; women "have their substantial vocation in the family, and their ethical disposition consists in this piety."

The Addition to section 166, drawn from student lecture transcripts compiled by Eduard Gans, contains the now notorious simile: "The difference between man and woman is like that between animal and plant; the animal is closer in character to the man, the plant to the woman, for the latter is a more peaceful unfolding whose principle is the more indeterminate unity of feeling." Hegel continues: when women govern, "the state is in danger, for they act not according to the demands of universality but according to contingent inclination and opinion." As several scholars emphasize, the Addition is from student notes rather than Hegel's own manuscript, but it tracks and sharpens the published Remark and section. Nothing in Hegel's own hand contradicts it, and the basic schema appears in the main text of the 1821 edition as well.

In the *Phenomenology of Spirit* (1807), Hegel treats sexual difference in the chapter on Spirit, specifically in the subsections on "The ethical world," "Ethical action," and their aftermath (roughly VI.A.a and b). Through a reading of Sophocles' *Antigone*, he distinguishes the "divine law" of the family, associated with the feminine and the blood-bond, from the "human law" of the city and the state, associated with the masculine. The collision of these laws in Antigone's defiance of Creon stages, for Hegel, the necessary breakdown of the ancient ethical order. Here occurs one of the most quoted sentences in feminist philosophy: womankind is "the everlasting irony in the life of the community," who "changes by intrigue the universal end of the government into a private end, transforms its universal activity into a work of some particular individual, and perverts the universal property of the state into a possession and ornament for the family." The feminine is acknowledged as ethical, but as the ethical moment that must be superseded for world-historical Spirit to advance.

In the *Lectures on the Philosophy of World History* (delivered 1822-1831), Hegel's explicit statements about women are sparse, but women scarcely appear as world-historical agents, and his schema of the progress of Spirit through peoples coincides with his schema in which women are placed on the side of particularity, family, and nature. The public-political sphere of world history is occupied by world-historical individuals, who are invariably male. In the *Lectures on Aesthetics* (compiled posthumously from 1818-1829 lectures), Hegel returns repeatedly to *Antigone*, which he calls "one of the most sublime, and in every respect most excellent, works of art" and the exemplary tragedy of ethical substance divided against itself. Antigone represents the "pathos" of the family and the divine law; Creon that of the state; and the play's greatness lies in the fact that both are partially right and must perish in their one-sidedness.

## The structural reading

A major current of feminist scholarship argues that these claims are not detachable prejudices but load-bearing elements of Hegel's system. Luce Irigaray's "The Eternal Irony of the Community" in *Speculum of the Other Woman* (French 1974, English 1985) provides the paradigmatic version. For Irigaray, Hegel's dialectic depends on constructing the feminine as nature, blood, and immediacy so that masculine Spirit can posit itself through their negation. Antigone is not simply sublated; she is buried alive, and with her an entire maternal-genealogical order is foreclosed. The feminine cannot appear in Hegel's system except as the "other of the same," the internal boundary against which the universal constitutes itself.

Carole Pateman's *The Sexual Contract* (1988) places Hegel within a broader indictment of modern contract theory. For Pateman, Hegel inherits and codifies the sexual contract presupposed by Rousseau, Locke, and Kant: civil society is a fraternity, and the family is the patriarchal precondition of the public sphere. Hegel's explicit confinement of women to the family, she argues, is not an archaism but the articulate form of a structure that contract theory kept silent.

Patricia Jagentowicz Mills' *Woman, Nature and Psyche* (1987) develops a Frankfurt-School inflected critique that locates the problem in Hegel's metaphysics of nature and subjectivity. Her edited collection *Feminist Interpretations of G.W.F. Hegel* (1996) remains the most comprehensive anthology of the debate, with essays spanning structural, reconstructive, and deconstructive readings. Genevieve Lloyd's *The Man of Reason* (1984, 2nd ed. 1993) situates Hegel in a long history in which Reason itself is gendered masculine through its opposition to matter, feeling, and nature. On Lloyd's account, Hegel is not an outlier but the high-water mark of a structural gendering in which universality is the prerogative of a disembodied, and tacitly male, subject.

Alison Stone's "Matter and Form: Hegel, Organicism, and the Difference between Women and Men" (in Mills and others' 2010 volume *Hegel's Philosophy and Feminist Thought: Beyond Antigone?*, edited by Kimberly Hutchings and Tuija Pulkkinen) presses the structural case through Hegel's philosophy of nature. Stone argues that Hegel inherits the Aristotelian association of men with form and women with matter, and builds it into his organicist account of the state. If the state is an organism, and if women are the "vegetable" pole whose development remains passive, then women's exclusion from political life is not contingent but follows from Hegel's natural philosophy.

Kimberly Hutchings' *Hegel and Feminist Philosophy* (2003) is careful to hold both sides, but catalogues in detail how Hegel's specific claims about women track the architecture of the dialectic itself: the family as immediate ethical substance, civil society as the sphere of particularity mediated through labor and need, the state as universal. Because women are assigned to the first moment, they cannot be full participants in the higher mediations without collapsing the differentiation on which ethical life depends.

## The separability, charitable, and reconstructive readings

A second major tradition argues that Hegel's dialectical architecture is recoverable once his specific claims about women are removed or revised. Seyla Benhabib's *Situating the Self* (1992) includes the influential chapter "On Hegel, Women and Irony," which concedes that Hegel's treatment of women is indefensible and that Irigaray's reading of the *Phenomenology* is textually strong. But Benhabib argues that the resources Hegel supplies, his critique of Kantian formalism, his account of situated moral agency, and his theory of recognition, are precisely what feminist ethics needs. The task is critical appropriation, not repudiation. Benhabib reads Antigone's "irony" against Hegel, treating the feminine voice as a standing critique of any ethics that proclaims itself fully universal while resting on excluded particularities.

Allen Wood's *Hegel's Ethical Thought* (1990) offers the most systematic Anglophone reconstruction of Hegel as a modern ethical theorist. Wood treats Hegel's views on women and the family as an empirical sociological thesis about early nineteenth-century German life that Hegel mistook for a rational necessity. On Wood's reading, the dialectic of the family, civil society, and the state does not require that women occupy the family and only the family; it requires that there be differentiated spheres of ethical life, which a reformed Hegelianism can accommodate without the gendered assignment. Charles Taylor's *Hegel* (1975) and his later work on recognition take a similar line: the Hegelian theory of recognition is a general account of the social constitution of the self that can and should be detached from Hegel's contingent misogyny. Stephen Houlgate, in his introductions to Hegel and in work such as *An Introduction to Hegel* (2005), likewise treats Hegel's claims about women as historically conditioned errors rather than structural commitments. Sally Sedgwick's work on Hegel's critique of Kant, while not primarily feminist, supplies philosophical backing for the reconstructive camp by showing that Hegel's ethical theory does not rest on biological essentialism but on a critique of abstract formalism that can be extended to critique its own gendered exclusions.

## The Antigone debate

Antigone is the site where the structural and reconstructive readings meet most sharply. Irigaray reads Hegel's Antigone as the definitive subordination of the feminine: she is acknowledged as the bearer of the divine law only in order to be buried, and her burial is the condition of the transition to the state. For Irigaray, Hegel cannot recognize Antigone as a political actor without dismantling the scaffolding of his dialectic.

Judith Butler's *Antigone's Claim: Kinship Between Life and Death* (2000) produces a different but not simply opposed reading. Butler argues that Hegel, and later Lacan, needed Antigone to stand for kinship as the pre-political ground on which the state arises, but that Antigone actually does the opposite: she speaks in public, invokes the state's language against the state, kills herself outside a proper family, and destabilizes the very kinship order she is said to represent. Butler's Antigone is a figure of aberrant kinship who exposes the instability of the normative family on which Hegel's Sittlichkeit depends. Butler is therefore closer to Irigaray on the diagnosis, Hegel's text cannot contain Antigone, but reaches a partly rehabilitative conclusion, in which Antigone names the kinship arrangements that Hegel's frame excludes.

Joanna Hodge's work on Hegel and sexual difference, and the essays collected in Hutchings and Pulkkinen's *Beyond Antigone?* (2010), chart the proliferation of Antigone readings since Butler. A common move is to insist that the figure has become over-determined: feminists now read Antigone differently not because Sophocles is ambiguous but because they are reading against a tradition of readings in which Hegel's is canonical.

## Institutional and intellectual inheritance

The feminist critique does not stop at Hegel. A substantial literature argues that Hegelian categories, once transmitted into modern social and political theory, carried their gendered foundations with them. Pateman's analysis connects Hegel's family to the continuing public-private divide in liberal democratic theory. Nancy Fraser's work on recognition, though sympathetic to Hegel, develops the critique that recognition politics can entrench status hierarchies if it does not address redistribution and representation alongside cultural recognition. Axel Honneth's Hegelian theory of recognition in *The Struggle for Recognition* (1992) has been criticized by feminists such as Fraser and Amy Allen for inheriting Hegel's tendency to treat the family as a sphere of love rather than power.

On the philosophy of history side, critics such as Susan Buck-Morss (*Hegel, Haiti, and Universal History*, 2009) have argued that Hegel's exclusions of Africa, and the attendant exclusions of non-European and feminine agency, are not removable impurities but shape what counts as history for him. Historians of feminism including Moira Gatens and Alison Stone have traced how the organicist theory of the state, in which the family is the immediate ethical moment, travels into twentieth-century political theory from Gentile through communitarianism, carrying with it an implicit division of labor.

On the family as institution, Laura Wildemann Kane's "On Hegel, Women, and the Foundation of Ethical Life: Why Gender Doesn't Belong in the Family" (*CLIO: A Journal of Literature, History, and the Philosophy of History* 44, no. 1, 2014) is representative of a recent reconstructive move: preserve the Hegelian insight that the family is an ethical institution, but strip out the claim that gender assigns specific roles within it. The critical counterpoint, associated with Mills, Stone, and Hutchings, is that once gender is genuinely stripped out, the distinctive Hegelian picture of the family as the sphere of immediate, feeling-based ethical substance is hard to reconstruct.

## Live debates

Three debates remain open in the scholarship. First, whether the *Philosophy of Right* Addition at section 166 is evidential of Hegel's considered view. Most commentators on both sides accept that the Addition represents Hegel's lecture voice accurately enough to be weighed, but structural readers use it more freely than reconstructive readers. Second, whether Hegel's Antigone is the "definitive" subordination Irigaray takes it to be, or whether the text's ambivalence, the fact that Antigone is treated as genuinely ethical and as genuinely tragic, supplies resources against Hegel's own conclusions. Butler, Benhabib, and Hutchings all exploit this ambivalence, though they come out in different places. Third, whether contemporary Hegelianisms, from Honneth and Pippin to Brandom, can still be defended once the gendered architecture is removed, or whether the resulting theory is Hegelian in name only. On this last question the literature genuinely splits, and no consensus is in sight.
