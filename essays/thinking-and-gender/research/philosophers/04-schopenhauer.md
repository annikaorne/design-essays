# Schopenhauer

Arthur Schopenhauer (1788 to 1860) is a limit case for any effort to separate a philosopher's system from his prejudices. Unlike Kant, whose remarks on women appear mostly in the *Anthropology* and in occasional essays, and unlike Hegel, whose sexual division of labor is folded into a larger theory of ethical life, Schopenhauer wrote a short, widely reprinted, openly contemptuous essay titled "On Women" ("Über die Weiber") and integrated a gendered theory of sexual desire into the metaphysical supplements of his masterwork. The literature therefore runs along two tracks. One track asks whether the misogyny is incidental to the metaphysics of Will and can be quarantined as nineteenth-century bias. The other asks whether the Will/representation dichotomy, and the account of sexual love as Nature's "cunning" working through the sexed individual, is already gendered at the level of the system. This review maps both tracks, the reception history that carried Schopenhauer's misogyny into Nietzsche, Wagner, Freud, and literary modernism, and the comparatively thin feminist engagement with Schopenhauer relative to the Kant and Hegel literatures.

## Primary texts and what he actually said

The notorious essay is "Über die Weiber," chapter 27 of the second volume of *Parerga and Paralipomena* (1851). In English, the standard scholarly rendering is now Adrian Del Caro and Christopher Janaway's translation in the Cambridge Edition (*Parerga and Paralipomena*, vol. 2, 2015), which supersedes E. F. J. Payne's earlier Oxford translation (1974). The essay runs about a dozen pages and makes a compact set of claims that later readers have cited in isolation: that women are "the *sexus sequior*, the second sex, inferior in every respect to the first"; that they are "childish, frivolous and short-sighted, in a word, big children all their lives"; that they exist "solely for the propagation of the species"; that they are incapable of "purely objective" interest and so cannot produce anything truly great in art or science; that they have "no proper sense of justice"; and that monogamy is unnatural and disadvantages women by forcing a single wife into a position of artificial equality with the husband (Schopenhauer [1851] 2015, chap. 27, §§362 to 370; Payne translation [1974] 2000).

The metaphysical framework that sits behind the essay is worked out earlier. *The World as Will and Representation*, vol. 1 (1818), Book II, introduces the Will as the thing-in-itself, a blind, striving, purposeless force that manifests as nature and as the human body. Book IV turns to ethics and the denial of the Will, with asceticism and compassion as the routes by which the individual, bound to the Will through the body, can achieve a relative freedom through aesthetic contemplation and, ultimately, saintly renunciation (Schopenhauer [1818] 2010, Book IV, §§54 to 71). Volume 2 (1844), which Schopenhauer added as a book of supplements, contains chapter 44, "Metaphysik der Geschlechtsliebe" ("The Metaphysics of Sexual Love"). This is the chapter most often cited in the structural reading. Sexual love, Schopenhauer argues, is not an individual emotion but the Will of the species operating through the individual, who is deceived into believing that he or she acts from personal passion while in fact being manipulated to produce offspring whose constitution the Will "selects" for. Within that account, men and women are assigned contrasting roles: the man as active chooser whose rational faculties are temporarily overridden by the species, the woman as the site of gestation and as the cunning vehicle through which Nature secures its ends (Schopenhauer [1844] 2018, chap. 44; on the chapter as template for later theories of unconscious sexual motivation, see Young 2005, ch. 7; Janaway 1994, ch. 5).

Translation and citation matter here. Payne's English has been the most widely circulated and is the version most often quoted in the polemical literature. The newer Cambridge Edition, under the general editorship of Janaway, has normalized terminology across the corpus and is now the standard for scholarly citation (Janaway, ed., 2010 onward).

## The structural reading

The structural reading holds that the Will/representation dichotomy is itself sexed, that sexual difference is not an incidental example but the privileged case through which the Will shows itself, and that the misogyny is therefore load-bearing rather than decorative.

Sarah Kofman's *Le respect des femmes* (1982) reads Kant and Rousseau together and frames Schopenhauer as the philosopher who, more than any other, makes sexual difference the site at which the rational subject confronts its own bodily subjection. In Kofman's later work on Nietzsche (*Nietzsche and Metaphor*, 1972 [1993]; *Explosion I* and *II*, 1992 to 1993), she traces how the Schopenhauerian coding of woman as Nature's instrument, as seductive surface concealing metaphysical depth, is inherited and radicalized by Nietzsche. Kofman's reading is careful and close; she does not claim that every passage in Schopenhauer requires misogyny, only that the system offers a ready template in which "woman" names the point where Will shows through representation.

Genevieve Lloyd's *The Man of Reason* (1984) treats Schopenhauer briefly but pointedly. Lloyd places him in the lineage in which "reason" is defined by contrast with what has been coded feminine (body, instinct, particularity, reproduction), and notes that Schopenhauer inverts the usual Enlightenment valuation without disturbing the underlying sexing: where Kant privileges reason over inclination, Schopenhauer privileges the Will over representation, and in both cases the feminine sits on the side of nature, body, and immediacy. The inversion preserves the dichotomy.

Kathleen Higgins, in "Schopenhauer and Nietzsche on the Death of God and the Eternal Feminine" (in *Schopenhauer, Philosophy, and the Arts*, ed. Jacquette, 1996) and in her survey work on nineteenth-century German philosophy, argues in a mixed mode. Higgins grants that much of Schopenhauer's system can be stated without reference to sex, but she holds that the "Metaphysics of Sexual Love" chapter, and the aesthetics of music and tragedy that depend on a contrast between blind striving and contemplative stillness, draw expressive force from a gendered imaginary that Schopenhauer himself did not invent but did consolidate for later readers.

Patricia Matthews's recent work on women in nineteenth-century German philosophy (Matthews 2020; Matthews and Newhard, eds., 2024) situates Schopenhauer in a longer conversation that includes Hegel, the early German romantics, and women philosophers such as Rahel Varnhagen and Bettina von Arnim, whom the male canon largely erased. Matthews argues that the absence of women from Schopenhauer's list of possible interlocutors is not a neutral historical fact but a philosophical decision consistent with his account of women's incapacity for "objective" interest.

Mary Warnock's introductions to Schopenhauer (especially her preface to the Everyman edition of *The World as Will and Idea*, 1995, and her chapter in *Women Philosophers*, 1996) are comparatively concise but critical. Warnock notes that Schopenhauer's antisemitism and his misogyny both sit uneasily alongside the ethical core of his system, which privileges compassion for all sentient beings, and she treats this as an internal tension in the system rather than a detachable embarrassment. Her position is closer to the structural reading than to the charitable one, though she is more measured than Kofman.

Robert Wicks's *Schopenhauer* (2008) and his Stanford Encyclopedia entry (Wicks 2021) explicitly acknowledge the debate without resolving it. Wicks lays out both readings, notes that the "Metaphysics of Sexual Love" is the most serious candidate for a structural role, and concludes that Schopenhauer's published remarks on women are severe enough that contemporary readers cannot simply excise them.

## The separability and charitable reading

The charitable reading has been advanced, often in passing, by the major Anglophone Schopenhauer monographs of the 1980s to 2000s, which together built the current scholarly infrastructure around Schopenhauer in English.

Bryan Magee's *The Philosophy of Schopenhauer* (1983; rev. 1997) is the fullest popular exposition in English. Magee treats "On Women" as an outburst reflecting Schopenhauer's bitter relationship with his mother Johanna (a successful novelist who barred him from her salon after protracted family conflict) and with Caroline Richter, and argues that the essay is biographically intelligible but philosophically non-load-bearing. In Magee's reading, the Will is not sexed in any intrinsic sense; it is a metaphysical principle that manifests as striving in all organisms, and the gendered examples in the supplements are illustrations rather than proofs. Magee does not defend the misogyny; he quarantines it.

Christopher Janaway's *Self and World in Schopenhauer's Philosophy* (1989) and his later *Schopenhauer: A Very Short Introduction* (1994) take a similar line on the structural question. Janaway's project is to reconstruct the arguments of Book I and Book II on their merits, and he largely brackets the social and sexual asides. His later work as editor of the Cambridge Companion (Janaway, ed., *The Cambridge Companion to Schopenhauer*, 1999) and of the Cambridge Edition (Janaway et al., 2010 onward) has been to bring the full corpus, including *Parerga* and *Paralipomena*, into scholarly view, which has the side effect of making the misogyny harder to ignore but does not itself argue that it is structural.

David E. Cartwright's *Historical Dictionary of Schopenhauer's Philosophy* (2005) and his biography *Schopenhauer: A Biography* (2010) are the standard reference works on Schopenhauer's life and on the genesis of particular texts. Cartwright documents the composition history of "Über die Weiber" (drafted and expanded in Schopenhauer's notebooks through the 1820s and 1830s before being published in 1851), the biographical context, and the essay's early reception. Cartwright treats the misogyny as a settled historical fact and as a moral problem for readers, but does not argue that it is metaphysically required by the system.

Julian Young's *Schopenhauer* (2005) is a detailed philosophical study that engages directly with the sexual-love chapter. Young argues that the Schopenhauerian picture of sexual desire as species-manipulation can be stated in gender-symmetric terms, and that Schopenhauer himself partly does so (both sexes are manipulated; both are deceived). On Young's reading, Schopenhauer adds a layer of 19th-century stereotype on top of a structurally symmetric account, and the stereotype is removable. Young is not an apologist; he thinks the essay is bad philosophy. His claim is narrower: the stereotype is not entailed by the metaphysics.

John E. Atwell's *Schopenhauer on the Character of the World* (1995) and Dale Jacquette's *The Philosophy of Schopenhauer* (2005) occupy adjacent ground. Both concentrate on the metaphysical and ethical core (Will, representation, compassion, the denial of the Will) and treat the misogyny as an unfortunate layer rather than a constitutive feature.

The split between the two readings is therefore real but less polarized than it first appears. Nearly all commentators grant that "On Women" is indefensible by contemporary standards. The question is whether, once that essay is set aside, the remaining system is neutral between the sexes, or whether the supplements and the ethics of the Will carry the gendering forward in a subtler form. The charitable readers say neutral; the structural readers say subtler form.

## Influence as a vehicle for misogyny

The reception history is the part of the story where the stakes of the debate become clearest, because whether or not the misogyny is structural to the system, it was a highly portable and widely imitated template.

Nietzsche's relation to Schopenhauer is the most studied case. Nietzsche discovered *The World as Will and Representation* in a Leipzig bookshop in 1865 and described the encounter as a conversion (*Schopenhauer as Educator*, 1874, in the *Untimely Meditations*). His later break with Schopenhauer, staged in *The Gay Science* (1882) and *Beyond Good and Evil* (1886), does not extend to the misogyny; the remarks on women in *Beyond Good and Evil* §§231 to 239 and in *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* ("Of Old and Young Women") draw on the Schopenhauerian template even when they invert or parody it. Kofman's *Nietzsche et la scène philosophique* (1979) and *Explosion* (1992 to 1993), Kelly Oliver's *Womanizing Nietzsche* (1995), and Maudemarie Clark's chapter in *Nietzsche on the Women Question* (in Schutte, ed., *Nietzsche's Feminism*, 1984) all treat Schopenhauer as the proximate source.

Wagner's Schopenhauerism is the most culturally consequential case. Wagner read *The World as Will and Representation* in 1854 and reoriented *Der Ring des Nibelungen* and, especially, *Tristan und Isolde* (1859) around a Schopenhauerian metaphysics of erotic longing, night, and renunciation. Bryan Magee's *Wagner and Philosophy* (2000), Mark Berry's *Treacherous Bonds and Laughing Fire* (2006), and Julian Young's *The Philosophies of Richard Wagner* (Lexington, 2014) trace the detailed influence. The gendered coding, woman as erotic lure and as site of the night-world of Will, was absorbed into operatic and then popular culture through Wagner's reach.

Freud's relation is more contested. Freud claimed to have read little Schopenhauer directly, but scholars including Marcel Zentner (*Die Flucht ins Vergessen*, 1995) and Christopher Young and Andrew Brook ("Schopenhauer and Freud," *International Journal of Psychoanalysis* 75, 1994) have documented substantial conceptual overlap between Schopenhauer's Will and Freud's drive theory, between Schopenhauer's account of repression-through-aesthetic-contemplation and Freud's sublimation, and between the "Metaphysics of Sexual Love" and the theory of the unconscious. The gendering in Freud (activity and passivity, castration, the enigmatic woman) has a Schopenhauerian ancestry even where it passes through intermediaries.

Thomas Mann's *Buddenbrooks* (1901) stages a Schopenhauerian conversion scene, and his essays on Schopenhauer (collected in *Essays of Three Decades*, 1947) are a warm, if qualified, endorsement. Nicholas White's *The Family in Crisis in Late Nineteenth-Century French Fiction* (1999) and Timothy Armstrong's *Haunted Hardy: Poetry, History, Memory* (2000) document the Schopenhauerian current in late-Victorian and fin-de-siècle literature, in which the "On Women" essay became a reference point for pessimistic accounts of sex, marriage, and desire.

Chandak Sengoopta's *Otto Weininger: Sex, Science, and Self in Imperial Vienna* (2000) is important for the present review because it shows how Schopenhauer's framework, mediated through Weininger's *Geschlecht und Charakter* (1903), provided a pseudo-scientific scaffolding for the most virulent misogyny of fin-de-siècle Central European culture. Weininger's "W" and "M" principles, his claim that women are pure sexuality and therefore incapable of genius or morality, is a radicalization of Schopenhauer's position, explicitly credited by Weininger himself. Sengoopta's work is the standard reference.

## Institutional and intellectual inheritance

Three long-term inheritances deserve separate note.

The first is philosophical pessimism as a tradition. Schopenhauer is the modern source for a line that runs through Eduard von Hartmann's *Philosophie des Unbewussten* (1869), Julius Bahnsen, Philipp Mainländer, early Nietzsche, Emil Cioran, and, in English-language analytic philosophy, David Benatar's *Better Never to Have Been* (2006). Frederick Beiser's *Weltschmerz: Pessimism in German Philosophy, 1860 to 1900* (2016) is the authoritative recent history. Pessimism as such is not a misogynistic doctrine, but its founding texts, and many of its nineteenth-century vehicles, carried a gendered account of desire and reproduction that later pessimists have largely not repudiated.

The second is the concept of "will" as it entered psychology, psychoanalysis, and cultural theory. William James's *Principles of Psychology* (1890), the Freudian drive, Nietzsche's will to power, and the broader vocabulary of "unconscious" motivation all owe something to Schopenhauer. The gendered flavoring is variable; James is comparatively neutral, Freud is not, Nietzsche is explicit.

The third is literary modernism. Beckett's *Proust* (1931) and his engagement with Schopenhauer through Hardy, Conrad, and Mann are documented in J. D. O'Hara's *Samuel Beckett's Hidden Drives* (1997). Proust himself in *Le Temps retrouvé* (1927) articulates a Schopenhauerian aesthetics. The tradition tends to inherit the pessimism and the aestheticism while varying on the misogyny: some writers reproduce it, others complicate it, few directly repudiate its Schopenhauerian provenance.

## Live debates

Feminist engagement with Schopenhauer is thinner than with Kant or Hegel. There is no volume in the Penn State "Re-Reading the Canon" series devoted to Schopenhauer (there are volumes on Kant, Hegel, Nietzsche, Plato, Aristotle, and many others). There is no monograph comparable to Schott's *Feminist Interpretations of Kant* (1997) or Hutchings's *Hegel and Feminist Philosophy* (2003). The best available entries are Kofman's chapters on Schopenhauer inside her Nietzsche work, Matthews's recent essays, Warnock's introductions, and scattered chapters in edited volumes such as Janaway, ed., *The Cambridge Companion to Schopenhauer* (1999), and Vandenabeele, ed., *A Companion to Schopenhauer* (2012).

Several explanations have been offered for the thinness. Schopenhauer is not a foundation for modern liberal institutions the way Kant and Hegel are, so the incentive to rehabilitate him for democratic theory, constitutional law, or political ethics is lower. The misogyny is so overt, and so concentrated in a single essay, that the separability debate looks less philosophically interesting than the Kant debate, where the prejudices are more diffusely woven. Schopenhauer's readers have historically been literary and artistic rather than academic-philosophical, and the pessimist tradition has tended to recruit readers who find the grim tone attractive rather than readers who arrive wanting to critique it. Wicks (2021) and Matthews (2020) both note the imbalance without resolving it.

The live debate, such as it is, concerns three questions. First, whether "On Women" can be read as a literary performance in the mode of Schopenhauer's broader polemical essays (against professors, against Hegel, against journalism), which would partially contextualize without excusing it, or whether it is sincere doctrine. Cartwright and Young lean toward sincere doctrine embedded in a polemical rhetoric. Second, whether the "Metaphysics of Sexual Love" can be restated in gender-symmetric terms, as Young argues, or whether its explanatory force depends on the asymmetric roles it assigns to the sexes, as Kofman and Higgins argue. Third, whether contemporary readers who are drawn to Schopenhauer's ethics of compassion (Cartwright 2010; Janaway 2007) can consistently hold that ethic while rejecting his claim that women lack "the sense of justice"; the tension here, as Warnock notes, is internal to the system and has not been fully worked through.
