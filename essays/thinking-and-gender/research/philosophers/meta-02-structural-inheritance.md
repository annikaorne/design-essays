# Structural inheritance of philosophical sexism into modern institutions

The "structural inheritance" thesis holds that the sexist content of canonical modern philosophy was not incidental to the institutions those thinkers helped design but constitutive of them. On this reading, the modern democratic state, the university, common law, scientific medicine, and the wage economy were all built with gendered assumptions about reason, agency, and the body baked into their foundations, and those assumptions persist as structure even after they have been formally disavowed. The literature supporting this thesis is large and multidisciplinary. So is the literature that resists it, in versions ranging from institutional-modernization accounts to procedural liberalism. What follows maps the major positions, the empirical evidence each marshals, and the sharpest points of disagreement.

## Democracy and political theory

The foundational modern statement of the structural claim in political theory is Carole Pateman's *The Sexual Contract* (Stanford University Press, 1988). Pateman argues that the social contract tradition running from Hobbes through Locke, Rousseau, and Kant was not merely a contract among abstract individuals but a specifically fraternal compact that presupposed, and secured, men's access to women's bodies and labor. The "individual" of liberal theory is male by construction, not by oversight, because contractual subjecthood was defined against the dependent, embodied, reproductive figure of the wife. Pateman's companion essays in *The Disorder of Women* (Polity, 1989) extend the claim: what looks like women's gradual inclusion into citizenship is more accurately their incorporation on terms a prior fraternal settlement already fixed.

Joan Landes's *Women and the Public Sphere in the Age of the French Revolution* (Cornell University Press, 1988) makes a parallel historical case. The revolutionary public sphere was not gender-neutral and then narrowed; it was constituted through the active exclusion of the salon and the "public woman," and Rousseau's prescriptions in *Emile* and the *Letter to d'Alembert* were operational, not decorative. Ursula Vogel's work on marriage law and liberal citizenship (see "Whose Property? The Double Standard of Adultery in Nineteenth-Century Law," in Carol Smart ed., *Regulating Womanhood*, Routledge, 1992) and Moira Gatens's *Imaginary Bodies* (Routledge, 1996) press the point that the liberal subject is imagined with a specific (male) body, which becomes neutral by fiat rather than by design.

Anne Phillips, in *Engendering Democracy* (Polity, 1991) and *The Politics of Presence* (Oxford University Press, 1995), agrees that classical democratic theory inherits gendered exclusions but asks whether current democracies still do, and how. Her answer is calibrated: formal inclusion has advanced, but the distinction between public reasoning and private dependence remains legible in who stands for election, whose interests are treated as "particular," and what counts as political speech. Nancy Fraser's "Rethinking the Public Sphere" (*Social Text* 25/26, 1990) develops the critique of Habermas along similar lines, arguing that the bourgeois public sphere's claim to inclusive rationality was founded on gendered and class exclusions that shaped its norms of deliberation. Iris Marion Young's *Justice and the Politics of Difference* (Princeton University Press, 1990) adds that the ideal of the impartial citizen, absorbed from Kant and Rousseau, systematically devalues embodied and affective modes of public reasoning associated with women and other subordinated groups.

The empirical case these authors marshal tends to be institutional-historical: the chronology of suffrage (Kerber, Cott, below), the gendered structure of welfare state entitlements (Fraser and Linda Gordon, "A Genealogy of Dependency," *Signs* 19(2), 1994), and persistent descriptive under-representation in legislatures. The claim is not that contemporary democracies are identical to Rousseau's *polis* but that the categories through which they distinguish citizen from dependent, reason from affect, public from private, carry forward the same asymmetries.

## Education

Educational historians supply some of the most granular evidence for structural inheritance. Barbara Solomon's *In the Company of Educated Women* (Yale University Press, 1985) tracks how 19th-century American higher education admitted women only after protracted debate over whether women's minds and bodies could bear college-level study, with the debate's terms drawn directly from Enlightenment and post-Enlightenment philosophy on female cognition. Charlotte Williams Conable's *Women at Cornell: The Myth of Equal Education* (Cornell University Press, 1977) shows that even a pioneering coeducational institution routed women into Home Economics while reserving the liberal arts and sciences for men, a structural sorting whose justifications were philosophical and medical in roughly equal measure.

Linda Eisenmann's *Higher Education for Women in Postwar America, 1945-1965* (Johns Hopkins University Press, 2006) extends the story forward, documenting how the postwar university reconciled female enrollment growth with an "educated motherhood" ideology that preserved disciplinary segregation well into the period often described as one of gender liberalization. Patricia Albjerg Graham's essays, including "Expansion and Exclusion: A History of Women in American Higher Education" (*Signs* 3(4), 1978), make the case that women's access to education expanded along gender-segregated tracks that reproduced, in institutional form, a division of cognitive labor with 18th- and 19th-century roots.

Mary Beth Norton's *Liberty's Daughters* (Little, Brown, 1980) and Cynthia Eagle Russett's *Sexual Science: The Victorian Construction of Womanhood* (Harvard University Press, 1989) document how 19th-century scientific claims about female mental capacity, themselves downstream of earlier philosophical anthropology, were imported into debates over curricula and admissions. Jo Anne Preston's work on the feminization of teaching, including "Domestic Ideology, School Reformers, and Female Teachers" (*New England Quarterly* 66(4), 1993), shows that normal schools trained women for teaching at the moment when teaching was being redefined as a low-paid, morally feminine extension of domestic labor, a redefinition that drew explicitly on Rousseauian and evangelical conceptions of womanhood.

The evidence these historians offer is archival and fine-grained: admissions files, curricular documents, faculty debates, normal school prospectuses. Whether that archival record supports a specifically *philosophical* causal claim, as opposed to the broader claim that educational structures inherited the gender ideology of their era, is a point to which critics return (see Counter-arguments).

## Law

In law, the structural inheritance claim has been made most forcefully by Reva Siegel, Catharine MacKinnon, Linda Kerber, Nancy Cott, and Hendrik Hartog. Siegel's "Reasoning from the Body: A Historical Perspective on Abortion Regulation and Questions of Equal Protection" (*Stanford Law Review* 44(2), 1992, 261-381) argues that 19th-century abortion statutes were explicitly justified by physicians and legislators invoking women's natural reproductive role, and that contemporary jurisprudence inherits those justifications when it treats reproductive regulation as neutral rather than as gender classification. Her later work, including "She the People: The Nineteenth Amendment, Sex Equality, Federalism, and the Family" (*Harvard Law Review* 115(4), 2002), pushes the constitutional version of the argument: the 14th Amendment's sex-equality doctrine developed under the shadow of coverture and has not fully shed it.

Catharine MacKinnon's *Toward a Feminist Theory of the State* (Harvard University Press, 1989) makes the strong structural claim that the liberal state is not neutral with respect to gender but is the codified form of male dominance. MacKinnon's argument is philosophical as much as doctrinal: the distinction between public and private, the standard of the reasonable person, the individualist construction of harm, are each carriers of an earlier philosophical anthropology.

Linda Kerber's *No Constitutional Right to Be Ladies: Women and the Obligations of Citizenship* (Hill & Wang, 1998) shows that American women's citizenship was built around exemptions, from militia service, from jury duty, from certain forms of contract, that presupposed women's dependent status. Nancy Cott's *Public Vows: A History of Marriage and the Nation* (Harvard University Press, 2000) documents marriage as a constitutive institution of American citizenship, with coverture at its center and its gendered asymmetries dissolving only slowly across the 20th century. Hendrik Hartog's *Man and Wife in America: A History* (Harvard University Press, 2000) traces coverture's long persistence in common law doctrine even after its formal abolition, arguing that the categories in which American marital and property law is still framed presuppose the husband-wife unit coverture created.

Taken together, these scholars make a concrete version of the structural claim: specific legal doctrines (coverture, the marital rape exemption abolished in US states only between 1976 and 1993, the endurance of head-of-household presumptions in tax and benefits law, the structure of equal protection scrutiny for sex) carry forward premises about female legal personality articulated in Blackstone, Kant's *Metaphysics of Morals*, Hegel's *Philosophy of Right*, and their common-law interpreters.

## Medicine and science

The structural inheritance thesis in medicine and science has its most influential statement in Londa Schiebinger's *The Mind Has No Sex? Women in the Origins of Modern Science* (Harvard University Press, 1989) and *Nature's Body: Gender in the Making of Modern Science* (Beacon, 1993). Schiebinger documents how late 17th- and 18th-century scientific institutions, alongside Enlightenment natural philosophy, constructed sex difference as a deep biological fact in precisely the period when the "rights of man" were being universalized, and how those constructions shaped everything from anatomical illustration to taxonomy.

Thomas Laqueur's *Making Sex: Body and Gender from the Greeks to Freud* (Harvard University Press, 1990) offers the strongest version of the genealogical argument: a shift occurred in the late 18th century from a "one-sex" model, in which female anatomy was understood as a lesser variant of the male, to a "two-sex" model of incommensurable difference, and this shift was philosophical and political before it was empirical. Laqueur's thesis has been criticized on historical-evidentiary grounds (Helen King's *The One-Sex Body on Trial*, Ashgate, 2013, is the best-known rejoinder, arguing that Laqueur overstates the coherence of both models), but even critics concede that scientific accounts of sex tracked political needs as much as anatomical ones.

Cynthia Eagle Russett's *Sexual Science* (1989), cited above, documents how Victorian science worked backward from prior philosophical and theological assumptions about women's nature to produce the craniometry, energy-conservation theories, and evolutionary arguments that barred women from higher education and the professions. Barbara Ehrenreich and Deirdre English's *For Her Own Good: Two Centuries of the Experts' Advice to Women* (Anchor, 1978; revised Anchor, 2005) traces the same pattern into 20th-century medical advice literature.

Anne Fausto-Sterling's *Sexing the Body: Gender Politics and the Construction of Sexuality* (Basic Books, 2000) extends the argument into contemporary biology and endocrinology, showing how even recent research programs inherit binary frames whose philosophical genealogy she makes explicit. Sandra Harding's *The Science Question in Feminism* (Cornell University Press, 1986) offers the most philosophically ambitious version: the epistemology of modern science itself, with its detached knower and objectified nature, is the epistemology of a specifically masculine subject position.

Contemporary empirical work on medicine extends the argument into diagnosis and treatment. Arthur Kleinman's clinical ethnography, in *The Illness Narratives* (Basic Books, 1988) and later essays, established the framework within which later scholars have documented gender asymmetries in pain treatment and symptom interpretation. Maya Dusenbery's *Doing Harm: The Truth About How Bad Medicine and Lazy Science Leave Women Dismissed, Misdiagnosed, and Sick* (HarperOne, 2018) and Gabrielle Jackson's *Pain and Prejudice: How the Medical System Ignores Women* (Greystone, 2019) synthesize a growing body of clinical research documenting that women present for longer, are believed less, and are undertreated for pain relative to men with equivalent presentations. Both works explicitly connect these findings to the 18th- and 19th-century construction of the female body as hysterical and unreliable.

## Economics and labor

Silvia Federici's *Caliban and the Witch: Women, the Body, and Primitive Accumulation* (Autonomedia, 2004) argues that capitalist labor markets were founded on a gendered expropriation: the violent subordination of women's reproductive and domestic labor during the enclosures and witch hunts produced the gendered division of paid and unpaid work that still structures the global economy. Maria Mies's *Patriarchy and Accumulation on a World Scale* (Zed Books, 1986) makes a parallel international-political-economy argument: modern capitalism depends on the continued classification of "women's work" as outside the wage relation.

Nancy Folbre's *The Invisible Heart: Economics and Family Values* (New Press, 2001) and *Greed, Lust and Gender* (Oxford University Press, 2009) reconstruct the history of economic thought to show how classical political economy, including Smith, defined productive labor against reproductive labor, and how that distinction persists in national accounts. Paula England's body of work, including "The Separative Self: Androcentric Bias in Neoclassical Assumptions" (in Ferber and Nelson eds., *Beyond Economic Man*, University of Chicago Press, 1993), argues that the core assumptions of neoclassical economics, the independent utility-maximizing individual, the separability of preferences, the impersonality of market exchange, are philosophically male-coded inheritances.

Claudia Goldin's historical work, including *Understanding the Gender Gap: An Economic History of American Women* (Oxford University Press, 1990) and her 2023 Nobel lecture, is more cautious. Goldin accepts the persistence of gender gaps and documents them with unusual rigor but explains them primarily through labor-market frictions (especially the structure of high-hour "greedy" occupations) rather than through a philosophical genealogy. Her work is often cited by both sides of the debate.

## Counter-arguments (the separability camp at institutional level)

Three lines of objection recur.

First, **modernization arguments** hold that institutions evolve, progressively shedding the prejudices of their origins, and that the relevant question is where they are now, not where they came from. Ronald Inglehart and Pippa Norris's *Rising Tide: Gender Equality and Cultural Change Around the World* (Cambridge University Press, 2003) is a leading statistical version: gender attitudes and women's political representation improve with post-industrial development, and the trend is robust across institutional inheritances. Francis Fukuyama in various works has made a similar structural-optimist case. Structural readers respond (Phillips, Fraser) that improvement along some dimensions is compatible with persistence along others, and that measuring only what the original philosophy counted as politically relevant will miss what it excluded.

Second, **consensus-history arguments** hold that legal and political frameworks are the product of pluralist bargaining over time, not the authorial expression of any single thinker or tradition. Will Kymlicka, in *Contemporary Political Philosophy* (Oxford University Press, 2nd ed. 2002), while sympathetic to feminist critique, notes that liberal institutions have proved unusually open to internal reform and that attributing their current form to Locke or Kant underweights two centuries of contestation. Susan Moller Okin, in *Justice, Gender, and the Family* (Basic Books, 1989), sits between the camps: she accepts that canonical theory is deeply sexist but argues, contra Pateman, that liberal premises themselves contain the resources for their own correction if the family is brought inside the scope of justice.

Third, **procedural-liberal optimism** holds that Rawlsian reflective equilibrium, or its cousins, can identify and revise inherited exclusions without abandoning the framework. Martha Nussbaum's capability approach (*Women and Human Development*, Cambridge University Press, 2000) is the best-developed version: liberal universalism, properly reformulated around functioning and capability, can meet the feminist challenge without accepting the strong structural claim. MacKinnon's rejoinder is that procedures cannot correct for biases built into the categories the procedure uses.

## Connection to the empirical literature on contemporary outcomes

A number of empirical findings from contemporary social science are increasingly cited by structural-inheritance scholars as confirmation of the thesis. Levanon, England, and Allison, "Occupational Feminization and Pay: Assessing Causal Dynamics Using 1950-2000 U.S. Census Data" (*Social Forces* 88(2), 2009, 865-891), use fixed-effects models on half a century of US Census data to show that as an occupation becomes more female, its pay falls, controlling for education and skill, and that the effect did not attenuate across the period. Wenhao Jiang's "The Cultural Devaluation of Feminized Work: The Evolution of U.S. Occupational Prestige and Gender Typing in Linguistic Representations, 1900 to 2019" (*American Sociological Review*, 2025) extends the finding to prestige, using computational text analysis to show that feminization triggers a linguistic downgrade independent of wage change. Marie Hicks's *Programmed Inequality: How Britain Discarded Women Technologists and Lost Its Edge in Computing* (MIT Press, 2017) provides a single-case counterpart, documenting how Britain's computing workforce was structurally degraded once it became associated with women, with measurable costs to national competitiveness.

These findings resonate with broader descriptive statistics: the McKinsey and LeanIn.Org *Women in the Workplace* series (annually since 2015) on the "broken rung" from entry to manager; Blau and Kahn, "The Gender Wage Gap: Extent, Trends, and Explanations" (*Journal of Economic Literature* 55(3), 2017), on the stubborn residual of the US pay gap after observable controls. England, in "The Gender Revolution: Uneven and Stalled" (*Gender & Society* 24(2), 2010), argues that the revolution in gender roles has stalled precisely at the places where devaluation operates: the feminization of occupations, the gendering of care, the asymmetry of household labor.

The explicit bridge between these findings and the philosophical structural claim is made most directly by England, Folbre, Federici, Fausto-Sterling, Dusenbery, and Hicks. Their argument is that the devaluation cycle, the broken rung, and the pain-treatment gap are not random frictions but consistent with the precise exclusions the canonical tradition institutionalized: women's labor is valued less because it remains classified as reproductive or domestic in origin; women's reasoning is trusted less in clinical encounters because the 19th-century "hysterical" body is still a live category; the public sphere is harder to enter because its implicit subject retains a specific embodiment.

Goldin and others in the labor-economics mainstream accept the patterns but prefer explanations that do not route through a philosophical genealogy: compensating differentials, fertility timing, the architecture of long-hours jobs. The disagreement, then, is not mainly over what is happening now but over why, and whether the "why" runs through Rousseau, Kant, Hegel, and their institutional heirs.

What the literature taken together supports is a moderate but substantive version of the structural inheritance claim: the institutions under discussion were built with the gender ideology of their era as a load-bearing element, that ideology was articulated in part by canonical philosophers whose names still organize the relevant disciplines, and the institutional categories, the citizen, the legal person, the rational knower, the productive worker, the normal patient, continue to carry its imprint in patterns that are measurable. Whether that inheritance is reformable within the existing frameworks, or only by displacing them, is the live dispute.
