# Kant

Immanuel Kant (1724-1804) is the pivotal figure of modern moral philosophy, and more than any other philosopher in the Western canon, he shapes the normative vocabulary now used to argue for universal human dignity, autonomy, and rights. He is also the author of a substantial body of published claims about women, race, and social rank that, on any ordinary reading, are incompatible with those same values. This literature review surveys what Kant wrote about women, how the major schools of secondary scholarship read him on that question, and what is live in the debate as of the late 2020s.

## Primary texts and what he actually said

Kant's published comments on women span his pre-critical, critical, and late-critical periods.

In *Observations on the Feeling of the Beautiful and Sublime* (1764), section three, titled "Of the distinction of the beautiful and the sublime in the counter-relation of the two sexes," Kant classifies women with the beautiful and men with the sublime or "noble." Women, he writes, have "a strong inborn feeling for everything that is beautiful, refined, and decorated," while the understanding proper to women is "a beautiful understanding" rather than the "deep understanding" proper to men (Kant 1764/2011, 2:229-230). He states that women should not learn geometry: "laborious learning or painful pondering, even if a woman should greatly succeed in them, destroy the merits that are proper to her sex" (2:229). Virtue itself, in the female case, is a "beautiful virtue" grounded in sentiment rather than principle; male virtue is "noble virtue" grounded in reason (2:231-232).

In *Anthropology from a Pragmatic Point of View* (1798), Part II, "On the character of the sexes" (§§48-49, 7:303-311 in the Akademie pagination used by the Cambridge edition), Kant returns to these themes in more systematic form. He holds that "the woman should reign and the man govern; for inclination reigns and understanding governs" (7:309). Woman's "character" is defined in terms of the preservation of the species and of culture through refinement; she is assigned "civil" rather than "active" citizenship, and her proper sphere is the domestic. In the 1797 *Metaphysics of Morals*, the Doctrine of Right (§§24-27, 6:277-280), Kant defines marriage as "the union of two persons of different sexes for lifelong reciprocal possession of their sexual attributes" and insists that, in the legal order of the household, the wife stands under the husband's direction, though he tries to soften this by insisting the relationship is one of reciprocal "possession." Married women, servants, children, apprentices, and day-laborers are classified as "passive" citizens, possessing civil personality but not the vote (6:314-315).

The third Critique, the *Critique of the Power of Judgment* (1790), does not contain a focused section on women, but the aesthetic framework of beauty versus sublimity developed there maps onto the gendered schema of the 1764 *Observations* in ways Kant never disavows (Schott 1997, introduction). The student-transcribed *Lectures on Anthropology* (Ak. 25) extend the published remarks, often more bluntly: women are described as unsuited to scholarship, naturally cunning, and oriented by inclination rather than duty (Louden 2000, 86-92).

These are not fugitive asides. They appear in works Kant prepared for publication at the height of his critical project, and they are internally cross-referenced. Any interpretation must reckon with that.

## The structural reading

The structural reading holds that Kant's views on women are not detachable from his moral philosophy because the very categories that do the work in the critical ethics (rational agency, autonomy, the capacity to give oneself universal law) are constructed in explicit contrast to femininity.

Robin May Schott's edited collection *Feminist Interpretations of Immanuel Kant* (1997) gathered the founding generation of this argument. In her introduction and her own chapter, Schott argues that Kant's account of reason requires the suppression of embodiment, sentiment, and particularity, and that he offloads these suppressed contents onto women, thereby constituting the rational subject as implicitly male (Schott 1997, 1-15, 319-339). Hannelore Schröder's chapter in the same volume, "Kant's Patriarchal Order" (Schröder 1997, 275-296), argues that the juridical structure of the *Metaphysics of Morals*, in particular the passive-citizen doctrine and the marriage law, treats women as property-like objects of a contract they cannot themselves fully author. Jean P. Rumsey's contribution argues that Kant's developmental account of moral agency effectively excludes women by definition: the capacity for autonomous principled action is what Kant denies them (Rumsey 1997, 125-144).

Carole Pateman's *The Sexual Contract* (1988), though not exclusively about Kant, supplies the broader framework. Pateman argues that the social contract tradition is doubled by a sexual contract that installs male dominion over women in the household and in the civil order. Kant's treatment of marriage in the Doctrine of Right is her paradigm case: the "reciprocal possession" formulation, she contends, is a legal fiction that in fact encodes patriarchal property relations (Pateman 1988, ch. 6). Charles Mills, in *The Racial Contract* (1997) and later in "Black Radical Kantianism" (Mills 2018), extends the analytical move to race, arguing that Kantian universalism is "Herrenvolk universalism," nominally universal but effectively bounded by the categories of white and male (Mills 1997, 56-72).

More recent work continues the structural line. Sarah Clark Miller, though ultimately a reconstructionist, accepts that the traditional Kantian duty of beneficence is blind to relations of dependency and to the embodied needs of care (Miller 2012, chs. 1-3). Inder Marwah's "What Nature Makes of Her: Kant's Gendered Metaphysics" (2013) argues, on the basis of the anthropology and teleology, that for Kant women must adopt "an explicitly nonmoral character" if the species is to move toward its moral end, meaning gender hierarchy is a condition of the moral-historical narrative rather than an incidental prejudice (Marwah 2013, 551-567).

The structural reading thus turns on three linked claims: that Kant's active rational subject is constructed through the exclusion of the properties he assigns to women, that this exclusion is written into the juridical architecture of his political philosophy, and that it is teleologically required by his philosophy of history.

## The separability or charitable reading

The separability reading concedes that Kant held sexist views but argues that those views are logically independent of the foundational commitments of his moral theory, and that the universalist logic of the categorical imperative in fact provides the resources to criticize them.

Allen Wood's *Kant's Ethical Thought* (1999) and his later *Kantian Ethics* (2008) are the most developed statements of this position. Wood holds that Kant's specific claims about women belong to his "impure" or empirical anthropology, not to the a priori part of the moral theory, and that Kant himself would have been committed on his own principles to repudiating them had he reasoned consistently (Wood 1999, 4-8, 228-234). Thomas E. Hill Jr. makes a similar move in *Dignity and Practical Reason in Kant's Moral Theory* (1992) and in *Respect, Pluralism, and Justice* (2000), arguing that the Formula of Humanity, read as a demand to treat every rational agent as an end in itself, straightforwardly condemns the patriarchal uses to which Kant put it.

Onora O'Neill's *Constructions of Reason* (1989) offers a methodological version of the argument. Reading Kantian reason as constructivist rather than as a set of metaphysical claims about rational subjects, O'Neill argues that the content of Kantian ethics is determined by what policies agents could in principle share, which does not presuppose any particular anthropology. Her subsequent work on hunger, trust, and bioethics (O'Neill 1996, 2002) applies this constructive Kantianism to questions of global justice in ways the historical Kant could not have formulated. Christine Korsgaard's *Creating the Kingdom of Ends* (1996) and *Self-Constitution* (2009) take the autonomy framework to be fundamentally about the self-authorship of any rational agent, which by its own logic cannot be gender-restricted.

Robert Louden's *Kant's Impure Ethics* (2000) sits between the two camps. Louden reconstructs Kant's empirical anthropology in detail, acknowledges its sexism and racism, and argues that Kant's species-level teleology still commits him to the eventual inclusion of everyone in moral progress (Louden 2000, 93-105). Critics of the charitable reading often press on exactly this point: the teleology may name everyone as an endpoint, but the path runs through gendered and racialized hierarchy.

## Reconstruction: neo-Kantian feminism and Kleingeld's second-thoughts thesis

A third body of work neither separates Kant's sexism cleanly from his ethics nor treats it as defeating the ethics. Instead it reconstructs Kantian resources for feminist use.

Carol Hay's *Kantianism, Liberalism, and Feminism: Resisting Oppression* (2013) is the most programmatic statement. Hay argues that a properly understood Kantian duty to respect one's own rational nature generates a prima facie obligation on the oppressed to resist their own oppression, and that the liberal framework of autonomy is indispensable rather than dispensable for feminism (Hay 2013, chs. 2, 4, 5). Lara Denis, in "From Friendship to Marriage: Revising Kant" (2001) and "Kant on the Wrongness of 'Unnatural' Sex" (1999), concedes the heteronormativity and patriarchy of Kant's sexual ethic but argues that Kant's own concept of friendship, extended consistently, undermines those conclusions. Helga Varden, in "Kant and Women" (2017) and *Sex, Love, and Gender: A Kantian Theory* (2020), mounts a sympathetic-but-critical reading that distinguishes Kant's anthropological remarks from his moral-theoretical commitments and argues that embodiment and care can be integrated into a Kantian framework without abandoning it.

Pauline Kleingeld's "Kant's Second Thoughts on Race" (2007), published in *The Philosophical Quarterly*, reframes the broader debate. Kleingeld argues, on textual grounds, that Kant substantially revised his views on race between the 1780s and the 1790s, dropping the hierarchical racial taxonomy and endorsing a more genuinely egalitarian cosmopolitanism (Kleingeld 2007, 573-592). If the second-thoughts thesis is correct for race, the question arises whether something similar might be said of gender. The consensus so far is that the textual evidence is weaker in the gender case: Kant's 1797 and 1798 works still classify married women as passive citizens and still map beauty-sublimity onto the sexes, so there is no parallel recantation (Kleingeld 2019, "On Dealing with Kant's Sexism and Racism"). Kleingeld herself is careful about this asymmetry.

The reconstructionist position is therefore not that Kant was secretly a feminist, but that the categorical-imperative framework, the Formula of Humanity, and the political ideal of a kingdom of ends are powerful enough to be turned against their author. Whether that turning counts as a vindication or as a symptom of the original problem is exactly what is at stake.

## Institutional and intellectual inheritance

The institutional stakes are large because Kant's vocabulary is the operating language of several live normative systems. Autonomy-based medical ethics, articulated in Beauchamp and Childress's *Principles of Biomedical Ethics* and in Onora O'Neill's *Autonomy and Trust in Bioethics* (2002), descends directly from Kant. The modern human rights framework, as historians such as Samuel Moyn have noted, draws on a Kantian vocabulary of dignity even where it does not cite Kant by name. Cosmopolitanism in international political theory, from Habermas to Rawls to Kleingeld herself, takes Kant's "Perpetual Peace" as a canonical text. Deontological reasoning in constitutional law and in just-war theory operates within Kantian parameters.

Feminist political theorists who take the structural view argue that these inheritances carry the gendered foundations forward. Pateman (1988) and Mills (1997) both argue that contemporary contractualism reproduces the exclusions of the classical contract tradition unless those exclusions are explicitly named and reworked. Eva Feder Kittay's *Love's Labor* (1999) argues that liberal theories of justice grounded in Kantian autonomy systematically under-theorize dependency work, which remains gendered. Martha Fineman's vulnerability jurisprudence makes a related argument in legal theory. Miller (2012) and Kittay between them press the claim that the autonomous rational agent of bioethics and rights discourse is an abstraction that masks, rather than addresses, the gendered distribution of care.

The reconstructionist camp responds that these same institutions have been, and can continue to be, extended by immanent Kantian argument: the inclusion of women and formerly colonized peoples in the scope of dignity-bearing agents is a Kantian outcome achieved against the historical Kant, not against his framework (Hay 2013; Varden 2020).

## Live debates

Four debates structure the current literature.

First, the applicability of the Kleingeld second-thoughts thesis to gender. Kleingeld's 2007 case for a late Kantian revision on race is now widely accepted. Whether any analogous revision occurred on gender is contested, and most specialists, including Kleingeld, conclude that it did not (Kleingeld 2019; Varden 2017). This matters because it blocks one of the easier separability moves.

Second, the neo-Kantian feminist project itself. Hay (2013), Denis (2001), and Varden (2020) maintain that Kantian resources are indispensable for feminism. Pateman (1988), Mills (1997, 2018), and contributors to Schott (1997) maintain that Kantian universalism was always exclusive and that the project of reconstruction risks laundering that exclusion. The dispute is partly empirical, about what the texts say, and partly methodological, about what it means to use a historical framework.

Third, the status of the anthropology. Louden (2000) and Wood (1999) treat the *Anthropology* and the *Observations* as empirical writings that Kant's own critical philosophy demotes relative to the pure moral law. Schott (1997), Marwah (2013), and Mills (2018) treat them as load-bearing, arguing that Kant's own statements in the third Critique and the Doctrine of Right make the anthropology constitutive of the moral philosophy rather than supplementary to it.

Fourth, the question of how to teach and cite Kant in the 2020s. This has become a genuine disciplinary debate, addressed in Kleingeld (2019) and in symposia in *Kantian Review* and *Hypatia*. The range of proposals runs from business as usual, to systematic contextualization, to partial decanonization.

A neutral summary is possible. Everyone in the literature accepts that Kant wrote what he wrote about women. The disagreement is about whether those writings are an embarrassment that the framework can shed, a feature of the framework that can be turned against itself, or a structural commitment that the framework cannot coherently disown. The scholarly question has not been settled, and the distribution of views does not track any obvious political alignment.
