# The separability vs. structural debate

When a canonical philosopher writes that women lack the capacity for principled moral judgment, or that the rational subject is implicitly male, what should a contemporary reader do with that text? The question sounds simple. The literature is not. For roughly four decades, scholars across analytic, continental, feminist, and historical traditions have argued about whether the sexism (and racism) found in the canon is **separable** from the philosophy proper, **structural** to the systems those philosophers built, or something more complicated. The dispute is methodological before it is substantive: it concerns what reading a historical philosophical text *is for*, what counts as charitable interpretation, and which exclusions in the tradition were incidental versus load-bearing. This review maps the major positions, the key theorists, and the institutional stakes, without endorsing any one side.

## The separability / charitable position

The separability view holds, in its strongest form, that a philosopher's prejudices and their philosophical arguments are logically distinct, and that the arguments can be evaluated, repaired, and extended without dragging the prejudices along. The view rests on three pillars: a principle of charity in interpretation, a commitment to rational reconstruction, and a particular picture of what the history of philosophy is *for*.

In Kant scholarship, Allen W. Wood has been the most sustained defender of a charitable reading. In *Kant's Ethical Thought* (1999) and elsewhere, Wood argues that Kant's universalist moral architecture (the dignity of rational nature, the formula of humanity) provides resources that condemn Kant's own empirical prejudices about women and non-Europeans more sharply than any external critique can. On this view, the prejudices are local empirical errors grafted onto a universalist system, not entailments of it.

Christine Korsgaard's neo-Kantian reconstruction in *The Sources of Normativity* (1996) and *Self-Constitution* (2009) goes further. Korsgaard treats the Kantian apparatus as a live theoretical resource: she rebuilds it from the ground up around the concept of practical identity, deliberately discarding Kant's anthropological commitments. The reconstruction does not pretend Kant was right about women; it claims that nothing essential to the moral theory required his being right about them.

Onora O'Neill's constructivism (*Constructions of Reason*, 1989; *Towards Justice and Virtue*, 1996) makes a related move at the level of method. O'Neill argues that Kantian universalizability is a procedure for testing maxims, and that its abstraction from particular empirical features (including the empirical features Kant himself attributed to women) is precisely what makes it usable.

In analytic philosophy more broadly, the principle of charity associated with Donald Davidson ("On the Very Idea of a Conceptual Scheme," 1974; *Inquiries into Truth and Interpretation*, 1984) and Quine functions as a background norm: an interpretation that makes a text's central claims come out reasonable is, all else equal, preferable to one that makes them come out absurd. Applied to history, this norm tilts readers toward reconstructions that maximize the rational core.

Historical contextualism cuts a slightly different path to a similar destination. Quentin Skinner (*Visions of Politics*, vol. 1, 2002) and J.G.A. Pocock (*Virtue, Commerce, and History*, 1985) argue that political and philosophical texts must be read as interventions in the debates of their own moment, in their own vocabularies. The contextualist is wary of anachronism in *both* directions: it is as much a mistake to credit Hobbes with twenty-first-century liberalism as to convict him of failing it. J.B. Schneewind's *The Invention of Autonomy* (1998) exemplifies how contextualism can recover the genuine novelty of a thinker's contribution while bracketing prejudices the surrounding culture took for granted.

Richard Rorty's "The Historiography of Philosophy: Four Genres" (in *Philosophy in History*, eds. Rorty, Schneewind, and Skinner, 1984) provides the most useful taxonomy for this whole debate. Rorty distinguishes **historical reconstruction** (what did the philosopher mean, in their own terms?), **rational reconstruction** (what is the best argument that can be assembled from their premises?), **Geistesgeschichte** (what tradition does this thinker belong to, and what does that tradition tell us about ourselves?), and doxography. Rorty's point is that these are different projects with different criteria of success, and conflating them generates pseudo-disagreements. A defender of separability can occupy the rational-reconstructive ground without denying that historical reconstruction reveals a sexist Kant.

The arguments supporting this camp converge on a few claims: charity is a precondition of interpretation; a philosopher's empirical errors do not automatically infect their formal apparatus; the canon contains intellectual resources that have repeatedly been turned against the prejudices their authors held; and demanding moral purity from historical authors risks making philosophy impossible.

## The structural / inseparability position

The structural reading rejects the picture of prejudice as a removable contaminant. Its strongest claim is not that historical philosophers were biased (everyone concedes that) but that their sexism and racism do **specific philosophical work** inside their systems. Remove it and the system does not run, or runs differently than its defenders advertise.

Carole Pateman's *The Sexual Contract* (1988) is the founding text. Pateman argues that the social contract tradition (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Kant) presupposes a prior **sexual contract** in which men's authority over women is established before the political contract among men is even possible. The "individual" of contract theory is not a neutral abstraction; it is implicitly the male head of household, and the apparent universality of contractarian language is purchased by relegating women to a non-contractual domestic sphere. On Pateman's reading, you cannot simply add women to the contract and stir; the contract's structure depends on their exclusion.

Charles W. Mills extends this analysis to race in *The Racial Contract* (1997) and *Black Rights/White Wrongs* (2017). Mills's central methodological claim is that liberal contractarianism is structured by an unspoken racial contract that defines the class of full persons. In *Black Rights/White Wrongs*, Mills argues explicitly that the sexual and racial contracts are not parallel critiques but **structurally integrated** domination contracts. Mills also pioneered "non-ideal theory" in mainstream analytic political philosophy, arguing that ideal theory abstracts from the very injustices it ought to be theorizing.

Genevieve Lloyd's *The Man of Reason* (1984) makes the parallel argument for the philosophy of mind and epistemology. Lloyd traces how, from the Greeks through Descartes, Hume, and Hegel, "Reason" was constructed in opposition to the feminine: the rational is what transcends the bodily, the emotional, the particular, and these poles were systematically gendered. The maleness of reason on this account is not a stray remark but a constitutive symbolic structure that organizes what counts as knowledge.

Susan Moller Okin's *Women in Western Political Thought* (1979) anticipates many of these arguments by showing, philosopher by philosopher, that the treatment of women is not a marginal afterthought but a load-bearing element of the political theory. Okin's later *Justice, Gender, and the Family* (1989) carries the critique into Rawls.

Robin May Schott's *Cognition and Eros: A Critique of the Kantian Paradigm* (1988) and her edited *Feminist Interpretations of Kant* (1997) argue that Kant's separation of pure reason from inclination is itself gendered, and that the gendering does philosophical work in his epistemology and aesthetics. Nancy Tuana, as series editor of Penn State's *Re-Reading the Canon* (the *Feminist Interpretations* series, beginning in the 1990s), has institutionalized this method across more than two dozen volumes covering nearly every major figure.

Sally Haslanger's ideology critique in *Resisting Reality* (2012) provides the contemporary analytic backbone for the structural reading. Haslanger argues that social categories like gender and race are constituted by ideological practices, and that philosophical concepts inherited from a tradition shaped by those practices carry the ideology forward whether or not individual users intend it. Kristie Dotson's work on epistemic oppression (e.g., "Conceptualizing Epistemic Oppression," *Social Epistemology*, 2014) and Myisha Cherry's writing on ideal theory press the same point: theories that bracket structural injustice for the sake of tractability tend to reproduce it.

The arguments supporting this camp share a structure. First, a diagnostic claim: the prejudice is not in the margins but in the architecture. Second, a methodological claim: a reconstruction that excises the prejudice without attending to the work it did is not a charitable reading but an evasive one. Third, a political claim: because these systems shaped modern institutions, the philosophical work the prejudice did continues to do work in the world.

## Reconstruction and middle positions

Several major theorists occupy the space between separability and structural critique, often by historicizing both.

Pauline Kleingeld's "Kant's Second Thoughts on Race" (*Philosophical Quarterly*, 2007) and "Kant's Second Thoughts on Colonialism" (in *Kant and Colonialism*, eds. Flikschuh and Ypi, 2014) show that Kant himself revised his views on race late in life. The argument is methodologically interesting because it complicates both poles: the structural reader cannot treat Kant's racism as a fixed system feature if Kant changed his mind, and the separability reader cannot treat the early racism as merely external to the philosophy if the late revisions involve substantive philosophical work.

Nancy Fraser's *Fortunes of Feminism* (2013) and earlier *Justice Interruptus* (1997) offer a Hegelian-reconstructive model: feminist theory should appropriate the categories of recognition and redistribution from a tradition (Hegel, Marx) whose own treatment of gender was inadequate, but should rebuild those categories around the experience the tradition excluded. The reconstruction is neither separation nor wholesale rejection.

Seyla Benhabib's *Situating the Self* (1992) develops a similar move out of Habermasian discourse ethics, arguing that the universalist procedural framework can be saved if it is reframed around the "concrete other" rather than the abstract generalized other. The structural critique is taken seriously; the universalist project is not abandoned.

Carol Hay's *Kantianism, Liberalism, and Feminism* (2013) defends a feminist Kantianism that takes Pateman's critique seriously and argues that Kantian respect for autonomy generates an obligation to resist one's own oppression. Hay's project is openly reconstructive: she does not deny Kant's sexism, she argues that Kant's own framework, properly extended, demands its repudiation.

Sally Haslanger's "amelioration" or "ameliorative analysis" (developed across *Resisting Reality* and elsewhere) offers a methodological middle path at the level of concepts: rather than asking what a concept *means* descriptively or what its historical use *was*, ask what concept we *should* use given what we want the concept to do. Ameliorative analysis can be deployed in either separabilist or structuralist directions, but it shifts the question from textual fidelity to political adequacy.

## Methodology of reading historical texts (Skinner, Rorty, Foucault, Baier)

Beneath the substantive debate sits a methodological one about what reading a historical philosophical text is for.

Skinner's contextualism (*Visions of Politics*, 2002; "Meaning and Understanding in the History of Ideas," *History and Theory*, 1969) insists on recovering authorial intention within a linguistic context. The contextualist reads to understand, not to use; presentist appropriation (whether to celebrate or to indict) is a category error.

Rorty's four genres (1984) cut across this. Rational reconstruction is unapologetically presentist and aims at our problems. Historical reconstruction aims at theirs. Geistesgeschichte tells a story about how we got from there to here. Each is legitimate; confusion arises when practitioners of one genre criticize practitioners of another for not doing their job.

Foucault's genealogy (*The Archaeology of Knowledge*, 1969; *Discipline and Punish*, 1975; "Nietzsche, Genealogy, History," 1971) reframes the question entirely. Genealogy does not ask whether a philosopher's claims were true but how a discourse became sayable, what it made possible, and what subjects it produced. For genealogical readers, the sexism in the canon is data about the formation of modern subjectivity, not a defect to be repaired or excused.

Annette Baier's *Postures of the Mind* (1985) and *A Progress of Sentiments* (1991) defend a humanistic conception of the history of philosophy as a moral resource. Baier argues, against both pure contextualism and pure rational reconstruction, that we read historical philosophers because thinking with them changes how we think, and that this use is compatible with sustained criticism of their blind spots.

## Feminist philosophy as corrective (Fricker, Haslanger, Manne)

A distinct strand argues that feminist philosophy is not a supplement to the canon but a required corrective, because the canon's exclusions produced systematic distortions in what counts as philosophy at all.

Miranda Fricker's *Epistemic Injustice* (2007) introduces the concepts of testimonial injustice (deflated credibility based on identity prejudice) and hermeneutical injustice (gaps in collective interpretive resources caused by the marginalization of certain groups). Applied to the history of philosophy, Fricker's framework predicts both that women's philosophical contributions were under-credited and that concepts needed to articulate women's experience were never developed.

Sally Haslanger's *Resisting Reality* (2012) argues that ideology operates through unmarked default categories, and that a philosophy that fails to interrogate its defaults reproduces the ideology whether or not the philosopher endorses it. The implication is methodological: feminist analysis is a form of conceptual hygiene the discipline cannot do without.

Kate Manne's *Down Girl: The Logic of Misogyny* (2017) distinguishes misogyny (the enforcement mechanism) from sexism (the justifying ideology) and argues that mainstream moral philosophy lacked the conceptual resources to see this distinction because of who did the theorizing.

Elizabeth Anderson's "democratic" social epistemology (e.g., "The Epistemology of Democracy," *Episteme*, 2006) and Amia Srinivasan's *The Right to Sex* (2021) extend the corrective project: epistemic communities reach better conclusions when previously excluded perspectives are included, and the inclusion is not optional charity but an epistemic condition.

The shared argument: a tradition built without the contributions of half the population is not a neutral baseline to which feminist concerns are added; it is an artifact of exclusion whose distortions need correction.

## Counter-arguments to the structural reading

The separability camp has serious arguments that deserve to be stated at full strength.

**Anachronism.** Holding historical authors to contemporary standards risks failing to understand them on their own terms (Skinner). It can also obscure genuine moral progress by making it look as if moderns simply have correct views rather than having argued our way to them.

**The genetic fallacy.** The fact that an argument was produced by a person with bad views does not, by itself, refute the argument. To say otherwise is to commit the genetic fallacy. The structural reader replies that the claim is not genetic but architectural, but separabilists argue the architectural claim is often asserted rather than demonstrated.

**Strong dependence theses are hard to prove.** Showing that a philosopher *believed* something sexist is easy. Showing that the formal apparatus of their theory *requires* that sexism, in the sense that the theory cannot be stated or run without it, is much harder. Critics like Wood and Korsgaard argue that many structural readings slide between "Kant believed X" and "Kantianism entails X" without licensing the slide.

**No ground to stand on.** If we reject every philosopher who held views we now find objectionable, very little canon survives, and the standards we use to judge them are themselves products of that canon. The structural reader has answers (we are not rejecting, we are reconstructing; the standards are revisable; the tradition contains its own internal critics) but the worry is real.

**Ameliorative versus descriptive aims.** Rational reconstruction, openly presentist, can sidestep some structural critiques by acknowledging from the start that it is doing philosophy, not history. Whether this concedes too much is itself contested.

## Institutional stakes and the sociology of philosophy

The methodological debate is not academic in the dismissive sense. It determines syllabi, hiring, citation patterns, and which problems count as philosophical.

The data on women in philosophy are well-documented. Sally Haslanger's "Changing the Ideology and Culture of Philosophy: Not by Reason (Alone)" (*Hypatia*, 2008) showed that philosophy has the lowest representation of women of any humanities discipline, comparable to the physical sciences. Jennifer Saul's edited volume *Implicit Bias and Philosophy* (2016, with Michael Brownstein) and her work on "stereotype threat" examine the mechanisms. Kristie Dotson's "How Is This Paper Philosophy?" (*Comparative Philosophy*, 2012) analyzes how disciplinary norms about what counts as philosophy systematically exclude work by women of color.

Eric Schliesser's work on the history of the profession (e.g., his contributions to the *Digressions & Impressions* blog and *Sympathy: A History*, 2015) examines how the canon was constructed institutionally, often through deliberate narrowing in the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries. The implication is that "the canon" is not a natural kind but a curatorial product, and curatorial products can be revised.

Public-facing debates on the Philosophers' Cocoon, Daily Nous, and at APA sessions over the past fifteen years have repeatedly returned to these questions: who is on the syllabus, who gets cited, what counts as a "core" area, and whether feminist philosophy is a subfield or a methodological orientation that should reshape the core.

What hangs on the methodological question, then, is concrete. If the separability view is right, the canon needs supplementing. If the structural view is right, the canon needs rebuilding. If the reconstructive middle is right, the work of rebuilding is itself a continuation of the tradition's own best impulses. None of these positions is silly, and the live disagreement among serious scholars is part of what makes the question worth pursuing.
