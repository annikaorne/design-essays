# §3a — Women's credibility in professional settings

> Literature review. Neutral posture. Western (US + Europe) focus. Contemporary academic sources with foundational studies cited where essential.

## Key findings

### 1. The Heidi vs. Howard case and identical-resume experiments

The "Heidi/Howard" finding comes from a Columbia Business School teaching case built on Flynn and Anderson's manipulation of Kathleen McGinn and Nicole Tempest's 2000 HBS case on venture capitalist Heidi Roizen (Flynn and Anderson 2003). MBA students read the case with either the original name ("Heidi") or a male name ("Howard"). Both were rated equally competent, but Howard was rated more likable and a more desirable colleague; Heidi was rated more selfish and "not the type of person you would want to hire or work for." The study is small and classroom-based, but it crystallized a finding that has since been replicated in more rigorous designs: successful women in agentic roles incur a social penalty (see "agentic penalty" below). A partial conceptual replication by Bohnet, van Geen, and Bazerman (2016) confirmed that individual evaluation (versus joint evaluation) amplifies gender-stereotypic judgments.

### 2. Goldin and Rouse's blind auditions — and the replication debate

Goldin and Rouse (2000), published in the *American Economic Review*, used a natural experiment in which major US orchestras adopted screens during auditions between the 1970s and 1990s. They reported that blind auditions "explain about 30 percent" of the increase in women hired and increased the probability a female candidate advanced past preliminary rounds. The study became one of the most-cited empirical demonstrations of gender bias in evaluation.

In 2019, Jonatan Pallesen and others (summarized in Andrew Gelman's *Statistical Modeling, Causal Inference, and Social Science* blog and Christina Hoff Sommers' commentary) argued that Goldin and Rouse's own regression tables do not consistently support the headline finding: some coefficients are statistically insignificant, others point the opposite direction, and the sample sizes in key cells are small. No formal peer-reviewed replication has overturned the paper, but the magnitude of the effect is now treated as uncertain. Goldin and Rouse have not retracted; the paper remains in the canon. The honest contemporary summary: the intervention (blind auditions) is directionally consistent with bias reduction, but the precise 30 percent figure is contested and the underlying estimates are fragile (Sommers 2019; Gelman 2019).

This matters because Goldin-Rouse is often cited as a uniquely clean identification of evaluator bias. The critique does not dissolve the broader literature — it narrows what this single study can prove.

### 3. Prove-it-again bias and double standards in evaluation

Joan C. Williams and the Center for WorkLife Law have documented what Williams calls the "four patterns" of gender bias at work, of which "Prove It Again" is the most evaluated. Williams, Phillips, and Hall (2014/2015) surveyed 557 women scientists and found that two-thirds reported having to provide more evidence of competence than male colleagues; Black women reported the highest rates. Williams and Dempsey's *What Works for Women at Work* (2014) synthesizes interview data from 127 successful women and maps the pattern across industries.

Independent experimental work supports the qualitative finding. Moss-Racusin et al. (2012), in *PNAS*, randomly assigned science faculty to evaluate a student application packet with either a male or female name attached. Faculty of both genders rated the male applicant as more competent, more hireable, and worthy of a higher starting salary (about $4,000 more). Reuben, Sapienza, and Zingales (2014), also in *PNAS*, showed that in a math task, employers predicted men would perform better than women by a factor of about two to one, even when actual performance was identical, and even when candidates truthfully reported their past performance.

### 4. Performance-review language

Kieran Snyder's 2014 *Fortune* analysis of 248 performance reviews from 180 people in tech companies found that women received critical feedback in 87.9 percent of reviews versus 58.9 percent for men, and that the word "abrasive" appeared 17 times across 13 women's reviews and never for men. Snyder's sample was a non-random convenience sample and has not been peer reviewed, but subsequent peer-reviewed work has confirmed the pattern. Correll and Simard (2016) at Stanford's Clayman Institute analyzed 200 reviews from a tech firm and found women were 1.4 times more likely than men to receive critical subjective feedback and more likely to have their personalities, rather than their work, critiqued. Smith, Rosenstein, and Nikolov (2018), using a large dataset of military fitness reports, found that men were more likely to be described with "competent" attributes and women with "compassionate" ones, even controlling for performance ratings.

The EEOC Select Task Force report on Harassment in the Workplace (Feldblum and Lipnic 2016) also flagged performance evaluation as a site of gender-differentiated standards, particularly in male-dominated industries.

### 5. Trans men's post-transition credibility (Schilt)

Kristen Schilt's *Just One of the Guys?* (2010) synthesizes interviews with 54 trans men in the US workforce. The consistent finding: respondents reported being taken more seriously after transitioning. They described being interrupted less, having their ideas repeated back to them and credited, and being seen as more competent — often for the same work they had been doing before. A subset reported receiving raises, promotions, or respect from peers that had been absent pre-transition. Schilt (2006, *Gender & Society*) formalized this as "patriarchal dividends" accruing to men regardless of when masculinity is acquired.

Methodologically, the study is qualitative and focused on white trans men (Schilt is explicit that trans men of color, particularly Black trans men, reported a different and more complicated experience, sometimes involving increased surveillance and criminalization). But the within-subject design — the same worker doing the same job, observed before and after a gender-presentation change — is an unusually strong naturalistic test of gendered credibility effects. It is consistent with, and arguably a qualitative replication of, the identical-resume experiments cited above.

### 6. Deliberation, speech, and interruption

Karpowitz and Mendelberg's *The Silent Sex* (2014) is the most systematic study of gendered participation in group deliberation. Across more than 100 experimental groups, they found that when women were in the numerical minority and the group used majority rule, women spoke about 75 percent as much as men would have under proportional conditions; their ideas were less often adopted by the group; and they were interrupted more and with more negative affect. Under unanimous-decision rules, or when women were a clear majority, those gaps narrowed or disappeared. The finding suggests the deficit is about group composition and institutional rules, not inherent speech preferences.

Hancock and Rubin (2015), in the *Journal of Language and Social Psychology*, recorded three-person conversations and found men interrupted women significantly more than they interrupted other men, and women were interrupted more than men across conditions.

Jacobi and Schweers (2017), in the *Virginia Law Review*, coded Supreme Court oral arguments from 1990, 2002, and 2015. Female justices were interrupted roughly three times as often as male justices, both by male justices and by male advocates. The gap persisted even as the Court became more gender-balanced. Conservative justices interrupted liberal justices more, but controlling for ideology, the gender effect remained.

Zhao and Gelfand's work on group dynamics (and the broader meta-analytic literature in Leaper and Robnett 2011) reinforces that interruption is not evenly distributed and is modulated by status, gender, and context.

### 7. Stereotype threat in professional performance

Steele and Aronson (1995), in *JPSP*, introduced "stereotype threat": the finding that reminding members of a stereotyped group of the stereotype depresses their performance on diagnostic tasks. Spencer, Steele, and Quinn (1999) applied this to women in math and found that telling women a math test produced gender differences caused their scores to drop relative to a control; removing the cue eliminated the gap.

The meta-analytic picture is now more cautious. Nguyen and Ryan's (2008) meta-analysis of 76 studies found an overall small-to-moderate effect (d around -0.26 for women in math). More recently, Flore and Wicherts (2015) and Shewach, Sackett, and Quraishi (2019) have reported publication bias and argued that the effect in real-world testing contexts is smaller than the lab literature suggests, though not zero. The phenomenon has not been disconfirmed; the claim that stereotype threat fully explains real-world achievement gaps has been weakened.

### 8. The agentic penalty

Rudman and Glick (2001) introduced evidence that women who display agency (assertiveness, competitiveness, self-promotion) are rated as less socially skilled and less hireable than equally agentic men. Rudman, Moss-Racusin, Phelan, and Nauts (2012), in the *Journal of Experimental Social Psychology*, formalized the "status incongruity hypothesis": backlash against agentic women is driven specifically by the perception that they violate prescriptive gender status hierarchies, not merely descriptive stereotypes. Heilman's decades of work (summarized in her 2012 review in *Research in Organizational Behavior*) documents the "lack of fit" model, in which women in masculine-typed roles face either a competence penalty (if seen as feminine) or a likeability penalty (if seen as competent and agentic) — the classic double bind.

Eagly and Karau's (2002) role congruity theory provides the parallel framework for leadership specifically (treated more fully in §3b).

Bowles, Babcock, and Lai (2007) extended the penalty to negotiation: women who initiated salary negotiations were more likely than men to be seen as "demanding" and less likely to be hired, a finding that has been replicated with some contextual moderation (Amanatullah and Morris 2010; Mazei et al. 2015 meta-analysis).

## Synthesis

Across laboratory experiments, field studies, qualitative interviews, and natural experiments (including the unusual case of trans men), a convergent picture emerges: women's contributions in professional settings are systematically discounted, credited less often, interrupted more often, and evaluated against a different standard than men's. The specific mechanisms — descriptive stereotypes (women are communal, not agentic), prescriptive stereotypes (women should be communal), and status beliefs (men are higher-status, therefore more competent by default) — are well theorized, most comprehensively in Cecilia Ridgeway's *Framed by Gender* (2011) and Laurie Rudman and Peter Glick's *The Social Psychology of Gender* (2008/2021).

Ridgeway's contribution is to frame gender as a "primary frame" for social interaction: status beliefs about gender get activated automatically in any cross-sex encounter, biasing attributions of competence before any evidence is in. This framework predicts and explains most of the findings above. It also explains why bias persists in ostensibly meritocratic institutions: meritocracy rhetoric does not deactivate the primary frame; it often protects it from scrutiny (Castilla and Benard 2010, the "paradox of meritocracy").

The scholarship also converges on structural moderators. Numerical minority status worsens the penalty (Kanter 1977's tokenism framework, updated in Karpowitz and Mendelberg). Institutional rules (blind review, structured interviews, consensus decision rules) can reduce it. The presence of other women, and of explicit accountability structures, dampens it.

## Gaps and debates, including replication concerns

1. **Goldin and Rouse**: as discussed, the specific magnitude of the blind-auditions effect is not as solid as the citation record implies. Sommers (2019) and Gelman's analysis have not produced a peer-reviewed refutation, but the paper has not been independently replicated with the original data and specifications. The broader claim that blinding reduces bias in evaluation is supported by other evidence (e.g., Budden et al. 2008 on journal peer review, though that too has been contested); the specific 30-percent figure should be cited with care.

2. **Stereotype threat**: publication bias in the lab literature is well established (Flore and Wicherts 2015; Shewach et al. 2019). The phenomenon is real but smaller than often claimed, and its real-world impact on women's professional performance is probably modest relative to structural evaluation biases.

3. **Snyder's performance-review analysis** is often cited but was a non-peer-reviewed trade-press piece with a convenience sample. Correll and Simard (2016) and Smith et al. (2018) are the peer-reviewed anchors; Snyder should be cited as the original observation, not the definitive evidence.

4. **Heidi vs. Howard** originated as a classroom exercise, not a published experimental study. Bohnet et al. (2016) and the larger Rudman/Heilman experimental tradition are what actually supports the underlying claim. The "Heidi vs. Howard" label is useful shorthand but should not be cited as if it were a standalone empirical paper.

5. **Intersectionality is underpowered in the classic studies.** Most of the canonical experiments (Moss-Racusin, Reuben, Rudman) do not include enough nonwhite candidates to test race-by-gender interactions. Williams et al. (2014) and the *Double Jeopardy?* report (2015) begin to fill that gap, and Rosette, Livingston, and colleagues' work on Black women in leadership (§3b) goes further, but the intersectional evidence base is thinner than the white-woman-versus-white-man base.

6. **Schilt's sample** is qualitative and mostly white. The pattern she documents is striking but should be cited as a rich natural experiment, not a quantitative estimate of effect size.

7. **The agentic penalty appears real but context-dependent.** Recent work (Williams and Tiedens 2016 meta-analysis in *Psychological Bulletin*) finds that dominance displays by women produce negative evaluations, but competence-based assertive behavior does not consistently do so. The "women can't win" framing may be stronger as a meme than the underlying evidence supports.

8. **Generalization across Western contexts.** Most experimental work is US-based. European studies (e.g., the UK Athena SWAN evaluations, Dutch work on hiring discrimination) broadly replicate the patterns but with smaller effect sizes in countries with stronger formal anti-discrimination enforcement.

The overall pattern — that women's professional credibility is systematically discounted in ways that are measurable, replicable in direction if not always in magnitude, and traceable to identifiable mechanisms — is one of the better-supported claims in contemporary social science. The individual studies vary in rigor; the convergent evidence across methods is strong.
