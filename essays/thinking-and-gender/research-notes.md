## The throughline

Across all five sections, the literature converges on a single pattern: **the categories we use to distribute seriousness, competence, and credibility are gendered in structure, not only in application.** This is not a claim about individual prejudice. It is a claim about inherited conceptual architecture that specific philosophers codified, specific institutions hardened, specific cultural shifts moved, and specific measurable outcomes track to this day.

Four mechanisms recur across sections:

1. **Categorical gendering.** A feature of work (creativity, care, soft skills, emotion) or a field (computing, PR, psychology) gets coded as masculine or feminine, and that coding then operates independently of the feature's actual intellectual demand.
2. **Devaluation follows feminization.** When a field tips female, its pay and prestige decline, controlling for skill. This is best documented in Levanon, England, and Allison (2009), replicated in Mandel (2018) and Jiang (2025).
3. **Double standards in evaluation.** The same work, product, or behavior is judged differently depending on the gender of the producer. Documented in identical-resume experiments (Moss-Racusin et al. 2012), performance reviews (Correll and Simard 2016; Smith, Rosenstein, and Nikolov 2018), creativity attribution (Proudfoot, Kay, and Koval 2015), and trans men's before/after workplace experience (Schilt 2010).
4. **The reason/emotion binary is philosophically unstable but culturally entrenched.** Feminist philosophy of emotion (Jaggar 1989; Nussbaum 2001; Ahmed 2004) dismantles it; mainstream institutions and popular culture still enforce it.

## §1 — Creative thinking across disciplines

### Headline findings

The wall between "creative" and "analytical" cognition is historically recent. It consolidates in two moments:

1. **The Romantic construction of the artist** (ca. 1790–1830) as an expressive, emotive genius — a figure Renaissance and early-modern thinkers would not have recognized. Pre-Romantic workshop traditions integrated making and knowing.
2. **The post-war "two cultures" split** (C.P. Snow 1959), which institutionally bifurcated science and the humanities in UK and US universities.

Contemporary cognitive neuroscience (Beaty, Benedek, Silvia, and Schacter 2016; Jung et al. 2013; Beaty and Kenett 2023) finds strikingly similar network dynamics across domains. Divergent thinking, poetry, jazz improvisation, and scientific insight all recruit cooperation between the default mode network and the executive control network. Scientists themselves describe their work in aesthetic and imaginative terms (Hadamard on Poincaré; Einstein; Dirac; Feynman). Philosophers of science (McAllister, Chandrasekhar) argue aesthetic judgment is load-bearing in theory choice. Dunbar's in-vivo lab studies confirm analogical reasoning is the primary mechanism of scientific discovery.

### Gendered twist

On the gender side, the evidence is strong:

- **Proudfoot, Kay, and Koval (2015)**: identical creative output is rated more creative when attributed to a man.
- **Leslie, Cimpian, Meyer, and Freeland (2015)**: fields whose practitioners believe "brilliance" is required show lower female (and lower African American) PhD representation, independent of actual math-intensity.
- **Carli, Alawa, Lee, Zhao, and Kim (2016)**: scientist stereotypes overlap with male stereotypes and diverge from female stereotypes.

### Live debate

Domain-generality (Simonton, Sawyer) vs. domain-specificity (Baer, Kaufman) of creativity. Most researchers are now in a hybrid position; the network-neuroscience evidence points to substantial shared substrates.

**Load-bearing sources:** Beaty et al. 2016 (*TICS*); Leslie et al. 2015 (*Science*); Proudfoot, Kay, Koval 2015 (*Psych Science*); Snow 1959; Root-Bernstein & Root-Bernstein 1999.

## §2.1 — Feminization of marketing and social sciences

### Headline findings

The same empirical pattern recurs across marketing, PR, HR, psychology, sociology, and anthropology: each field tipped from majority-male to majority-female between the 1970s and 2000s, and each shift coincided with declines in pay and prestige.

**The key causal finding** is Levanon, England, and Allison (2009), analyzing the 1950–2000 US Census: the data support "devaluation" (pay falls after feminization) over the reverse "queuing" story (men leave because pay is already falling). Mandel (2018) replicates and qualifies: the devaluation penalty is strongest in high-paid professional occupations and in recent decades. **Jiang 2025** (*American Sociological Review*) is the newest and strongest: combining Census data with linguistic corpora, she shows cultural prestige of occupations (measured in language) declines alongside demographic feminization from 1900 to 2019. This is the best direct evidence that rhetorical re-coding of feminized fields is real, not just anecdotal.

### Field-by-field

- **PR** is the textbook "velvet ghetto": >70% female practitioners, ~20% in top management (Grunig, Hon, Toth 2001; Aldoory and Toth 2002, 2021).
- **Psychology** crossed 50% female doctorates in the late 1980s; ~75% by the 2010s (APA 2017). Women remain underrepresented at full-professor level and in highest-paid subfields.
- **Sociology and anthropology** feminized parallel to psychology; expansion of qualitative, interpretive, gender-focused subfields coincided with relative prestige decline in econ-dominated policy debates.
- **Marketing** is mixed: ~60% female in the workforce, ~50% of CMO seats, but the discipline's self-description migrated from quantitative-economic toward interpretive-emotional (branding, storytelling, empathy) across the same decades.

### Three live explanations (not mutually exclusive)

Devaluation (England), queuing (Reskin & Roos 1990), and pollution theory (Goldin 2002). Jiang (2025) and Mandel (2018) argue they operate on different timescales.

**Load-bearing sources:** Levanon, England, and Allison 2009 (*Social Forces*); Mandel 2018; Jiang 2025 (*ASR*); Grunig, Hon, and Toth 2001; APA 2017.

## §2.2 — Masculinization of engineering and computing

### Headline findings

The mirror case of §2.1. Computing went the other direction: from feminine-coded clerical/support work in the 1940s-50s to masculine-coded, high-status technical profession by the 1970s-80s. The mechanisms are well-documented by three central historians:

- **Ensmenger 2010, *The Computer Boys Take Over*** (MIT): US case. Professionalization, aptitude and personality testing that selected for "antisocial" traits, founding of computer science as an academic discipline.
- **Hicks 2017, *Programmed Inequality*** (MIT): UK case. The British civil service reclassified its majority-female computing workforce into dead-end grades in the 1960s, tried to replace them with scarce male managers, and watched its early computing lead collapse. This is a remarkable natural experiment in what happens when a state systematically forces out its technical workforce on gender grounds.
- **Abbate 2012, *Recoding Gender*** (MIT): links cultural shifts with labor-market structure; Light 1999 ("When Computers Were Women") anchors the earlier story.

### Quantitative spine

Women's share of US CS bachelor's degrees: 13.6% (1970) → **37.1% peak (1984)** → ~18% (2010-11). Computing is the **only major STEM field to have moved backward** on this metric (Camp 1997; AEI 2018).

### Uneven across engineering

NCSES (2023): ~45% women in biomedical engineering vs. ~14% in computer, ~14% in electrical, ~15% in mechanical. This sub-field variation is a natural test of the "it's just the math" story — which fails, since BME requires comparable quantitative work.

**Leslie et al. 2015** (the brilliance-bias finding) explains much of this variation: fields where success is framed as requiring innate genius have fewer women, independent of actual math content.

**Proof of tractability:** Margolis and Fisher (2002) at Carnegie Mellon took women's share of incoming CS majors from 7% (1995) to 42% (2000) through structural curricular reform.

**Load-bearing sources:** Hicks 2017; Ensmenger 2010; Abbate 2012; Light 1999; Leslie et al. 2015; Margolis and Fisher 2002; Cheryan et al. 2009.

## §3a — Women's credibility in professional settings

### Headline findings

The literature is rich and mostly converges. Key findings, ordered by evidential weight:

1. **Identical-resume experiments.** Moss-Racusin et al. 2012 (*PNAS*): science faculty of both genders rate a male applicant more competent, hireable, and worth ~$4,000 more starting salary than an identical female applicant. Reuben, Sapienza, and Zingales 2014 (*PNAS*): in a math task, employers predict men outperform women 2:1, even when actual performance is identical.

2. **Heidi vs. Howard.** Flynn and Anderson 2003 Columbia classroom study based on McGinn and Tempest's 2000 HBS case on Heidi Roizen. Equally competent; Howard more likable and hireable; Heidi "selfish." A classroom study, not a peer-reviewed paper, but the underlying pattern has been confirmed by peer-reviewed work.

3. **Prove-it-again bias.** Williams, Phillips, and Hall (2014/2015): two-thirds of 557 women scientists report needing more evidence of competence than male peers; Black women report highest rates.

4. **Performance reviews.** Snyder 2014 (non-peer-reviewed) and Correll and Simard 2016 (Stanford Clayman Institute); Smith, Rosenstein, and Nikolov 2018 (military fitness reports): women get more personality-focused feedback; men get more competence-focused feedback, controlling for rated performance.

5. **Schilt 2010, *Just One of the Guys?*** — 54 trans men interviewed. The near-universal finding: they were taken more seriously post-transition, interrupted less, and had ideas credited more often, for the same work. Qualitative, white-sample-weighted, but the within-person design is an unusually strong naturalistic test of gendered credibility. Black trans men reported a different and more complicated experience.

6. **Deliberation and interruption.** Karpowitz and Mendelberg 2014 (*The Silent Sex*): women in minority positions under majority rule speak ~75% as much as proportional conditions predict; unanimity rule or female-majority restores parity. Jacobi and Schweers 2017 (*Virginia Law Review*): SCOTUS female justices are interrupted ~3× as often as male justices. Hancock and Rubin 2015 (*JLSP*): men interrupt women more than they interrupt other men.

### Important caveats to flag

- **Goldin-Rouse blind auditions (2000)** is the most-cited study in this literature, but its headline "30% of the increase" figure has been contested since Sommers 2019 and Gelman's analyses. No formal peer-reviewed retraction; the intervention direction is consistent with bias reduction; the precise magnitude is now considered fragile.
- **Stereotype threat** has a mixed meta-analytic record. Flore and Wicherts 2015 and Shewach et al. 2019 find attenuation and publication-bias concerns. The phenomenon is real in specific conditions but less universal than popularized.
- **The agentic penalty** (Rudman et al. 2012; Heilman & Okimoto 2007) is real but moderated. Williams and Tiedens 2016 meta-analysis shows context-dependence.

**Load-bearing sources:** Moss-Racusin et al. 2012; Reuben, Sapienza, Zingales 2014; Schilt 2010; Karpowitz and Mendelberg 2014; Ridgeway 2011.

## §3b — Women in leadership: broken rung, glass cliff, double bind

### Headline findings

1. **The "glass ceiling" has been supplanted by "labyrinth"** (Eagly and Carli 2007, HBR and book). The useful diagnostic: women face interlocking obstacles at many stages, not one terminal barrier.

2. **The broken rung is the biggest leak.** McKinsey/LeanIn *Women in the Workplace* 2024: for every 100 men promoted to manager, **81 women** are promoted (79 in 2018). Sharper by race: 54 Black women and 65 Latinas per 100 men. Because managerial attainment compounds, the broken rung alone explains most of the gap at senior levels.

3. **The glass cliff is real but context-dependent.** Ryan and Haslam 2005 found women disproportionately appointed to FTSE 100 boards during poor performance. Morgenroth et al. 2020 meta-analysis: small overall effect, stronger in political/experimental studies than CEO archival data. Bechtoldt, Bannier, Rock 2019 found no effect in German/UK boards. Honest framing: context-dependent phenomenon, supported in several domains, weaker in others.

4. **Role congruity theory** (Eagly and Karau 2002) gives the mechanism: leader role coded agentic, female role coded communal, incongruity produces the penalty. Heilman and Okimoto 2007 identified the specific driver — "implied communality deficit" — which is attenuated when communal traits (motherhood, helping) are made explicit.

5. **The double bind** (Catalyst 2007): too soft or too tough, rarely right; higher competence bar for lower rewards; competent or likable but not both.

6. **Negotiation penalty.** Bowles, Babcock, Lai 2007: women who initiated negotiations penalized; men who did not were. This is why "just ask" advice cannot close pay gaps alone.

7. **Intersectionality.** Rosette, Koval, Ma, Livingston 2016 (*Leadership Quarterly*): the agentic deficiency and penalty do not distribute evenly. Black women are stereotyped dominant-but-not-competent; Asian American women as competent-but-not-dominant. The compound barrier is the "concrete ceiling."

8. **Quotas work on what they target.** Norway's 40% board quota (2003/2008): Matsa and Miller 2013 find different managerial style, not incompetence; Ahern and Dittmar 2012 find short-term valuation effects. California SB 826 raised female board seats from 15% to 32% before being struck down on equal-protection grounds in 2022. EU Women on Boards Directive (2022) requires 40% non-executive / 33% overall by mid-2026.

9. **VC is the starkest numeric gap.** PitchBook 2024: all-female teams received ~1% of US VC dollars; ~17% of VC decision-makers at $50M+ AUM firms are women.

**Load-bearing sources:** Eagly and Carli 2007; McKinsey/LeanIn 2024; Ryan and Haslam 2005; Eagly and Karau 2002; Rosette et al. 2016; Matsa and Miller 2013; Catalyst 2007.

## §3c — Pay equity

### Headline findings

1. **Current state (2024 data):** US women full-time 83% of men's median weekly earnings (BLS 2025); Pew broader measure 85% (Kochhar 2023; Pew 2025). EU 11.1% unadjusted gap, 2024 (Eurostat 2024). Modest movement since 2003.

2. **Controlled vs. uncontrolled framing.** Controlled residual in US is ~5-8% (Blau and Kahn 2017). Critics (O'Neill and O'Neill 2012; Perry and Biggs 2014) emphasize this number. Response: many of those controls (occupation, hours, tenure) are themselves gendered outcomes.

3. **Goldin's Nobel-recognized argument.** Goldin 2014 (*AER*); *Career and Family* 2021; 2023 Nobel. The remaining gap in high-earning professions is principally generated by **"greedy jobs"** — roles that pay disproportionately per hour for long, inflexible, on-call availability. Because heterosexual couples typically assign the greedy job to the man, couple-level equity is traded away.

4. **The motherhood penalty drives most of the modern gap.**
   - Budig and England 2001: ~5% wage penalty per child net of experience.
   - Correll, Benard, and Paik 2007: audit and lab evidence that employers discount mothers' competence and starting salary; fathers get a small bonus.
   - Kleven, Landais, and Søgaard 2019: the child penalty now accounts for ~80% of Denmark's gender earnings gap.

5. **Negotiation.** Bowles, Babcock, Lai 2007 (as above). Roussille 2024 (*QJE*): in engineering-hire data, a 2.9% "ask gap" generates essentially the full final-offer gap; a UI prefill eliminated it.

6. **Occupational segregation (Blau and Kahn 2017)** and devaluation (Levanon, England, Allison 2009) remain major channels — the same pattern that drives §2.1.

7. **Race.** In 2023: Latinas ~58¢, Black women ~67¢, AANHPI women ~93¢ per white-male dollar; Indigenous women lowest (IWPR 2024).

8. **Pay transparency.** Colorado 2021 Equal Pay for Equal Work Act raised posted and realized pay with gains concentrated among women (Arnold, Quach, Taska 2024, NBER WP 34480). Cullen 2023 meta-review: modest narrowing, 1-2 log points.

**Load-bearing sources:** Goldin 2014, 2021; Blau and Kahn 2017; Correll, Benard, Paik 2007; Levanon, England, Allison 2009; Roussille 2024.

## §4 — 19th–20th century philosophical underpinnings

> **Expanded coverage:** A dedicated deep-dive set of 10 research files lives in `research/philosophers/` covering eight thinkers individually (Rousseau, Kant, Hegel, Schopenhauer, Nietzsche, Freud, Proudhon, Weininger) plus two meta files on the separability debate and institutional inheritance. See `research/philosophers/README.md` for the synthesis. The summary below is the broader chronological sweep; the philosophers folder goes into primary texts, separability vs. structural readings, and reconstruction attempts case by case.

### Headline findings

1. **The "rational subject" was not neutral.** Rousseau, Kant, and Hegel constructed it through explicit and repeated contrast with femininity, associated with particularity, feeling, the domestic, and nature (Okin 1979; Lloyd 1984; Pateman 1988). Kant's *Anthropology* and Rousseau's *Emile* are primary texts here; Hegel's *Philosophy of Right* §166 explicitly locates women in the family, not civil society.

2. **Earliest responses did not reject reason.** Wollstonecraft (1792), J.S. Mill (1869), and Harriet Taylor Mill (1851) argued women's apparent irrationality was the effect of denied education and civil status, not a natural fact.

3. **Separate-spheres ideology got scientific warrant** from evolutionary biology and medicine (Russett 1989; Ehrenreich and English [1978] 2005). Spencer's *Principles of Biology* and the "female complaint" medical literature provided the apparent evidence for exclusion.

4. **Weber's rationalization thesis is implicitly gendered** (Bologh 1990, *Love or Greatness*): Weber's ideal of public greatness requires subordination of brotherly love to instrumental rationality, coding the public/masculine sphere as the site of meaningful action.

5. **Twentieth-century feminist philosophy**, Beauvoir → Lloyd → Pateman → Fraser → Butler → Federici → Srinivasan, argues the apparent neutrality of reason, the social contract, the public sphere, and the wage relation conceal a sexed structure that still shapes institutions.
   - **Beauvoir 1949** — *The Second Sex*, immanence vs. transcendence.
   - **Lloyd 1984** — *The Man of Reason*, reason itself gendered in Western philosophy.
   - **Pateman 1988** — *The Sexual Contract*, social contract as a fraternal pact.
   - **Fraser** — public sphere, care work, recognition vs. redistribution.
   - **Butler 1990** — *Gender Trouble*, performativity.
   - **Federici 2004** — *Caliban and the Witch*, gendered labor under primitive accumulation.
   - **Srinivasan 2021** — *The Right to Sex*, contemporary philosophical stakes.

### Live debates

- Fraser vs. Butler 1997-98 (*New Left Review* I/227 and I/228) on recognition vs. redistribution.
- Materialist feminists vs. Butler on performativity.
- How to read Beauvoir's immanence/transcendence (Moi 1999; Heinämaa 2003).
- Structural vs. reparative readings of Kant (Schott ed. 1997).

### Acknowledged limit

This pass is scope-limited to Western philosophy. Black feminist traditions (Hill Collins, Lorde, Combahee River Collective) and postcolonial feminism (Mohanty, Spivak) are necessary companions for a full account. The user has her own source list for this section; my pass complements it.

**Load-bearing sources:** Lloyd 1984; Pateman 1988; Okin 1979; Beauvoir 1949; Fraser 1995, 1998; Federici 2004; Bologh 1990.

## §5.1 — Masculine pursuits as serious / morally superior

### Headline findings

1. **Classical Stoicism is not the "alpha male" philosophy its modern popularizers suggest.** Seneca, Epictetus, and Marcus Aurelius taught a cognitive theory of the passions; *apatheia* meant freedom from destructive passions, not absence of feeling (Long 2002; Nussbaum 2001; Graver 2007). Musonius Rufus explicitly argued women should study philosophy on the same terms as men (Nussbaum 2002).

2. **But classical Stoicism was articulated within Greco-Roman patriarchy, and its vocabulary of "manly" virtue (*andreia*, *virtus*) carried gendered freight**, even where individual Stoics resisted the inference (Colish 1985; Reydams-Schils 2005).

3. **The Enlightenment intensified the reason/emotion coding, recoding it as a public/private split.** Lloyd 1984 traces "the Man of Reason" across Western philosophy; Pateman 1988 shows social contract theory constructs the public sphere as fraternal and rational, displacing care, emotion, and embodiment into the feminine private sphere.

4. **Weber's rationalization is structurally gendered** per Bologh 1990: public greatness requires subordinating brotherly love to instrumental rationality, coding the public/masculine sphere as the site of meaningful action.

5. **Contemporary cultural Stoicism is heterogeneous.**
   - **Philosophically serious revivalists** (Pigliucci 2017, *How to Be a Stoic*; Becker 1998, *A New Stoicism*; Robertson 2019) have explicitly distanced themselves from misogynist appropriations.
   - **Self-help / manosphere versions** (Holiday 2014, 2016; Peterson 2018; Red Pill forums) strip Stoic texts for discipline-and-dominance content.
   - **Zuckerberg 2018, *Not All Dead White Men*** (Harvard) is the definitive scholarly account of the classics-to-manosphere pipeline.

6. **Brilliance beliefs predict women's underrepresentation** (Leslie, Cimpian, Meyer, Freeland 2015) — linking cultural coding of "serious" thought directly to structural gender gaps. This is the same finding that recurs in §1, §2.2, and here.

7. **"Facts don't care about your feelings" is a documented gendered rhetorical weapon** in alt-right and manosphere media (Nagle 2017; Marwick and Lewis 2017; Phillips 2015; Kimmel 2013).

8. **Feminist philosophy of emotion rejects the reason/feeling binary on epistemic grounds** — emotions are themselves cognitive (Jaggar 1989; Nussbaum 2001, *Upheavals of Thought*; Ahmed 2004, *The Cultural Politics of Emotion*; Shields 2002).

**Load-bearing sources:** Zuckerberg 2018; Lloyd 1984; Pateman 1988; Nussbaum 2001; Jaggar 1989; Bologh 1990; Leslie et al. 2015.

## §5.2 — Feminine pursuits as frivolous / unserious

### Headline findings

1. **Taste hierarchies are not gender-neutral.** Bourdieu's *Distinction* (1984) established taste as a class marker. Feminist cultural studies (Felski 1995; Skeggs 1997; Huyssen 1986, *After the Great Divide*) added that the traits Bourdieu coded "legitimate" (distance, formal complexity, restraint) track masculine, while "popular" traits (immersion, emotion, sentiment, the body) track feminine. Huyssen's "mass culture as woman" thesis is load-bearing here.

2. **Feminine-coded genres have been systematically treated as aesthetically inferior.** The scholarly rehabilitation stretches from Modleski 1982/2008 (*Loving with a Vengeance*) and Radway 1984 (*Reading the Romance*) through Ang 1985 (*Watching Dallas*), Linda Williams 1991 on melodrama, Regis 2003 (*A Natural History of the Romance Novel*), and Harzewski 2011 on chick lit.

3. **Rockism vs. poptimism is the clearest contemporary case.** Sanneh's 2004 *NYT* "The Rap Against Rockism" named the ideology. Carl Wilson's *Let's Talk About Love* (2007; expanded 2014) on Celine Dion fandom is the book-length study that explicitly deploys Bourdieu to argue taste judgments function as social sorting.

4. **"Pinking" and infantilization are marketing phenomena with political reach.** Orenstein 2011 (*Cinderella Ate My Daughter*) documents Disney's early-2000s princess engineering. Projansky 2014, Cooper 2018 (*Eloquent Rage*), and Harris 2023 (*Wannabe*) argue "girl" operates as both infantilizing diminutive and racialized category — white girlhood is protected, Black girlhood is adultified.

5. **Women's sports coverage provides a quantitative backbone.** Cooky, Messner, and Musto 2015 (*Communication & Sport*): across 25 years, women's sports received 2-3% of televised sports news coverage, **no improvement**, directly contradicting the "popularity catches up" narrative.

6. **The counter-view (craft-based hierarchy) has serious advocates.** Some critics maintain complexity, formal innovation, and difficulty are defensible criteria independent of gender. Wilson 2014 is explicit that poptimism must not collapse into "everything is equal." The scholarly task is separating genuine craft arguments from gender-coded assumptions smuggled in as craft arguments.

7. **Reclamation scholarship has accelerated since ~2000**: romance (Regis 2003; Roach 2016), fashion (Entwistle 2015; Steele), rom-coms, reality TV, and fandom have moved from margin to center of cultural studies. The broader culture-industry hierarchies remain largely intact.

**Load-bearing sources:** Bourdieu 1984; Felski 1995; Radway 1984; Huyssen 1986; Wilson 2014; Cooky, Messner, Musto 2015.

## Cross-cutting threads

### 1. The devaluation cycle (drives §1, §2.1, §2.2, §3c)

When a field, skill, or pursuit is coded feminine, its prestige and compensation decline. Levanon, England, and Allison 2009 and Mandel 2018 provide the US longitudinal evidence; Jiang 2025 provides contemporary language-corpus evidence. This is the same mechanism that (in reverse) explains the masculinization of computing in §2.2, and that operates at the meta level when "creative" vs. "rigorous" is being assigned in §1.

### 2. The brilliance trap (drives §1, §2.2, §5.1)

Leslie, Cimpian, Meyer, and Freeland 2015 is the single most cited paper across the research. Fields where insiders believe innate "brilliance" is required have lower female (and lower African American) PhD representation, net of actual math content. This links the philosophical coding of "serious" thought (§5.1) directly to the observable composition of academic fields (§2.2). Ginther and Kahn 2015 have raised methodological critiques; the core correlation survives but effect sizes are contested.

### 3. The double bind (drives §3a, §3b)

Agentic behavior (competence, directness, leadership claim) is penalized in women because it violates the communal role expectation (Eagly and Karau 2002; Heilman and Okimoto 2007; Rudman et al. 2012; Bowles, Babcock, Lai 2007). Communal behavior fails to register as leadership. There is no consistent "winning" presentation.

### 4. The public/private split (drives §4, §5.1, §3c)

Philosophically codified in Rousseau, Kant, and Hegel; critiqued by Wollstonecraft forward; mapped onto the Stoic/Enlightenment reason/emotion coding in §5.1; still operative in Goldin's "greedy job" analysis in §3c, which is essentially an economist's description of how the 18th-century public/private split now allocates couple-level labor.

### 5. Intersectionality (cross-cuts all)

Across every section, race and class compound the pattern and sometimes invert specific findings:

- §2.1: Early feminization was disproportionately white and middle-class.
- §3a: Black women report highest rates of prove-it-again bias (Williams et al. 2014). Black trans men's post-transition experience differs from white trans men (Schilt 2010). SCOTUS interruption patterns compound by ideology and gender (Jacobi and Schweers 2017).
- §3b: Concrete ceiling; broken-rung gap sharpest for Black women and Latinas (McKinsey/LeanIn 2024).
- §3c: Latinas 58¢, Black women 67¢, AANHPI 93¢, Indigenous women least (IWPR 2024).
- §5.2: Black girlhood adultified where white girlhood is protected (Cooper 2018; Harris 2023).

### 6. The reason/emotion binary is philosophically defeated but culturally intact

Feminist philosophy of emotion (Jaggar 1989; Nussbaum 2001; Ahmed 2004) has largely dismantled the reason/feeling binary on epistemic grounds — emotions are cognitive, reason is not affect-free, the split is incoherent. Yet the binary is the entire scaffold of cultural stoicism, "facts don't care about your feelings" discourse, and the valorization of masculine-coded professions. This is the single sharpest gap between the state of the academic literature and the state of popular culture.
